MLB Fourth Of July Board: Sale, Rasmussen And Yamamoto Headline A Full 15-Game Holiday Slate

A full 15-game holiday board runs from a late-morning opener in Washington to a Dodger Stadium nightcap. Chris Sale faces the fading Mets, Drew Rasmussen duels Hunter Brown in Houston, Yoshinobu Yamamoto closes the night against San Diego, and Hunter Greene makes his season debut in Cincinnati. Verified starters, records and venues below. Analysis only, no picks.

1
Morning Opener

Pirates at Nationals

11:05 AM ET | Nationals Park | Braxton Ashcraft vs Carson Palmquist | PIT 44-45, WSH 46-43

The holiday begins in Washington with the traditional late-morning first pitch, and the visiting starter is the reason to be up for it. Braxton Ashcraft has been one of Pittsburgh's best stories at 8-3 with a 3.33 ERA and 115 strikeouts across 102.2 innings, a young arm who misses bats and pounds the zone in equal measure. The Pirates at 44-45 have hovered just under .500 all summer, and starts like his are the reason they have stayed within shouting distance of the Wild Card picture.

Washington counters at 46-43 with the left-hander Carson Palmquist, who has barely been stretched out at the big-league level with just 4.1 innings on the season, though he has been effective in that tiny sample with a 2.08 ERA. That gap in workload is the tension of the game: a proven, durable arm for Pittsburgh against a home starter whose outing will likely be shorter by design, putting the Nationals bullpen on notice from the middle innings on.

The late-morning start adds its own wrinkle. Day baseball in the July heat in Washington tends to reward the team that scores early and settles in, and with Palmquist unlikely to work deep, the Pirates' path is to grind out at-bats and get into the Nationals' relief corps by the fifth. Washington's counter is a fast start against Ashcraft before he finds his rhythm, because once he does, the strikeouts pile up.

2
Bronx Matinee

Twins at Yankees

1:35 PM ET | Yankee Stadium | Zebby Matthews vs Brendan Beck | MIN 42-47, NYY 49-38

The Yankees are the class of the early window at 49-38, but the pitching matchup injects real volatility into what looks like a comfortable home date. New York hands the ball to Brendan Beck, a rookie with all of three big-league innings and a 6.00 ERA in that sliver of work. Against a Minnesota club at 42-47 that has underachieved but still carries dangerous bats, a green starter in a hitter-friendly park is no sure thing.

Minnesota sends Zebby Matthews, who sits at 4-5 with a 4.15 ERA and a strong 1.14 WHIP across 56.1 innings. The WHIP tells the more flattering story: Matthews limits baserunners, and his outings tend to stay clean until a mistake finds the short porch. Yankee Stadium in July is exactly the venue where one elevated fastball changes a line score, which cuts both ways for two starters who live in the strike zone.

The lineup depth gap is the Yankees' cushion. New York can absorb a short start from Beck because the offense has carried thinner pitching days all season, while the Twins' margin for error is smaller on the road. If Matthews keeps the Yankees in the park through five, Minnesota has a real chance to steal the holiday matinee; if not, the Bronx crowd gets the early fireworks.

3
Afternoon

Tigers at Rangers

4:05 PM ET | Globe Life Field | Jack Flaherty vs Cal Quantrill | DET 38-50, TEX 45-43

Texas hosts the middle-afternoon window at 45-43, still clinging to the fringes of the American League race, and draws a Detroit club that has sunk to 38-50. The Tigers send Jack Flaherty, whose season has unraveled to a 1-8 record with a 4.97 ERA and a bloated 1.53 WHIP. The strikeouts are still there, 87 of them in 70.2 innings, but the traffic on the bases has been constant, and against a Rangers lineup playing meaningful July games, traffic tends to score.

The Rangers counter with Cal Quantrill, who has quietly been excellent in a swing role at 3-0 with a 3.31 ERA across 35.1 innings. Quantrill does not miss many bats, with 25 strikeouts in that span, but he manages contact and keeps his defense engaged, a formula that plays well under the roof at Globe Life Field where the ball does not carry the way it does in the open Texas heat.

This is a game about floor versus ceiling. Flaherty's strikeout stuff gives Detroit a theoretical path to a shutdown start, but the 1-8 record reflects how rarely the clean version has shown up this season. Quantrill's steadier profile against a bottom-five offense makes Texas the more predictable side of the pitching ledger, and for a team fighting to stay relevant in the AL West, predictable is exactly what a holiday home date calls for.

4
Pitcher's Park

Blue Jays at Mariners

4:10 PM ET | T-Mobile Park | Shane Bieber vs Logan Gilbert | TOR 42-46, SEA 45-44

Seattle's 4:10 PM ET start is one of the day's best run-prevention environments. T-Mobile Park has suppressed offense for years, and the Mariners hand the ball to Logan Gilbert, a front-line arm at 6-5 with a 3.42 ERA, 107 strikeouts and a sparkling 1.01 WHIP across exactly 100 innings. Gilbert is tailor-made for the venue: he attacks the top of the zone, lets the marine air knock down fly balls, and rarely beats himself with walks.

Toronto counters with Shane Bieber, who is still working his way back to form with a 6.00 ERA and a 2.00 WHIP in just nine innings this season. The name carries weight, but the current version is a rebuild in progress, and the Blue Jays at 42-46 have little margin to absorb a short, messy start on the road. For a lineup that has struggled to slug consistently, a park that punishes fly-ball hitters compounds the assignment.

The Mariners at 45-44 remain squarely in the AL West mix, and this is the kind of game their season has been built on: a low-scoring grind where Gilbert hands a slim lead to the bullpen. Toronto's route is to make Bieber's line irrelevant by scratching out early runs and turning it into a battle of relief corps. Before a pitch is thrown, everything about the matchup projects toward the lower end of the run spectrum.

5
Greene's Debut

Orioles at Reds

7:10 PM ET | Great American Ball Park | Brandon Young vs Hunter Greene | BAL 41-48, CIN 40-47

The story in Cincinnati is a homecoming. Hunter Greene, the flame-throwing face of the Reds' rotation, makes his 2026 season debut on the Fourth of July after a long injury absence, and Great American Ball Park will treat it like the event it is. For a 40-47 club that has spent the first half treading water, getting its hardest thrower back is the kind of lift that can reshape a second half.

Baltimore is no pushover in this spot despite its 41-48 record, because Brandon Young has quietly been one of the Orioles' most reliable arms at 6-2 with a 3.11 ERA across 72.1 innings. The catch is the venue: Great American Ball Park is one of the most homer-friendly yards in the league, and Young's fly balls will get stress-tested in ways they would not at Camden Yards.

Debut starts off long layoffs carry natural workload caps, so Greene's night is likely measured in electricity rather than length, with the Reds bullpen shouldering the back half. That puts a premium on Baltimore's patience: run the pitch count, get Greene out early, and attack the middle relief. Cincinnati's script is the opposite, a loud early cushion behind their returning ace and a holiday crowd that gets to exhale for the first time all season.

6
Pitching Duel

Rays at Astros

7:10 PM ET | Daikin Park | Drew Rasmussen vs Hunter Brown | TB 52-33, HOU 43-47

Houston hosts one of the two marquee pitching duels on the board. Tampa Bay, at 52-33 and leading the AL East, sends Drew Rasmussen, whose 2.45 ERA and 0.87 WHIP across 92 innings make him one of the stingiest starters in baseball. Rasmussen simply does not allow traffic: 94 strikeouts against that microscopic WHIP means innings end quickly and rallies die at one baserunner.

The Astros' answer is every bit his equal on current form. Hunter Brown has a 1.78 ERA with 32 strikeouts in 25.1 innings since returning from a shoulder issue, and the raw stuff has looked all the way back. The caveat is workload: Houston has managed him carefully since the return, so the Rays' deep, disciplined lineup may only need to survive five or six innings of Brown before the game shifts to the bullpens.

Two arms of this caliber at Daikin Park set up a low-event, tension-filled night between a first-place club and a 43-47 Astros team trying to steady a disappointing first half. Tampa Bay wants the methodical version of the game, controlling at-bats and handing a lead to its bullpen. Houston needs one big swing to tilt a game where runs will be scarce, and it has the middle-of-the-order bats capable of providing it.

7
AL Central

White Sox at Guardians

7:10 PM ET | Progressive Field | Sean Burke vs Parker Messick | CWS 45-42, CLE 47-42

Cleveland's matchup with Chicago is the quiet gem of the prime-time window. Guardians left-hander Parker Messick has been excellent at 7-5 with a 2.85 ERA and 106 strikeouts across 101 innings, and he draws a White Sox club that counters with a bat-misser of its own in Sean Burke, at 5-4 with a 3.69 ERA and 95 strikeouts in 92.2 innings. Two legitimate strikeout arms at Progressive Field is a recipe for a tight, low-scoring evening.

The standings make it matter. Cleveland sits at 47-42 and Chicago at 45-42, separated by a game and a half in an AL Central that has tightened into a genuine fight. The White Sox have been one of the season's real turnaround stories, and every head-to-head meeting between these two now carries direct divisional weight, raising the stakes on every run in a park that historically rewards pitching and defense.

With both starters capable of dominating, the margins shrink to execution in the middle innings: the timely two-out hit, the double play turned or missed, the shutdown frame after a rally. Whichever bullpen inherits the game first likely decides it, and the loser cedes real ground in a division where neither club has any cushion to give.

8
Rivalry

Cardinals at Cubs

8:08 PM ET | Wrigley Field | Kyle Leahy vs Shota Imanaga | STL 46-39, CHC 49-39

At Wrigley Field, the Cubs at 49-39 and the Cardinals at 46-39 renew one of the sport's oldest rivalries with real stakes attached: three games separate them in a National League Central race that also runs through Milwaukee. A holiday night game between these two, with both squarely in the playoff picture, is as good as regular-season atmosphere gets on the North Side.

Chicago sends Shota Imanaga, whose 5-6 record undersells him. The 4.30 ERA comes with a 1.08 WHIP across 98.1 innings, the profile of a command-first left-hander whose mistakes have too often left the yard but who rarely gives away free baserunners. St. Louis counters with Kyle Leahy at 6-4 with a 4.09 ERA, though his 1.48 WHIP tells the opposite story: constant traffic that his defense and sequencing have so far kept from fully cashing in.

That contrast frames the night. If the wind is blowing in, Imanaga's fly-ball contact dies at the track and his WHIP advantage compounds; if it is blowing out, Wrigley punishes his one flaw. Leahy's traffic-heavy profile means the Cubs should have chances in nearly every inning, and in a rivalry game with division position on the line, the team that cashes its early opportunities usually leaves with the win.

9
Marquee

Mets at Braves

8:08 PM ET | Truist Park | Sean Manaea vs Chris Sale | NYM 36-52, ATL 51-35

The best pitching mismatch of the night is in Atlanta, and it is the board's marquee. Chris Sale takes the ball for the 51-35 Braves carrying an 8-6 record, a 2.10 ERA and 109 strikeouts across 90 innings, still missing bats at an elite rate deep into his career. Across the diamond stands a Mets team that has cratered to 36-52, the most stunning collapse in the National League, and a holiday crowd at Truist Park that will be loud from the first pitch.

New York counters with Sean Manaea at 1-3 with a 4.71 ERA in 63 innings, a veteran left-hander who has battled his command all season. It is a stark collision of form and quality: an ace at the top of his game against a lineup pressing through one of the worst stretches in baseball. Sale is exactly the kind of arm that quiets a struggling offense, and struggling offenses rarely solve him in one night.

The danger for Atlanta is the one that always shadows lopsided matchups: a rivalry opponent with nothing to lose and a bullpen game essentially guaranteed once Manaea's evening ends. The Mets' only realistic path is an early burst against Sale before he settles, because once he is ahead in counts, the strikeouts arrive in bunches and the game shortens fast. Everything else about the night points toward the division leader flexing at home.

10
Coors Field

Giants at Rockies

8:10 PM ET | Coors Field | Robbie Ray vs Sean Sullivan | SF 36-51, COL 36-53

Coors Field hosts a battle of the National League's basement as the 36-51 Giants visit the 36-53 Rockies, two teams whose seasons have long since turned toward evaluation. What keeps the game interesting is the gap on the mound: San Francisco sends Robbie Ray at 7-6 with a 3.39 ERA and 82 strikeouts across 95.2 innings, while Colorado counters with the rookie Sean Sullivan, who has been hit hard to the tune of an 8.64 ERA and a 1.86 WHIP in 16.2 innings.

Altitude is the eternal equalizer. Coors inflates every mistake, flattens breaking balls, and turns routine fly balls into extra bases, which is why even a starter of Ray's quality treats a trip to Denver as a survival exercise rather than a showcase. Sullivan's traffic-heavy line at sea level becomes genuinely dangerous a mile up, and the Rockies' only consistent path to wins this season has been dragging opponents into track meets at home.

The sensible read is that the starting-pitching gap is too wide even for altitude to close: Ray's swing-and-miss stuff travels better than most because strikeouts are altitude-proof. But this venue has humbled better teams than these Giants, and with both clubs playing out the string, the game may hinge less on the aces' matchup than on which threadbare bullpen survives the seventh and eighth innings.

11
Interleague

Phillies at Royals

8:10 PM ET | Kauffman Stadium | Jesus Luzardo vs Michael Wacha | PHI 49-39, KC 35-53

Philadelphia at 49-39 remains one of the National League's steadiest teams, and it sends Jesus Luzardo to Kansas City with a 6-4 record, a 3.88 ERA and 116 strikeouts in 97.1 innings. The strikeout total is the headline: Luzardo's left-handed power stuff has kept opposing lineups off balance all season, and a Royals offense that has scuffled through a 35-53 first half is a favorable canvas for it.

Kansas City's counter is the reason this one is not a formality. Michael Wacha has been the Royals' most dependable arm at 5-5 with a 3.31 ERA and a 1.14 WHIP across a workhorse 108.2 innings, the veteran formula of soft contact, changeups off the plate, and six competent innings nearly every time out. Wacha keeps bad teams in games, which is precisely what Kauffman Stadium needs on a holiday night.

The gap in overall roster quality is wide, and it shows most in the late innings: Philadelphia's lineup depth grinds down middle relief, while Kansas City has to play close to perfect to hold a one-run game. Wacha versus Luzardo is a legitimately even pitching matchup; everything that surrounds it leans toward the visitors, which is why the Royals' best chance is a low-scoring game that never reaches the bullpen math.

12
Late

Red Sox at Angels

9:38 PM ET | Angel Stadium | Sonny Gray vs Sam Aldegheri | BOS 38-48, LAA 36-53

Boston's Sonny Gray is the class of the late window. At 9-1 with a 2.69 ERA across 83.2 innings, the veteran has been nearly untouchable in the win-loss column, mixing four pitches and pitching backward in a way that has made him a stabilizer for a 38-48 Red Sox club whose season has otherwise disappointed. Any night Gray starts, Boston's blueprint is simple: hand him a lead and shorten the game.

The Angels at 36-53 counter with the young left-hander Sam Aldegheri at 3-3 with a 4.85 ERA in 29.2 innings, a starter still finding his footing at the level. Angel Stadium plays close to neutral, so there is no park factor to hide behind; the matchup is simply a rookie-adjacent arm against one of the American League's most polished veterans, on a night when both teams are playing for evaluation more than October.

The one caution for Boston is familiar to anyone who has watched this team: run support has been sporadic, and Gray's gaudy record has been built on thin margins. If Aldegheri keeps the Red Sox lineup quiet early, the game stays within one swing far longer than the pitching gap suggests it should. The cleaner read is Gray doing what he has done all season, and the Angels needing their bullpen to win four-plus innings against a veteran-led club.

13
Launching Pad

Marlins at Athletics

9:40 PM ET | Sutter Health Park | Sandy Alcantara vs Aaron Civale | MIA 47-42, OAK 41-47

Sacramento's Sutter Health Park has played as one of baseball's most hitter-friendly venues, and that frames everything about Miami's visit. The Marlins at 47-42 are a genuine Wild Card presence, and they send their ace: Sandy Alcantara at 9-4 with a 4.20 ERA and 84 strikeouts across a league-leading workload of 115.2 innings. Alcantara has the pedigree and durability to work deep, which matters most in a park where every bullpen inning is a hazard.

The Athletics at 41-47 counter with Aaron Civale, who has labored to a 4.92 ERA with a 1.57 WHIP in 67.2 innings. That much traffic in this ballpark is a combustible mix, and it is why the game projects toward runs despite the presence of a former Cy Young contender on the other side. Even a strong Alcantara start can turn into a track meet here if the ball starts carrying.

Miami's plan is to lean on the starting-pitching edge and its more balanced roster to control the game before the park tilts it. Oakland's route is the same one it has used all season at home: survive the opposing starter's first two turns through the order, jump on middle-innings mistakes, and drag the game into the late-inning chaos that Sutter Health Park manufactures better than almost any yard in the majors.

14
NL Leaders

Brewers at Diamondbacks

9:40 PM ET | Chase Field | Brandon Woodruff vs Merrill Kelly | MIL 54-32, ARI 43-44

The National League's best record visits the desert. Milwaukee at 54-32 leads the league and hands the ball to Brandon Woodruff, who has been vintage since returning to the rotation: a 2.59 ERA and a 0.84 WHIP across 41.2 innings, with 41 strikeouts. The workload is still being managed, but on a rate basis Woodruff has been one of the least hittable starters in the sport since coming back.

Arizona at 43-44 counters with Merrill Kelly, whose season has gone sideways at 5-8 with a 5.84 ERA and a 1.53 WHIP in 81.2 innings. The veteran's command-and-guile profile has thin margins by design, and this year the mistakes have been punished relentlessly. Against a Brewers lineup that grinds at-bats as well as any club in the league, the traffic problem compounds quickly.

Chase Field can inflate offense with the roof open, but the matchup tilts clearly toward Milwaukee on the mound regardless of conditions. Arizona's realistic path is to get Woodruff's pitch count up early, force the Brewers to cover four bullpen innings, and win the game late. If Woodruff cruises through five the way his rate stats suggest he can, the best team in the National League usually finishes the job.

15
Nightcap

Padres at Dodgers

10:10 PM ET | Dodger Stadium | Wandy Peralta (opener) vs Yoshinobu Yamamoto | SD 43-44, LAD 58-31

The nightcap closes the board at Dodger Stadium, where Los Angeles owns the best record in baseball at 58-31 and hands the ball to Yoshinobu Yamamoto. His line reads 8-5 with a 2.67 ERA, 90 strikeouts and a 0.89 WHIP across 97.2 innings, front-line run prevention that has anchored the sport's deepest roster all season. On a holiday night at Chavez Ravine, it is a fitting headliner for a coast-to-coast slate.

San Diego at 43-44 goes the other direction entirely, opening with the left-handed reliever Wandy Peralta, who carries a 2.38 ERA across 45.1 innings of relief work into a bullpen-game script. The opener strategy is an honest admission of where the Padres' rotation stands right now, and it means the night becomes a parade of matchups: Peralta to turn over the top of the Dodgers' order once, then a chain of relievers trying to keep an elite lineup from ever settling in against one arm.

The rivalry guarantees an edge no matter the standings, but the structural gap is the story. Los Angeles gets a true ace on regular rest; San Diego needs eight-plus innings of piecework to hold down the best team in baseball. If the Padres' bullpen chain holds early and the game stays tight into the seventh, the pressure flips onto a Dodgers club expected to win. Everything before that point favors the home side, and it is the cleanest talent edge on the entire fifteen-game holiday board.
