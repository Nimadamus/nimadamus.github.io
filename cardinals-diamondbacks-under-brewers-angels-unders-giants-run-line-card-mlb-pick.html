Cardinals-Diamondbacks Under 9, Brewers and Angels Team Total Unders and the Giants Run Line: A Four-Play MLB Card

July 18, 2026|8 min read|BetLegend

Max Meyer walked off the mound this season with a 9-1 record and a 2.58 ERA, and almost nobody outside South Florida noticed. That is the strange gift of a Saturday afternoon in the middle of July: the best pitching stories are the quiet ones, and the market keeps pricing offenses off reputations while good arms make those offenses disappear. Today's four-play card is built on that idea from four different angles. Two team total unders sit behind arms who have been strangling lineups all year. A game total in the desert leans on a pair of starters who both keep the ball in the yard. And the one play that is not an under buys a run and a half of insurance on a road team that does not need to win, only to lose close. There is no hero-hitter requirement anywhere on this ticket. Total exposure is 7.5 units.

Cardinals-Diamondbacks Under 9: A Contact Duel At Chase Field

Chase Field with the roof closed is not the launching pad its reputation suggests, and this particular matchup gives the Under a real edge over the total of nine. Start with the Cardinals arm. Dustin May has been streaky on the year at 5-6 with a 4.55 ERA and 89 strikeouts across 93 innings, but the swing-and-miss stuff is still there, and when he keeps the sinker down he piles up quick outs. The bet here leans less on a shutdown starter than on the lineup he is facing: a Diamondbacks offense scoring just 4.3 runs a game, with the roof closed at Chase Field turning it into a far more neutral run environment than the park reputation implies.

Arizona counters with Brandon Pfaadt at 3-1 and a 4.70 ERA, a starter whose ERA looks worse than the run-prevention job the Diamondbacks offense hands the visitors. And that is the second leg of this Under: the Diamondbacks are not the relentless run machine the desert address implies. Arizona is averaging just 4.3 runs a game with 0.9 home runs per contest, a middle-of-the-pack offense that does its damage in spurts rather than avalanches. The Cardinals sit at 51-45 and the Diamondbacks at 49-48, two evenly matched clubs with pitching that has been better than either bat.

The math is friendly. If May keeps Arizona in the four-run neighborhood and Pfaadt keeps a modest Cardinals lineup in check for five or six, this game is tracking toward the seven-to-eight range that a two-run cushion under nine comfortably covers. One unit on the Under 9 at -120.

Brewers Team Total Under 4.5: Max Meyer Is The Whole Bet

This play narrows the bet to a single dugout, and it does so for one overwhelming reason. The Milwaukee Brewers own the best record in baseball at 60-37, but tonight they run into the arm listed at the top of this card. Max Meyer has been the story of the Marlins rotation, carrying a 9-1 record, a 2.58 ERA and a 3.53 FIP into American Family Field. A team total under is the cleanest way to buy an arm like that. You are not asking Miami to win. You are asking the Brewers, specifically, to fall short of four and a half runs against a pitcher who has spent the whole season keeping the best of them off the board.

The supporting evidence is in Milwaukee's recent bats, not just Meyer's line. The Brewers have gone 5-5 over their last ten games while hitting .256 as a team, a stretch in which they were outscored by a run despite the sparkling overall record. This is a club that wins with pitching and defense far more than it wins with a booming lineup, and that profile is a team-total-under's best friend. Milwaukee's offense is built to manufacture, not to explode, and manufacturing runs is precisely what Meyer's swing-and-miss profile shuts down.

Even the venue cooperates. American Family Field with the roof in play is a neutral-to-pitcher environment on a July afternoon, and the Marlins bullpen, thinned as it is by injuries, still only has to protect the middle innings after Meyer does the heavy lifting. If Meyer gives Miami five or six innings of the baseball he has thrown all year, the Brewers need a late rally against relievers just to sniff the number. A unit and a half on the Brewers team total Under 4.5 at -140.

Angels Team Total Under 3.5: Skubal Against A Silent Lineup

This is a two-unit play and it is the simplest to explain on the whole card. Tarik Skubal is pitching, the Los Angeles Angels are not scoring, and three and a half is a number a Skubal start suppresses as often as any team total in the league. Skubal arrives in Anaheim at 5-5 with a 3.09 ERA, a 0.95 WHIP and 89 strikeouts, the kind of top-of-the-rotation left-hander who turns a struggling lineup into an easy night's work.

And the Angels have been exactly that struggling lineup. Los Angeles sits at 38-60 and has lost eight of its last ten games, an offense in a genuine rut running into the best arm it will see all week. The Tigers, at 45-52, walk in on a three-game road winning streak, and their willingness to hand the ball to Skubal in a spot like this tells you everything about the confidence Detroit has in him closing a lineup down.

The counter-argument, and it is a fair one, is that the Angels are at home and that even a cold offense catches fire eventually. But betting a team total under against Skubal is not a bet on the Angels being permanently broken. It is a bet on one specific below-average lineup, in a rut, failing to reach four runs against a pitcher whose whole game is missing bats and keeping traffic off the bases. Two units on the Angels team total Under 3.5 at -145.

Giants Run Line +1.5: Buying The Cushion In Seattle

The biggest play on the card at three units is the one that is not an under, and it is a deliberate change of shape. Instead of betting a lineup to go quiet, we are buying a road underdog a run and a half of insurance in a low-scoring pitcher's park. The Giants are at 42-55 and the Mariners at 48-50, and Seattle is a modest home favorite at -152 on the moneyline. That price tells you the market sees a close game, and a close game is exactly the environment where a run line +1.5 pays.

The pitching matchup is why this is a coin-flip dressed up as a favorite. San Francisco sends Logan Webb, a genuine front-line starter carrying a 3.95 ERA, against Seattle's Bryan Woo at a 4.23 ERA. Two quality arms in T-Mobile Park, one of the best pitcher's environments in the American League, is a recipe for a tight, low-scoring game. When Webb is on the mound, the Giants are rarely blown out, and the run line asks nothing more than that they stay within a run and a half.

The value is in the shape of the likely outcome. A one-run Mariners win, a Giants win of any margin, or extra innings all cash this ticket. The only way it loses is a Seattle win by two or more, and with Webb starting in a park that flattens offenses, that is a narrower band of outcomes than -152 on the Seattle moneyline implies. Three units on the Giants run line +1.5 at -180.

What Can Beat It

Four plays, four honest failure paths, and none of them is exotic. The Chase Field under dies if either lineup runs into a couple of pitches early and the game becomes a slugfest before the starters settle in. May can be volatile, and a 4.70-ERA Pfaadt can give up a crooked inning of his own, and nine is not an impossible number to clear in the desert if both bullpens leak.

The Brewers team total under dies if the best record in baseball simply plays like the best record in baseball for one afternoon. A 60-37 club has plenty of nights where it pushes five across even against a good arm, and Max Meyer, for all his brilliance, is a 9-1 pitcher who occasionally gives back three or four. The Angels under dies the same way any Skubal-based team total does: an early hook, a short outing, or one three-run swing from a home lineup that only needs one good inning to reach four. And the Giants run line, the biggest swing on the card, dies only in the specific event of a Mariners win by two or more, which is a live outcome any time Webb has an off night or the Giants offense goes silent against Woo. Every one of these risks is baked into the price. None of them changes the read.

The Bottom Line

A Saturday board rewards patience, and this four-play card is patient by design. The Cardinals vs Diamondbacks Under 9 at -120 is a one-unit lean on Dustin May and a Diamondbacks offense scoring just 4.3 a game inside a roof-closed Chase Field. The Brewers team total Under 4.5 at -140 is a unit and a half on Max Meyer and his 2.58 ERA against a Milwaukee lineup hitting .256 over its last ten. The Angels team total Under 3.5 at -145 is two units on Tarik Skubal and a 0.95 WHIP against a lineup that has dropped eight of ten. And the Giants run line +1.5 at -180 is the biggest play at three units, a clean way to buy a run and a half of cushion behind Logan Webb in a pitcher's park where close games are the rule. Seven and a half units, three of them bought on run prevention and one bought on the math of a tight game, and not a single one of them needs a hitter to be a hero.
