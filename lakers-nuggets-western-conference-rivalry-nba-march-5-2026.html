9-Game Thursday Slate

NBA March 5, 2026

Thursday's loaded nine-game slate is headlined by an absolute monster in San Antonio, where the 45-15 Pistons visit the 44-17 Spurs in a potential Finals preview between the two best records in basketball. Prime Video doubles up with Luka Doncic and the Lakers visiting Nikola Jokic's Nuggets in a Western Conference heavyweight bout, plus the Rockets hosting a depleted Warriors squad missing Steph Curry and Jimmy Butler. Brandon Ingram and the Raptors travel to Minnesota, Orlando hosts the rebuilding Mavericks, Miami lays a massive 13.5-point spread against Brooklyn, and the tank race continues with Utah at Washington and New Orleans at Sacramento.

Pistons @ Spurs

Thursday, 8:00 PM ET | Frost Bank Center, San Antonio, TX
Spread
SAS -2.5
Moneyline
SAS -155 / DET +130
Total
O/U 228.5

Circle this one in permanent marker. This is the game of the night, and it might be the game of the week across the entire NBA. Detroit at 45-15 owns the best record in the Eastern Conference and the best record in basketball, while San Antonio at 44-17 is right on their heels with the second-best mark in the league. These are the two teams that have separated themselves from the pack this season, and Thursday night in San Antonio has the feel of a championship dress rehearsal. The fact that the Spurs are only 2.5-point home favorites in this matchup tells you just how much respect the market has for what Detroit has built, because that's essentially a coin-flip game with home-court advantage baked in.

The Pistons have been the most surprising story in the NBA this season, and they've done it with a suffocating defense and a collective offensive identity that doesn't rely on a single superstar carrying the load. Cade Cunningham has taken a massive leap into legitimate All-Star territory, and the supporting cast around him has bought into a system that grinds opponents down possession by possession. Detroit's 45-15 record isn't a fluke or a product of a weak schedule. They've beaten good teams all season, and they play with the kind of blue-collar toughness that makes them incredibly difficult to put away. The Pistons don't beat themselves, they don't take possessions off, and they play with a chip on their shoulder every single night because nobody expected them to be here.

San Antonio's transformation has been equally remarkable, and the blockbuster midseason acquisition of Fox was the move that changed everything for this franchise. Fox gives the Spurs the kind of explosive, downhill speed at the point guard position that unlocks everything else in their offense, and paired with Victor Wembanyama's generational two-way ability, San Antonio has a 1-2 punch that can match up with anyone in basketball. Wembanyama continues to rewrite the record books for what a 7-foot-3 player can do on both ends of the floor, and his combination of rim protection, perimeter defense, playmaking, and three-point shooting makes him the most unique player in the NBA. The Spurs at home have been nearly unbeatable, and the Frost Bank Center crowd is going to be absolutely electric for this one.

The 228.5 total is right in line with what you'd expect from two teams that play physical, grinding basketball without sacrificing offensive efficiency. Both Detroit and San Antonio are built on defense-first principles, but both also have enough offensive firepower to put up points when the game opens up. The fascinating chess match here is how Detroit's scheme deals with the Wembanyama-Fox pick-and-roll, and whether San Antonio's defense can contain Cunningham without giving up open looks to Detroit's shooters. This is the kind of game where coaching becomes the difference-maker, and both Gregg Popovich and the Pistons' coaching staff have shown they can make the adjustments necessary to win tight games against elite competition. If this is a preview of the Finals, basketball fans are in for a treat.

Here's what makes this matchup so compelling from a basketball purist's perspective: both teams play the right way. There's no hero ball, no selfish possessions, no stretches where one player holds the ball for 15 seconds and launches a contested three. Detroit moves the ball, finds the open man, and executes. San Antonio runs their offense through Wembanyama and Fox, but the supporting cast is always involved, always moving, always ready to attack when the defense commits. These are the two best-coached, most fundamentally sound teams in the NBA, and when they meet, the game is going to be decided by execution, toughness, and who wants it more in the fourth quarter. The SAS -2.5 spread is the tightest number on the board for any game involving these two teams, and it should be. This one is going to be a war.

Lakers @ Nuggets

Thursday, 10:00 PM ET | Ball Arena, Denver, CO
Spread
DEN -4.5
Moneyline
DEN -198 / LAL +164
Total
O/U 241.5

This is must-see television on Prime Video, and the main event is the matchup everyone has been waiting for all season: Luka Doncic versus Nikola Jokic, two of the most skilled offensive players the NBA has ever produced, going head-to-head in a game with massive Western Conference playoff seeding implications. Los Angeles at 37-24 has been on a tear since acquiring Doncic in the February 2025 blockbuster, and Luka has been absolutely sensational, averaging 32.4 points per game and playing with the kind of confidence and creativity that makes him nearly impossible to defend when he's locked in. The Lakers are 3-0 against Denver this season when Doncic plays, which is a remarkable stat that speaks to how Luka's presence fundamentally changes this matchup.

Denver at 38-24 is clinging to a slim lead over the Lakers in the standings, and the Nuggets desperately need this one to maintain their positioning. Jokic continues to be the most complete offensive player in basketball, averaging a triple-double with 28.7 points, 12.6 rebounds, and 10.3 assists per game, and his ability to orchestrate the Denver offense from the post and the elbow is unlike anything we've seen from a center in NBA history. The injury to Aaron Gordon, who is out for this game, removes one of Denver's most important defensive pieces and a key secondary creator in their half-court offense. Without Gordon, the Nuggets lose some of their defensive versatility and their ability to switch across multiple positions, which is going to be critical against a Lakers offense that attacks mismatches relentlessly.

The DEN -4.5 spread with Gordon out feels like the market is banking heavily on Denver's altitude advantage and Jokic's dominance at Ball Arena. Playing at 5,280 feet is a real factor, especially in the fourth quarter when legs start to fatigue, and the Nuggets have been one of the best home teams in basketball for the last several seasons because opponents simply can't keep up with their pace and execution when the thin air starts to take its toll. But the Lakers have solved that puzzle three times already this season with Luka on the floor, and Doncic is the kind of player who actually seems to elevate his game in big-moment, nationally televised matchups. He lives for these stages.

The 241.5 total is the highest on Thursday's entire board, and it reflects the offensive firepower both teams bring when their stars are cooking. Doncic and Jokic are going to combine for somewhere around 60 points if both are healthy and engaged, and the supporting casts on both sides are capable of contributing enough to push this well past 240. Jamal Murray's ability to create his own shot and get hot from three gives Denver a secondary weapon that can swing this game in a hurry, and the Lakers' supporting cast around Doncic has been much improved since the trade. This game has all the ingredients for a classic: two superstars, playoff-level intensity, national television, and a crowd at Ball Arena that's going to be rocking from tip to buzzer. Don't be surprised if this one comes down to the final minute.

Warriors @ Rockets

Thursday, 7:30 PM ET | Toyota Center, Houston, TX
Spread
HOU -8.5
Moneyline
HOU -380 / GSW +300
Total
O/U 215.5

Houston at 38-22 has been one of the biggest surprises in the Western Conference, and the KD acquisition last July transformed this team from an exciting young core into a legitimate championship contender overnight. Durant brings scoring gravity that warps defensive schemes, and his ability to shoot over anyone from anywhere on the floor gives Houston an offensive ceiling that very few teams can match. The Rockets' young core of Jalen Green, Alperen Sengun, and their developing pieces has matured significantly alongside Durant, and the combination of youth and veteran star power has Houston sitting comfortably in the top four of the Western Conference with real postseason aspirations.

Golden State at 31-30 is in a difficult spot, made worse by devastating injuries. Jimmy Butler, acquired from Miami in a trade this season, is done for the year with a knee injury, and Stephen Curry has missed the last 10+ games with a persistent right knee issue (patella-femoral pain/bone bruising) and isn't expected back until at least mid-March. Without their two biggest stars, the Warriors are essentially a developmental squad right now, fighting for a play-in spot with a roster that lacks a proven go-to scorer. At .500 on the season, Golden State is running on fumes, and road games against teams like Houston are the kind of mismatches that can spiral out of control quickly when you're missing your top two players.

The HOU -8.5 spread is substantial but completely justified given the injury situation. Without Curry and Butler, the Warriors are missing their two best players, and the HOU -380 moneyline tells you the market sees this as a borderline blowout. Houston at home has been dominant all season, and the Toyota Center crowd has embraced this new era of Rockets basketball with the kind of energy that makes it a difficult environment for visiting teams. Durant's ability to score 30 on any given night means the Rockets always have a floor of offensive production that's difficult for opponents to overcome, and when the supporting cast of Jalen Green, Alperen Sengun, and the rest contributes, Houston's ceiling is as high as anyone's in the West.

The 215.5 total is the lowest on Thursday's board, and it paints a picture of a game where Houston controls the pace and doesn't let Golden State get into transition. The Rockets' defense has been elite at protecting the paint, and that's going to be a problem for a Warriors offense that lacks a reliable go-to scorer with Curry and Butler sidelined. If Houston can force Golden State into half-court possessions and contest their three-point looks, the Warriors could have one of those nights where the shots simply don't fall and the game gets away from them in the third quarter. Draymond Green (listed probable with a back issue) and the Warriors' remaining rotation players will compete, but the talent gap between these two rosters right now, combined with Houston's home-court advantage, makes this a steep uphill climb for Golden State.

Dallas at 21-40 is a completely different franchise than the one that reached the NBA Finals a few years ago, and the trade that sent Luka Doncic to the Lakers in February 2025 was the moment the rebuild officially began. Without their generational offensive weapon, the Mavericks have been one of the worst teams in basketball, and their 21-40 record reflects a roster that's being held together by young players gaining experience and veterans playing out their contracts. The tank is fully engaged in Dallas, and while that's the right long-term strategy for a team that needs to accumulate draft capital, it makes for some rough Thursday nights when you're traveling to play a team with legitimate playoff aspirations.

Orlando at 32-28 is having the kind of steady, unspectacular season that doesn't generate many headlines but quietly puts them in excellent playoff position. The Magic have been one of the most improved defensive teams in the league, and their length and athleticism at every position makes them a nightmare for opposing offenses to solve. Paolo Banchero continues to develop into one of the most versatile offensive players in the Eastern Conference, and his ability to score from all three levels while facilitating for his teammates gives Orlando an offensive anchor that most young teams don't have. Franz Wagner has been equally impressive, and the Banchero-Wagner combination is one of the best young duos in basketball.

The ORL -8.5 spread is hefty, but it accurately reflects the talent gap between these two rosters in their current states. Orlando at home has been one of the better teams in the league, and the Kia Center crowd has rallied behind this young core in a way that creates a legitimate home-court advantage. Dallas doesn't have the offensive firepower to keep up with the Magic for 48 minutes, and their defense without elite talent at every position is going to struggle to contain Banchero and Wagner in the half court. This has the feel of a game where Orlando builds a comfortable lead by halftime and the only question is whether they cover the spread.

The 228.5 total suggests a moderately paced game, and the interesting question is whether Dallas can generate enough offense to push this toward the over. The Mavericks' young players tend to play with energy and take a lot of shots, which can keep the pace elevated even in blowouts. Orlando's offense has been efficient all season without needing to play at a breakneck tempo, and they're perfectly happy to control the pace, execute in the half court, and let their defense do the heavy lifting. If the Magic jump out early, this could turn into a developmental showcase for both teams' young players in the fourth quarter, with the starters sitting on the bench and the outcome long decided.

Jazz @ Wizards

Thursday, 7:00 PM ET | Capital One Arena, Washington, DC
Spread
WAS -2.5
Moneyline
WAS -135 / UTA +114
Total
O/U 242.5

Welcome to the tank bowl. Utah at 18-44 and Washington at 16-45 are two of the three worst teams in the NBA, and Thursday night's meeting between them is less about who wins the game and more about who wins the draft lottery. Both franchises are in full rebuild mode, both are developing young players through the fires of a losing season, and both are eyeing a summer draft class that could transform their futures. The WAS -2.5 spread is essentially home-court advantage and nothing else, because there is no meaningful talent gap between these two rosters in their current states. This is as close to a pick-em game as you'll find on Thursday's board.

Utah's season has been about development from day one, and the Jazz have been giving heavy minutes to their young players to see who can be part of the next competitive core. Without Lauri Markkanen (ankle) and with Walker Kessler done for the season (shoulder), Utah is running out a roster that would struggle in the G League some nights. But there's value in the process, because the Jazz are identifying which of their young pieces can handle extended NBA minutes and which ones are roster filler. Keyonte George has shown flashes of being a legitimate starting guard in this league, and his development is the single most important storyline for Utah this season.

Washington isn't much better, and their 16-45 record is tied for the worst in basketball. The Wizards' rebuild started before Utah's, but the results haven't been dramatically different. Bilal Coulibaly, Jordan Poole, and the rest of Washington's young core are getting the reps they need to develop, but winning games consistently is still a long way off. The Wizards have actually shown some fight at home recently, which is why they're favored here, but "some fight" for a 16-45 team means they lose by single digits instead of 20. The Capital One Arena hasn't had much to cheer about this season, and a game against the Jazz is probably going to feature a lot of empty seats.

The 242.5 total is the highest on Thursday's board, and that makes complete sense for a game featuring two of the worst defensive teams in the NBA. Neither Utah nor Washington has the personnel, the scheme, or the motivation to play real defense at this point in the season. This has the feel of a run-and-gun affair where both teams push the pace, take whatever shots are available, and the scoring flows freely in both directions. The over is the most interesting angle in this game, because when two bad defensive teams play each other with nothing to lose, the scoring tends to spiral. Expect a lot of three-pointers, a lot of fast breaks, and not a lot of half-court defense from either side.

The MIA -13.5 spread is one of the largest you'll see in a regular-season NBA game, and the BKN +550 moneyline puts an exclamation point on just how dire things are in Brooklyn. The Nets at 15-46 are having the worst season in recent franchise history, and there's no relief in sight when you walk into the Kaseya Center to face a Miami team that's fighting tooth and nail for playoff positioning. Brooklyn has been gutted by trades and roster turnover, and what's left is a collection of young players and G League call-ups who are gaining experience but simply aren't equipped to compete with functional NBA rosters for 48 minutes. The Nets are the kind of opponent that teams love to see on their schedule because it's essentially a guaranteed win.

Miami at 33-29 is in the thick of the Eastern Conference playoff race, and every game matters at this point in the season. The Heat have reinvented themselves since trading Jimmy Butler to the Warriors, and Bam Adebayo has stepped into the undisputed franchise player role with authority. Adebayo's two-way dominance, his ability to anchor the defense while facilitating the offense from the high post, gives Miami an identity that's sustainable regardless of who else is on the roster. Tyler Herro has been excellent as the primary scoring option, and the supporting cast has bought into Erik Spoelstra's system in a way that maximizes their collective talent. Miami's culture of toughness and accountability means they don't take games off, even against the worst teams in the league.

The 226.5 total is on the lower end of Thursday's board, and it reflects Miami's defensive identity and the likelihood that the Heat control the pace from start to finish. When you're a 13.5-point favorite at home, you don't need to push the pace or take risks on offense. Miami is going to play their game, execute in the half court, get stops on defense, and let the natural talent gap do the work. Brooklyn's offense is going to struggle to generate quality looks against Spoelstra's scheme, and the Nets don't have a player who can consistently create his own shot and break down the Heat's defense in one-on-one situations.

Here's the one thing to watch in this game: blowout fatigue. When spreads get to 13.5 points, the question isn't whether Miami wins, it's whether they win by two touchdowns or settle for a comfortable 10-point victory. The Heat's bench could see extended run in the second half if this game gets out of hand early, and that creates some unpredictability for the final margin. Brooklyn's young players will compete, because that's what young players do when they're trying to earn roster spots for next season, and that hustle can sometimes keep the score closer than the talent gap suggests. But make no mistake, Miami is the far superior team here, and the Heat are going to handle their business at home.

Moneyline

This is a sneaky good matchup between two teams that are both very much in the playoff conversation. Toronto at 35-26 has been one of the biggest surprises in the Eastern Conference, and the addition of Brandon Ingram from New Orleans has given the Raptors the kind of versatile scoring wing they've been missing since Kawhi Leonard left years ago. Ingram's ability to create his own shot from the midrange, finish at the rim, and facilitate for his teammates has elevated Toronto's offense to another level, and the Raptors' record reflects a team that's figured out its identity and is playing with genuine confidence. Scottie Barnes continues to blossom into one of the most unique talents in the league, and the Ingram-Barnes pairing gives Toronto a multi-positional scoring duo that can attack defenses in a variety of ways.

Minnesota at 39-23 has been one of the most consistent teams in the Western Conference all season, and the Timberwolves' defense has been the foundation of their success. Anthony Edwards continues his ascent into superstardom, and his ability to score 30 on any given night while playing physical, engaged defense makes him one of the most complete young players in the league. Rudy Gobert anchors a defense that's been elite at protecting the paint, and his presence on the back line gives Minnesota the kind of rim protection that allows their perimeter defenders to be aggressive without fear of giving up easy baskets. The Timberwolves at Target Center have been difficult to beat, and the home crowd has embraced this team in a way that creates a real advantage.

The MIN -6.5 spread is significant, and it suggests the market views this as a comfortable Minnesota win despite Toronto's solid record. Playing on the road in the Western Conference is always a challenge for Eastern Conference teams, and the Raptors are going to need their best effort to hang with a Timberwolves team that's been dominant at home. The key matchup is Ingram versus Edwards on the wing, and how Toronto's defense handles Minnesota's ability to generate looks through Edwards in isolation, through Gobert on lobs and put-backs, and through their supporting cast in catch-and-shoot situations. If the Timberwolves' defense can contain Ingram and force Toronto's secondary players to beat them, this could get out of hand.

The 227.5 total reflects Minnesota's defensive identity and the likelihood that this game is played at a controlled pace. The Timberwolves don't need to run to win, and they're perfectly content to grind games down in the half court and let their defense create transition opportunities. Toronto is going to need to be efficient in the half court, because getting into a track meet with Minnesota at altitude is a losing proposition. Ingram's midrange game and Barnes' ability to attack from multiple spots on the floor are Toronto's best weapons for staying in this game, but the Raptors are going to need contributions from their supporting cast to keep the margin manageable. Minnesota's depth and defensive versatility should ultimately be the difference in a game that the Timberwolves control from the second quarter onward.

Chicago at 25-37 is in no-man's land this season: not bad enough to tank effectively and not good enough to compete for a playoff spot. The trade that sent Zach LaVine to the Sacramento Kings stripped the Bulls of their most dynamic scorer and left them with a roster that lacks a true go-to offensive weapon in crunch-time situations. Coby White has been solid as the primary ball-handler, and some of Chicago's young pieces have shown improvement, but the ceiling for this group is limited without a star-caliber player to build around. Road games in the Western Conference are where these limitations get exposed, and a trip to Phoenix to face a 35-26 Suns team is about as tough a draw as the schedule can offer.

Phoenix at 35-26 has had a quietly excellent season since restructuring their roster around Devin Booker and Bradley Beal. The Suns have found a new identity built on offensive firepower and improved defensive effort, and they've been one of the better home teams in the Western Conference all season. Booker continues to be one of the most lethal scorers in basketball, and his ability to take over games in the third and fourth quarters is what separates Phoenix from the pack of teams jockeying for playoff positioning. The Footprint Center crowd has been energized by the Suns' resurgence, and home games against struggling teams like Chicago are opportunities to pile up comfortable wins.

The PHX -10.5 spread is the second-largest on Thursday's board, and the PHX -535 moneyline makes it clear that the market views this as a lopsided matchup. Chicago doesn't have the defensive personnel to slow down Booker and Beal on the perimeter, and the Bulls' offense without LaVine is going to struggle to generate enough quality looks to stay within striking distance. Phoenix has the kind of offensive versatility that can exploit mismatches at every position, and when Booker gets going in front of a supportive home crowd, the Suns can put up 130 without breaking a sweat. The question isn't whether Phoenix wins, it's whether they win by enough to cover a double-digit spread.

The 224.5 total is interesting because Phoenix has the firepower to push this well over that number by themselves if their offense is clicking. But the Bulls have actually played some competitive, grind-it-out basketball on the road recently, and they might be able to keep the pace slow enough to keep this total in play. Chicago's defense has shown some resilience this season, and their ability to compete even in games they're expected to lose is a credit to the coaching staff and the competitive spirit of the roster. That said, Phoenix at home against a 25-37 team is the kind of spot where the Suns should be able to name their score and cruise through the fourth quarter with the deep bench getting extended run.

If you thought Jazz-Wizards was the tank bowl of the night, this one gives it serious competition. New Orleans at 19-44 and Sacramento at 14-49 are both having miserable seasons, and their Thursday night meeting in Sacramento is a battle between two franchises that are fully focused on the future rather than the present. The Pelicans have been decimated by injuries all season, and their inability to keep their core healthy has turned what was supposed to be a competitive year into another lost season. New Orleans has the better record of the two, which is why they're favored, but being the better of two very bad teams isn't exactly a ringing endorsement.

Sacramento at 14-49 has the worst record in the entire NBA, and this season has been an unmitigated disaster from start to finish. The Kings traded Fox in February 2025, which was the right long-term move but left the franchise without its most dynamic player. LaVine was supposed to provide some scoring stability, but the fit hasn't worked the way Sacramento hoped, and the roster around him simply doesn't have enough talent to compete on a nightly basis. Domantas Sabonis continues to put up big numbers, because that's what Sabonis does regardless of the team around him, but the Kings' 14-49 record speaks to a franchise that needs a complete overhaul from top to bottom.

The NOP -4.5 spread is modest for a game between two teams with such poor records, and it reflects the reality that the Pelicans are only marginally better than the Kings right now. New Orleans has some talented pieces when healthy, but "when healthy" has been a condition this franchise has struggled to meet all season. The Pelicans' young players have gotten plenty of run this season, and some of them have shown enough to suggest they could be part of the next competitive core. But for now, this is a team that's counting down the days until the offseason, same as Sacramento.

The 234.5 total is the second-highest on Thursday's board, and like the Jazz-Wizards game, it reflects the lack of defensive intensity from both teams at this point in the season. Neither New Orleans nor Sacramento has any incentive to play tight defense when the priority is development and draft positioning, so expect a lot of offense and not a lot of effort on the defensive end. LaVine's scoring ability gives Sacramento a chance to keep this close, and the Golden 1 Center crowd, however sparse it might be, could give the Kings an emotional lift. But New Orleans has been the marginally better team this season, and their modest edge in talent should be enough to cover a 4.5-point spread against the league's worst team. This is the late-night game that puts you to sleep before the Lakers and Nuggets finish in Denver.
