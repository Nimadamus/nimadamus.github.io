Nationals Under 3.5 And Angels Under 3.5: Bryce Miller's 1.33 ERA And Shane McClanahan's 2.85 ERA Headline Two Team Total Unders

June 12, 2026| 9 min read| BetLegend

Friday night gives us two team total unders that come from the same place, and it is not the offenses. It is the arms standing 60 feet, 6 inches away. Washington Nationals team total under 3.5 at +113 on a 1.5-unit ticket and Los Angeles Angels team total under 3.5 at -125 on a 2-unit ticket are both bets on the visiting starter, not against the home lineup. In Washington, the Mariners hand the ball to Bryce Miller, who has been close to unhittable since returning, a 1.33 ERA and a 0.78 WHIP that says runners simply are not reaching base. In Anaheim, the Rays send out Shane McClanahan, a former strikeout king carrying a 2.85 ERA and 59 punchouts across 60 innings. Two elite starts, two lineups that have to manufacture everything against them, two unders. Let me walk through why the pitching, not a cold bat, is the thesis on both.

Bryce Miller Is The Whole Nationals Under

Here is the thing about the Nationals under, and it is important to be honest about it: Washington is not a weak offense. The Nationals are actually scoring 5.39 runs per game on the season, the highest mark of any team in this two-game breakdown, with 372 runs on the board through 69 games and a .742 team OPS. This is not a punchless lineup you are fading on reputation. This is a team total under that lives and dies entirely on the man throwing against them, and that man is the reason the number is worth 1.5 units.

Bryce Miller has been the best version of himself in 2026. Across four starts and 27 innings, he owns a 1.33 ERA, a 0.78 WHIP, and 29 strikeouts against a 9.67 K/9. That 0.78 WHIP is the number that matters most for a team total under. It means Miller is allowing well under one baserunner per inning, and a lineup that cannot get runners on base cannot score four-plus runs no matter how good its rate stats look over a full season. Washington's 5.39 runs per game is a season-long average built across every matchup. On a given night against a starter limiting traffic to a 0.78 WHIP clip, the realistic ceiling compresses fast, and under 3.5 is exactly the kind of number that compression lives below.

The +113 price is what makes it a play rather than a coin flip. A team total under 3.5 at plus money means the market itself is leaning slightly toward Washington reaching four, almost certainly a nod to that 5.39 runs-per-game offense. That is the value. You are getting a plus-money return on a bet whose single most important variable, the opposing starter's ability to keep the bases empty, points hard toward the under. When the strongest input on the board is on your side and you are still being paid plus money, the math is the bet.

Zack Littell Does Not Change The Read

It is fair to ask why the same logic does not flip the bet. Washington counters with Zack Littell, who carries a 4.76 ERA and a 1.31 WHIP across nine starts and 64.1 innings, so the Nationals are not exactly running an ace of their own out there. But this is a team total under on Washington's scoring, not a game total, so Littell's ERA is irrelevant to the ticket. What matters is the Seattle bullpen behind Miller, and the Mariners own a 3.59 team ERA with a 1.20 WHIP, the best pitching staff in this two-game look. Miller sets the table by keeping the bases empty, and a top-five run-prevention staff finishes the job. The path to Washington reaching four against that combination is narrow, and at +113 you are paid to bet that it stays narrow.

Shane McClanahan Smothers A Cold Angels Lineup

The Angels under is a cleaner story because both inputs point the same way. Los Angeles is hitting .233 as a team with a .701 OPS and is mired at 27-42 with a minus-42 run differential, one of the bottom offenses in the American League. They are scoring 4.46 runs per game, and the version of that lineup that shows up against quality left-handed pitching has been worse than the headline. Now they draw Shane McClanahan, the former Tampa Bay strikeout machine, who is carrying a 2.85 ERA, a 1.10 WHIP, and 59 strikeouts over 60 innings across 12 starts this season.

McClanahan at a 1.10 WHIP against a .233 lineup is the matchup you want for a team total under. The Angels need traffic to score, and McClanahan does not allow much of it. He is missing bats at an 8.85 K/9 clip, which matters against a lineup that already strikes out a ton, 649 team strikeouts on the year, the most of any club in this breakdown. Strikeouts are rallies that never start. A team that whiffs as often as the Angels do, facing a left-hander who misses bats and limits walks, has a genuinely hard time stringing together the three-plus hits in an inning it takes to clear four runs.

Why Both Unders Share A Spine: Bryce Miller (1.33 ERA, 0.78 WHIP) and Shane McClanahan (2.85 ERA, 1.10 WHIP) are both elite at the one skill that kills a team total, keeping the bases empty. Washington's real offense gets neutralized by traffic suppression; the Angels' cold offense gets buried by it. Two different lineups, one pitching-driven thesis.

Sam Aldegheri And The Rays Staff Hold Up The Other Side

The Angels send out Sam Aldegheri, a young left-hander with a small-sample 2.25 ERA across his early work, but again, this is a team total on the Angels, so Aldegheri's line does not touch the ticket. What backs the McClanahan start is a Tampa Bay staff sitting at a 3.91 team ERA. McClanahan goes deep enough to keep the Angels chasing, and a competent Rays bullpen closes it. The -125 price reflects that this is the cleaner of the two unders, both inputs aligned, and 2 units is the heavier stake of the pair for exactly that reason. The Nationals under at 1.5 units carries slightly more variance because Washington's bats are genuinely live, which is why it gets the smaller stake and the plus-money cushion.

The Honest Counterpoint

Both unders carry real risk and it would be dishonest to bury it. The Nationals are the scariest under fade on the board precisely because they score 5.39 runs a game. Bryce Miller's sparkling line comes in a small four-start sample of 27 innings, and one mistake count against a live home lineup can produce a three-run inning that clears the number on its own. Plus money exists for a reason. The Angels under is steadier, but a .233 lineup that lives on occasional power can still run into one swing, and Sam Aldegheri giving Tampa Bay a slugfest is not the scenario you need anyway, since this is the Angels' number, not the game total.

The structural read still holds on both. The single biggest lever on a team total under is the opposing starter's ability to suppress baserunners, and Miller at a 0.78 WHIP and McClanahan at a 1.10 WHIP are two of the better arms either of these lineups will see all month. Sizing reflects the variance: 1.5 units on the plus-money Nationals number where the offense is genuinely live, 2 units on the cleaner Angels number where both inputs line up. Two pitching-driven unders, one clear thesis.

The Bottom Line

Friday night, fade the offense by betting the arm. Bryce Miller and his 0.78 WHIP are the entire case for Washington under 3.5 at +113, a plus-money return on a bet whose most important variable points firmly toward the under even against a 5.39-run offense. Shane McClanahan and his 2.85 ERA are the case for the Angels under 3.5 at -125, a strikeout artist against a .233 lineup that already whiffs more than anyone in this breakdown. Take both team total unders, lean on the pitching, and let two elite starters do what elite starters do, which is keep the bases empty and the run column low.
