Dodgers-Yankees Under 9, Guardians Moneyline, Orioles and Rangers Team Total Unders and a Coors Field Over: A Five-Play MLB Card

July 17, 2026|9 min read|BetLegend

On July 8, Jared Jones threw six innings against the Atlanta Braves and gave up nothing. No hits. No walks. Eight strikeouts. Pittsburgh lost the game 3-0. That is the sport in one line, and it is the reason tonight's card is built the way it is: five tickets, most of them bought on run prevention, and a clear-eyed understanding that the arm doing the preventing is not always the arm you have money on. Baseball comes back from the All-Star break tonight after four dark days, and the fifteen-game board offers five plays worth a ticket. Two of them are pitching sides in the Bronx and Cleveland. Two more are team-total unders behind quality arms in Houston and Atlanta. And one is a deliberate swing the other way at Coors Field, where the thin air turns run prevention into a rumor and the smart money climbs onto the over. Total exposure is 8 units.

Under 9: The Bronx Total Is Priced Off A Name

Getting a plus number on a total is rare, and getting one on a game with the Dodgers in it is rarer still. The market has this at nine, and it took the Dodgers' reputation and the Yankees' brand to get it there. Neither one is swinging a bat tonight.

Because here is the part the number does not seem to have absorbed: Aaron Judge and Giancarlo Stanton are both on the 10-day injured list. Judge is dealing with a rib issue and was scheduled for follow-up imaging during the break. Stanton has been working back from a calf problem and has resumed running after a PRP injection. The two most dangerous right-handed bats in the sport are watching this one, and the total is still nine.

What is left is a Yankees club that has quietly built the better run-prevention machine of the two. New York's staff owns a 3.39 team ERA and a 1.19 WHIP, the lowest ERA of any team involved in tonight's card, and the Yankees have allowed 3.86 runs a game across 96 games. They are also playing their most disciplined baseball of the season, and they did it without Judge. Four straight wins, and the last three were 5-3, 4-2 and 5-3 in Washington: eight, six and eight total runs. Every one of those lands under tonight's number.

Gerrit Cole is at 3-4 with a 4.04 ERA across nine starts, and the shape of his last month matters more than the season line. He has gone 4.1, 5.1, 5.0 and 6.1 innings in his last four turns, allowing five, four, two and three earned runs, and his July 8 outing against Tampa Bay was six and a third with six strikeouts and zero home runs. The workload is climbing in a straight line. He still leads with the four-seam at 45 percent usage and 94 mph, backing it with a 19 percent slider and a 15 percent changeup, and across the full season he has walked eleven men in 49 innings. A starter who does not give away free bases is a starter who does not hand the Dodgers a crooked inning.

Roki Sasaki is the reason this number is not eight and a half. He carries a 5.33 ERA and 19 home runs allowed in 81 innings, and his July 2 start against San Diego was three innings, six earned runs, three homers. That is a real scar. But look at the mix rather than the ERA: 43 percent four-seam at 97.6 mph, a 25 percent splitter at 90, a 21 percent slider. That is swing-and-miss stuff, 8.89 strikeouts per nine, and his last time out he gave the Dodgers six innings on three earned against Colorado. The Yankees are not a free-swinging lineup that hands a splitter guy easy outs, but they are also not a lineup that has been scoring in bunches. New York is at 4.81 runs a game on the year. Los Angeles is at 5.22 and just got swept at home by Arizona 9-3, 9-2 and 5-3, scoring three, two and three.

The handicap: Both tickets buy the same product. The Under buys a Yankees staff at a 3.39 ERA plus a Dodgers offense that scored eight runs total in its last three games. The Guardians moneyline buys a rotation arm at 10.64 strikeouts per nine and a closer with 28 saves. Neither play needs a hitter to do anything.

Guardians Moneyline: Four Wins, Seven Runs Allowed

Cleveland walked into the break on a four-game winning streak, and the way they did it is the whole bet. Five to two in Minnesota. Three to two, four to one, five to two in Miami. Four wins, seven total runs allowed. This is a 51-46 team with a minus-two run differential and a 3.97 runs-per-game offense, which is to say Cleveland is not going to bludgeon anyone. They are going to hold you to two.

Gavin Williams is the best pitcher on either side of this game and it is not particularly close. He is 10-4 with a 3.81 ERA, 134 strikeouts in 113.1 innings and a 1.15 WHIP, holding hitters to .222, and he arrived at the break with his best start of the year: seven innings against Minnesota on July 9, three hits, eleven strikeouts, one walk. What makes him awkward to face is that he does not have a primary pitch. The sweeper leads at 26.7 percent, the curveball is at 22.5, the four-seam is only 22.9, the sinker sits at 19 percent and touches 95.6 mph, and there is a cutter underneath. Five pitches, none of them over 27 percent usage. A lineup that has not seen him gets no pattern to sit on.

Pittsburgh is the live problem here, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. The Pirates come in 50-47 and 7-3 in their last ten, they scored fourteen runs against Milwaukee on July 12, and their 5.32 runs per game is the highest of any team playing in tonight's two games. They also have Jared Jones, the man who threw those six hitless innings, running a 10.03 strikeouts per nine on a 98.6 mph four-seam and a 31.9 percent slider. His 4.37 ERA across eight starts and 35 innings undersells what he has been recently. At -122 Cleveland is roughly a coin flip with a small tax, and the honest read is that this is close to one.

Take it anyway, and take it for a reason: Jones has thrown 35 innings across eight starts, four and a half a turn, which means Pittsburgh's bullpen is getting the middle innings of a game Cleveland can win late. Williams has 113 innings behind him. The Guardians are 24-22 at home. Cade Smith is waiting with 28 saves, a 2.84 ERA and 63 strikeouts in 44.1 innings. One unit.

Reds vs Rockies Over 12: The Coors Field Swing

Every other ticket on this card is a bet on run prevention. This one is the deliberate exception, and it lives at the one address in the sport where run prevention goes to die. Coors Field sits a mile above sea level, the baseball travels, the breaking stuff does not break, and the outfield is the largest in baseball so balls that should be caught fall in. The number here is twelve, which is enormous by the standards of any other park, and at Coors it is a number the building has cleared plenty of times on its own.

Start with the home side. The Rockies score 5.06 runs a game at Coors this season with a .785 team OPS at home, a full run better than their .710 mark on the road, and their own pitching staff owns a 5.84 ERA in home games. When Colorado hosts, both dugouts eat. Tonight they hand the ball to Gabriel Hughes, a young right-hander with barely any big-league track record to hang a total on, which cuts both ways but does nothing to make Coors play smaller. Cincinnati counters with Brady Singer, and Singer is exactly the arm you want on the mound when you are laying the over. He is 3-9 with a 4.72 ERA, a 1.47 WHIP and 20 home runs allowed across 89.2 innings, he holds hitters to a fat .280 average, and he is a sinker-heavy contact pitcher whose game plan asks hitters to put the ball in play. Ask hitters to put the ball in play at altitude and you are asking for trouble.

The one honest caveat is the Reds offense, which is not a juggernaut. Cincinnati is at 4.16 runs a game with a .230 team average, so this is not two lineups that mash on paper. But the Reds do carry real thump, 119 home runs on the year, and Coors flatters exactly that profile: pull-side power that clears fences it would not clear anywhere else. Twelve runs sounds like a lot until you remember where the game is played and who is pitching it. A unit and a half on the Over 12 at -110.

Orioles Team Total Under 4.5: Lambert Has Been The Real Thing

This one narrows the bet down to a single lineup. We are not touching the Astros side of the ledger and we are not touching the game total. The play is simply that the Baltimore Orioles, as a team, do not cross four and a half runs, and the reason to believe that starts with the man on the Houston mound.

Peter Lambert has been one of the quieter good stories in the American League this year. He is 8-5 with a 3.14 ERA, 86 innings, 81 strikeouts and a 1.12 WHIP, and the number that matters most for a team-total under is the batting average against: hitters are managing just .201 off him. He misses bats at an 8.48-per-nine clip and he keeps traffic off the bases, which is the exact combination that strangles a single lineup's run total. Baltimore is a middle-of-the-pack offense at 4.61 runs a game with a .239 team average, good enough to punish mistakes but not the kind of relentless lineup that grinds out five against a pitcher who is not making many.

Add the venue. This game is at Daikin Park in Houston against a first-place-caliber pitching operation, not a bandbox, and the Orioles have to string together the kind of two-out rallies that Lambert's .201 average against has been shutting down all year. If Lambert gives Houston six innings of the baseball he has thrown most of the season, Baltimore needs its bullpen touches and a late run or two just to sniff the number. One unit on the Orioles team total Under 4.5 at -133.

Rangers Team Total Under 3.5: Chris Sale Is The Whole Bet

This is the biggest play on the card at three units, and it is the simplest to explain. Chris Sale is pitching, the Texas Rangers do not score much, and three and a half is a number a Sale start clears about as often as any team total in the league.

Sale is having the kind of season that reminds everyone what he is. He is 9-6 with a 2.20 ERA across 98 innings, 117 strikeouts against just 25 walks, a 1.11 WHIP and a 10.74 strikeouts-per-nine rate. That is an ace operating at the peak of his powers, and a team total under is the cleanest way to buy it. You are not asking Sale to win the game or the Braves bullpen to hold a lead. You are asking one specific, below-average offense to fail to reach four runs against the best pitcher it will see all week. Sale's job is to strike out the middle of the order and keep the Rangers off the board for six or seven, and he has done exactly that to better lineups than this one.

The Rangers are the softest offense in this matchup and one of the softer ones on the whole slate. Texas sits at 4.16 runs a game with a .245 team average and 109 home runs, a lineup that has spent the season scuffling to string hits together. Against a left-hander with Sale's swing-and-miss profile, in Atlanta, that offense is being asked to solve a problem it has struggled with against far lesser arms. The path to a loss here is a cheap Sale mistake or an early exit, but at a 2.20 ERA and 98 innings of workload behind him, neither is the way to bet. Three units on the Rangers team total Under 3.5 at -140.

What Can Beat It

Five plays, five clean failure paths, and none is exotic.

The Under dies if Sasaki is the version who gave up three homers in three innings against San Diego. Yankee Stadium punishes exactly the mistake he makes most, and 19 home runs in 81 innings is not a fluke sample. It also dies if the Dodgers' bats simply wake up after four days off, because a 5.22-run offense that has been napping is still a 5.22-run offense. Nine is not a wall.

The Guardians play dies if Jared Jones pitches like he did on July 8 and Cleveland's 3.97-run offense does what it usually does, which is very little. Pittsburgh has won three straight and hit fourteen in its last game. If Jones gets through five and hands a lead to his pen, -122 was too much to lay on a team with a negative run differential.

The Coors over dies the way overs at altitude occasionally do: a low-scoring outlier where the wind blows in and the pitching holds, which happens more than the average bettor thinks even in Denver. Buying the over means accepting the rare quiet night at the one park most likely to give you a loud one. The Orioles under dies if Baltimore catches Peter Lambert on an off night and a couple of the .239 hitters run into pitches early, because a middle-tier offense only needs one crooked inning to clear 4.5. And the Rangers under, the biggest play, dies the only two ways a Sale start ever does: an early hook that hands the game to the bullpen, or one three-run mistake before he settles in. All five risks are priced. None changes the read.

The Bottom Line

Baseball's second half opens with five games worth a ticket, and four of them tell the same story. Dodgers vs Yankees Under 9 at +100 is the anchor at a unit and a half: a plus price on a total propped up by a Dodgers reputation their last three games did not earn and a Yankees lineup missing Judge and Stanton, against a staff at a 3.39 ERA and a Gerrit Cole who has built his workload back to six and a third. Cleveland Guardians moneyline at -122 is one unit on Gavin Williams and a five-pitch mix nobody in that Pittsburgh lineup has timed, backed by a team that just won four in a row while allowing seven runs. The Orioles team total Under 4.5 at -133 is one unit on Peter Lambert holding a .201 average against, and the Rangers team total Under 3.5 at -140 is the biggest swing on the card at three units, a clean way to buy Chris Sale and his 2.20 ERA against the softest offense he will see this week. The lone counterpunch is the Reds vs Rockies Over 12 at -110 for a unit and a half, because at Coors Field the smart money stops preventing runs and starts counting them. Eight units, four bought on run prevention and one bought on the one park where prevention is a fantasy, and not one of them needs a hitter to be a hero.
