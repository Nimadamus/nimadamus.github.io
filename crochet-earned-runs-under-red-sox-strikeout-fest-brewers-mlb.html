255 Strikeouts Don't Lie

When you lead all of baseball in strikeouts, it isn't because you got lucky. It's because your stuff is genuinely unhittable on a consistent basis, and Garrett Crochet's stuff is exactly that. The fastball sits 96-98 and explodes through the zone with ride that hitters consistently underestimate. His slider has sharp two-plane break that freezes right-handed hitters and gets lefties to chase out of the zone. And the volume of strikeouts isn't just about pure stuff, it's about the confidence to attack hitters deep in counts, knowing that your best pitch is better than their best swing.

Through his first two starts of 2026, Crochet has punched out 15 batters in 11 innings. That 31.9% K rate is actually higher than his full-season mark from last year, which suggests his stuff hasn't just returned to the same level, it might be better. When a pitcher is missing bats at this rate, the probability of giving up earned runs drops dramatically because so many at-bats end in strikeouts rather than balls in play. Fewer balls in play means fewer opportunities for runners to reach base, and fewer runners means fewer runs. It's the simplest equation in baseball, and Crochet is the living embodiment of it.

Misiorowski on the Other Side Creates a True Pitchers' Duel

Here's what makes this game even more attractive for the under: Milwaukee is sending Misiorowski to the mound, and he's been every bit as dominant as Crochet to start the year. Misiorowski has racked up 18 strikeouts in 11 innings pitched, which is an absurd K rate that puts him among the league leaders. When both starters in a game are striking out batters at an elite clip, the game flow naturally trends toward low scoring. Both lineups are going to have short at-bats, high whiff rates, and limited opportunities to string together the kind of multi-hit rallies that produce crooked numbers.

The 6.5 game total reflects this reality. The oddsmakers see two strikeout artists squaring off and have priced the game accordingly. But for the Crochet ER under prop specifically, the opposing pitcher matters because it influences how the game plays out. If Misiorowski is also mowing through the Boston lineup, the game stays tight, the pressure on each pitcher remains low, and neither starter has to deal with the cascading effect of a big inning on the other side. In a 2-1 or 3-2 type game, Crochet's earned runs are almost certainly going to be 1 or fewer, which is exactly what we need.

Milwaukee's Back-to-Back Road Trip Is a Grind

The Brewers are playing their second consecutive game in Boston, and the travel-and-fatigue factor for a road back-to-back is a real edge for the pitcher they're facing. Milwaukee's hitters had to prepare for a game yesterday, play nine innings, and then come back and do it all over again tonight. The recovery window is tight, the preparation time for Crochet's specific pitch mix is limited, and the cumulative fatigue of a road trip chips away at the kind of sharp, patient at-bats that a team needs to score against an elite strikeout pitcher.

Crochet's pitch mix is particularly well-suited to exploit tired hitters. His fastball plays up because of the ride, which means hitters need to be precise with their timing to make solid contact. When you're even slightly fatigued, that timing window shrinks, and the result is either a swing and miss or a pop-up off the end of the bat. The slider is the same story: it breaks later than tired eyes expect, and the result is either a frozen called strike or a chase swing that ends the at-bat. Crochet facing a back-to-back opponent at Fenway Park is a recipe for a dominant pitching performance with minimal earned run damage.
