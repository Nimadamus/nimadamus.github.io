5-Game Wednesday Slate

NHL March 4, 2026

Wednesday's five-game slate features a true mismatch of the highest order when Carolina's 38-15-6 juggernaut travels to Vancouver, owners of the worst home record in the NHL. Vegas visits a resurgent Detroit squad with John Gibson's return status looming large. Toronto tries to snap a brutal four-game losing streak in New Jersey. The Islanders carry 75 points into Anaheim against an injury-depleted Ducks roster. And St. Louis heads to Seattle for a rematch just six days after a 5-1 Blues blowout in Missouri.

Golden Knights @ Red Wings

Wednesday, 7:00 PM ET | Little Caesars Arena, Detroit, MI

This is a fascinating cross-conference clash between two teams in very different situations but with the same goal: solidifying their playoff positioning. Vegas at 28-18-14 has accumulated 70 points, but that 14 in the overtime loss column is alarming. The Golden Knights have been in an absurd number of close games and keep finding themselves on the wrong end of extra hockey. Meanwhile, Detroit at 35-20-6 with 76 points has been one of the most pleasant stories of the 2025-26 season, establishing themselves as legitimate Eastern Conference contenders. The Red Wings' balanced attack and defensive discipline have earned them that record, and Little Caesars Arena has been a difficult place to play for visitors all season long.

Jack Eichel continues to be one of the most dynamic offensive players in the entire NHL, sitting at 68 points with 21 goals and 47 assists through the season. He's the engine that makes Vegas go, and when he's on his game, the Golden Knights can compete with anyone. Pavel Dorofeyev has been a revelation with 28 goals and 47 points, giving Vegas a legitimate secondary scoring threat that they've desperately needed. But this team has been inconsistent lately, going 3-5-2 in their last 10 games, and that's a trend that should concern anyone backing the Knights. Mitch Marner remains day-to-day with an estimated return of March 3, so his status for this one is up in the air, and Shea Theodore is also day-to-day with an illness.

Detroit counters with Alex DeBrincat's 31 goals and 60 points, plus Lucas Raymond's 64-point campaign that includes 20 goals and 44 assists. Raymond has been especially dangerous as a playmaker this season, and his ability to create for others makes Detroit's top six extremely difficult to contain. The big question here is in goal, where John Gibson has been outstanding since arriving in Detroit, posting a 23-12-2 record with roughly a 2.55 GAA and .905 save percentage. Gibson is listed as day-to-day with an estimated return of March 4, so his availability could be the deciding factor. If Gibson goes, Detroit has a clear edge in net over Akira Schmid, whose 16-7-6 record looks solid but the .895 save percentage tells you he's been surviving more than dominating.

The season series has been a defensive affair, with Vegas winning the only meeting 1-0 back on November 4. Detroit has gone 4-4-2 in their last 10, so neither team is exactly rolling into this one with momentum. This feels like a game that could be decided by a single goal or a power play conversion. Vegas' 25.6% power play is formidable, but Detroit's 78.9% penalty kill is solid enough to withstand it. If Gibson returns, the Red Wings have the home-ice edge and the better overall record. If he doesn't, this becomes a much more interesting proposition for Vegas.

Game 2
ESPN+

Maple Leafs @ Devils

Wednesday, 7:00 PM ET | Prudential Center, Newark, NJ

Toronto limps into Newark on a four-game losing streak, and the desperation level for this Maple Leafs team is reaching critical mass. At 27-24-10 with 64 points, the Leafs are in serious danger of missing the playoffs entirely, and when you consider the talent on this roster, that's a damning indictment of how this season has gone. William Nylander's 56 points (20 goals, 36 assists) have been the lone consistent bright spot offensively, but he can't carry this team by himself, and the supporting cast has been frustratingly inconsistent for months. Chris Tanev's absence on IR with a groin injury (not expected back until April 4) has gutted the defensive corps, and the Leafs' 3.43 goals against per game tells you everything about how porous they've been without him.

New Jersey at 29-29-2 with 60 points isn't exactly lighting the world on fire either, but the Devils have been playing better hockey recently, highlighted by a 3-1 win over St. Louis on February 28. That result snapped what had been a mediocre stretch, and the Devils need every point they can get if they want to stay in the playoff conversation. Nico Hischier's 43 points with 20 goals have been steady if unspectacular, and the Devils' identity this season has been built around structure rather than star power. Their 2.47 goals per game ranks near the bottom of the league, but the 3.03 goals against is manageable enough to keep them in games most nights. The 20.3% power play has been a modest weapon, and the 79.6% penalty kill is respectable without being elite.

The goaltending matchup pits Joseph Woll (13-9-4, 3.05 GAA, .907 SV%) against Jacob Markstrom (16-14-1, 3.13 GAA, .886 SV%), and neither goalie has been particularly dominant. Markstrom's .886 save percentage is concerning for a team that needs its goaltender to steal a game here and there, and Woll has been inconsistent enough that Toronto can never feel completely comfortable with a lead. The season series is tied 1-1, which means this rubber match carries extra weight for both teams in terms of tiebreaker implications and bragging rights.

Here's what makes this game intriguing: both teams are desperate, but for different reasons. Toronto is desperate to stop the bleeding and prove that the losing streak is a blip rather than a slide toward oblivion. New Jersey is desperate to keep pace in a wild card race where every point matters. Toronto's 19.6% power play isn't great, but their 83.1% penalty kill is one of the better marks in the league and could be the difference if this game stays tight. The Leafs have the talent advantage, but talent hasn't been winning them games lately. Sometimes a team on a losing streak is the worst team to back, and sometimes they're overdue. That tension is what makes this one worth watching.

Hurricanes @ Canucks

Wednesday, 10:00 PM ET | Rogers Arena, Vancouver, BC

If you're looking for the biggest mismatch on Wednesday's board, you've found it. Carolina at 38-15-6 with 82 points is the cream of the Eastern Conference crop, and they're heading into Rogers Arena to face a Vancouver team at 18-34-7 with a pathetic 43 points that has been the worst team in the NHL at home this season. The Hurricanes are 7-1-2 in their last 10 games, playing some of the best hockey in the entire league right now. Vancouver is 1-7-2 over the same stretch and is currently mired in a five-to-six game losing streak that shows no signs of ending. This is a team that has completely given up on the season, and the injury list reads like a hospital ward: Thatcher Demko out for the season after hip surgery, Tyler Myers out, and Jesper Lekkerimaki done with shoulder surgery. There's simply nothing left in the tank for the Canucks.

Carolina's numbers are ridiculous across the board. They're scoring between 3.40 and 3.50 goals per game while allowing just 2.50 to 2.80, generating 32.1 shots on goal per game, and running a power play at 21.9% with an 80.8% penalty kill. Sebastian Aho's 59 points anchor the offense alongside Seth Jarvis, who has been a revelation with 26 goals and 46 points. The Hurricanes play with a suffocating defensive structure that forces turnovers and transitions into instant offense, and they've lost Pyotr Kochetkov for the season to surgery, but Brendan Bussi has stepped in and been spectacular with a 24-3-1 record, 2.23 GAA, and .906 save percentage. That's the kind of goaltending depth that separates contenders from pretenders.

Vancouver's numbers are equally telling, just in the opposite direction. The Canucks score between 2.00 and 2.48 goals per game, surrender between 3.70 and 3.90 per night, and generate a meager 26.6 shots per game. Their 17.7% power play is below average and their 70.6% penalty kill is one of the worst in the NHL. Elias Pettersson's 35 points have been disappointing by his standards, and with the roster decimated by injuries, there's simply not enough talent healthy to compete with a team like Carolina. Kevin Lankinen has been left hanging out to dry behind a porous defense, posting a 7-20-4 record with a 3.69 GAA and .874 save percentage that reflects the chaos in front of him more than his own ability.

The last time these two teams met, Carolina won 4-3 in overtime with Andrei Svechnikov scoring twice, which tells you that even in a "close" game, the Hurricanes had the firepower to come from behind and finish the job. The -192 moneyline and -1.5 puck line at +140 reflect the massive gap between these teams. The 5.5 total is interesting because Carolina's defensive discipline could keep this game lower than expected, but Vancouver's leaky defense has been allowing goals in bunches all season. This is the kind of game where Carolina controls the puck for 35-plus minutes, generates 35-plus shots, and simply overwhelms a depleted opponent. The only question is the margin of victory.

Islanders @ Ducks

Wednesday, 10:00 PM ET | Honda Center, Anaheim, CA

The Islanders bring 75 points into Honda Center as one of the more quietly impressive teams in the Eastern Conference this season. New York at 35-21-5 has been built exactly the way head coach Patrick Roy wants: structured, defensively responsible, and opportunistic on offense. Their 2.74 goals against per game is one of the better marks in the league, and Ilya Sorokin has been the backbone of that success, posting a 22-13-2 record with a 2.47 GAA and .915 save percentage that makes him one of the best goaltenders in the NHL this season. The Islanders don't dazzle you with highlight-reel offense, but they grind you down with structure and discipline until you break.

Mathew Barzal's 55 points (17 goals, 38 assists) and Bo Horvat's 25 goals give the Islanders two legitimate offensive threats, but it's the team defense that defines this group. Their 81.2% penalty kill is excellent, and while the 15.8% power play is below average, the Islanders compensate by being incredibly disciplined at even strength. They don't give you easy chances, they don't take bad penalties, and they make you earn every inch of ice. That approach translates particularly well on the road, where the Islanders have been a consistently competitive team all season.

Anaheim at 33-23-3 with 69 points has had a breakout season led by the emergence of Cutter Gauthier, who has been sensational with 28 goals and 52 points. The Ducks are scoring 3.25 goals per game and playing with an energy and swagger that has made them one of the surprise teams in the Western Conference. However, the injury bug has bitten hard. Mikael Granlund, Dylan Strome, and Troy Terry are all day-to-day, Frank Vatrano is on IR with a shoulder injury, and Petr Mrazek is done for the season after hip surgery. That's a massive chunk of offensive production either out or questionable, and it leaves Lukas Dostal (24-13-2, 2.97 GAA, .896 SV%) needing to carry an even heavier load in net.

The Islanders won the first meeting of the season 5-2, and that result speaks to the kind of game New York can play when their structure is clicking. The Ducks' 3.49 goals against per game is a concern, especially with key forwards banged up, and Sorokin versus Dostal is a significant goaltending edge for the visitors. Anaheim's 18.3% power play and 77.5% penalty kill are both middle-of-the-road, and without their full complement of forwards, the Ducks could struggle to generate the volume of chances they need to keep pace. Kyle Palmieri is also out for the season for the Islanders and Jonathan Drouin is day-to-day with an estimated return of March 4, but New York's depth has been good enough to absorb losses like that all season.

Blues @ Kraken

Wednesday, 10:00 PM ET | Climate Pledge Arena, Seattle, WA

Six days ago, these two teams met in St. Louis and the Blues absolutely demolished the Kraken 5-1. Now the rematch shifts to Climate Pledge Arena, and the dynamic should be completely different. Seattle at 28-22-9 with 65 points is a legitimate playoff contender that has been formidable on home ice, posting a 16-9-5 record that ranks among the better home marks in the Western Conference. The Kraken know they got embarrassed in Missouri, and there's nothing quite like a rematch on your own ice to light a fire under a team with playoff aspirations. Expect a much more engaged, much more physical Seattle team than what we saw on February 26.

St. Louis at 22-29-9 with 53 points has been one of the most disappointing teams in the league this season, and their road record of 8-17-3 tells you just how brutal they've been away from Enterprise Center. Robert Thomas' 45 points and Pavel Buchnevich's 38 points have provided some offensive life, but the Blues allow 3.42 goals per game and their 73.0% penalty kill is one of the worst in the NHL. That's a catastrophic combination on the road, where you can't afford to give up goals in bunches and then compound the damage by bleeding on the penalty kill. Joel Hofer has been decent in goal with a 14-11-3 record and 2.81 GAA with an .899 save percentage, but he hasn't been good enough to single-handedly overcome the structural issues in front of him.

Seattle's offense runs through Jordan Eberle's 22 goals and 41 points along with Jaden Schwartz's 36-point campaign, and the Kraken's 22.0% power play has been a quietly effective weapon. Their 2.87 goals against per game is solid, and Joey Daccord's 18-13-5 record with a 2.74 GAA and .907 save percentage has given Seattle reliable goaltending that this franchise has craved since its inception. The 72.0% penalty kill is a concern, mirroring St. Louis' struggles in that area, which means special teams on both sides could be a sloppy affair.

Here's the thing about this rematch: the 5-1 result in St. Louis was the anomaly, not the norm. The season series is tied 1-1, and the other meeting was presumably more competitive. St. Louis' 8-17-3 road record is one of the worst in hockey, and Climate Pledge Arena is one of the tougher buildings to play in for visiting teams. The Kraken have been 16-9-5 at home for a reason, and they don't lose twice in a row to the same team at home very often. Seattle's offensive firepower at 2.83 goals per game, combined with their home-ice edge and the motivation of avenging a blowout loss, makes this a compelling spot for the Kraken. St. Louis can certainly compete when their top players show up, but the road has been absolutely brutal for this group all season, and there's no reason to believe that changes in one of the toughest environments in the West.
