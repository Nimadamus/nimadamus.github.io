Red Wings @ Lightning

Monday, 7:00 PM ET | Amalie Arena, Tampa, FL
Puck Line
DET +1.5 (-142) / TB -1.5 (+120)
Moneyline
DET +170 / TB -192
Total
O/U 6.5

There's something almost poetic about the Red Wings visiting Tampa Bay in mid-April with neither team having anything meaningful to play for in the standings, yet the on-ice product still carrying weight for very different reasons. Detroit at 41-30-9 with 91 points has been eliminated for the 10th consecutive season, a drought that has become a defining characteristic of this franchise's post-dynasty era. The Red Wings have talent, they have some promising young pieces, and they've been competitive enough to stay in the conversation for most of the year, but competitive enough doesn't get you into the playoffs when the Eastern Conference is this stacked. Tampa Bay at 49-25-6 with 104 points has already locked down the Atlantic 2 seed and can afford to manage their roster heading into the postseason.

The -192 moneyline for Tampa Bay reflects both the home ice advantage and the quality gap between these two rosters, but there's an interesting wrinkle here. Victor Hedman is listed as out with an IR designation, which means the Lightning's blue line takes a significant hit in terms of both defensive stability and power play quarterbacking. Hedman's absence doesn't make Tampa a bad team by any stretch, they still have Kucherov, Stamkos, and a deep roster built for playoff success, but losing your number one defenseman changes the complexion of any game. Detroit at +170 becomes a little more intriguing when you consider that Tampa might also be resting players with the postseason just around the corner.

Tampa Bay's dominance in this head-to-head matchup has been staggering. The Lightning are 15-5 in their last 20 home games against Detroit, a record that speaks to a near-decade of one-sided results in this building. Amalie Arena has been a house of horrors for the Red Wings, and that historical trend isn't easy to ignore even in a game where Tampa might not be at full strength. Detroit's 1-4 run over their last five games only reinforces the narrative that this is a team limping toward the finish line, playing out the string with an eye on the offseason and whatever changes need to be made to end that 10-year playoff drought.

The 6.5 total is consistent with what we've seen in Tampa Bay games all season. The Lightning have the offensive firepower to push a game into high-scoring territory even without Hedman, and Detroit's defensive structure has been leaky enough throughout the year that goals should flow in both directions. If Tampa treats this as a meaningful game, and there's an argument they should to stay sharp before the playoffs, the Lightning should control the tempo and ride their depth advantage to a comfortable home victory. But if the coaching staff decides to rest key contributors with the postseason in mind, Detroit's +170 price becomes one of the more interesting underdog plays on the Monday slate.

Rangers @ Panthers

Monday, 7:00 PM ET | Amerant Bank Arena, Sunrise, FL
Puck Line
NYR -1.5 (+220) / FLA +1.5 (-270)
Moneyline
NYR -122 / FLA +102
Total
O/U 6.5

This is without question one of the saddest games on the entire Monday slate, and it has nothing to do with the quality of hockey being played. The Florida Panthers, the team that won back-to-back Stanley Cups in 2024 and 2025, sit at 38-38-4 with 80 points and have been eliminated from playoff contention. Let that sink in for a moment. The defending champions, the team that hoisted the Cup just months ago, won't be defending their title this spring. And it gets worse: Florida is absolutely decimated by injuries, with Aleksander Barkov out on IR with a knee injury, Brad Marchand on IR, Sam Bennett listed as day-to-day, Carter Verhaeghe day-to-day, and seven players out in total. This is a shell of the team that dominated the league the last two years.

The Rangers at 33-38-9 with 75 points are also eliminated, which makes this a game between two teams playing out the string with nothing but pride on the line. New York is the slight road favorite at -122, which feels right when you consider Florida's injury carnage. The Panthers are basically dressing an AHL lineup at this point, and the sheer volume of missing talent would be crippling for any team in the league, let alone one that's already been struggling to find wins all season. The Rangers have their own problems, including a brutal 3-game road losing streak and a 3-8 record in their last 11 road games, so this isn't exactly a team brimming with confidence either.

Here's where it gets interesting from a pure hockey perspective: Florida is 5-1 as a home underdog in their last six games in that role. Eliminated teams with nothing to lose can be dangerous, and the Panthers' remaining healthy players know that their performance in these final games will determine roster decisions in the offseason. Young guys fighting for roster spots, veterans auditioning for potential trade destinations, there are plenty of individual motivations even when the team has nothing to play for. The 6.5 total suggests the market expects offense from both sides, and that makes sense when you're dealing with two teams that have no reason to play conservative, defense-first hockey.

The bigger picture here is a sobering reminder of how quickly things can change in the NHL. Florida went from dynasty status to missing the playoffs in one season, largely because of the injury avalanche that hit their roster at the worst possible time. Barkov's knee injury alone would be a season-altering loss for any team, but when you stack Marchand, Bennett, and Verhaeghe on top of it, you're looking at a team that lost its entire identity. The Rangers are a slightly better version of the same story, a team with postseason aspirations that fell apart during the year. This is the kind of April game that nobody circles on their calendar, but for the players on the ice, every shift matters for their future.

Hurricanes @ Flyers

Monday, 7:00 PM ET | Wells Fargo Center, Philadelphia, PA
Puck Line
CAR -1.5 (+215) / PHI +1.5 (-265)
Moneyline
CAR -115 / PHI -105
Total
O/U 5.5

Carolina at 52-22-6 with 110 points has been the class of the Metropolitan Division all season, clinching both the Metro title and the Eastern Conference's top seed. The Hurricanes have been elite in every facet of the game, from their suffocating defensive structure to their relentless forecheck to their ability to grind out wins against quality opponents. But here's the question that hangs over this game like a cloud: with everything locked up and the playoffs right around the corner, how much does Carolina actually invest in this one? The -115 moneyline makes the Hurricanes a narrow road favorite, and that tight price suggests the market isn't entirely sure whether Raleigh will bring their A-game or start the process of resting bodies for what really matters.

Philadelphia at 41-27-12 with 94 points has been eliminated from playoff contention, but the Flyers deserve enormous credit for the season they've put together. This was supposed to be a rebuilding year in Philly, and instead the Flyers pushed all the way to 94 points and were in the hunt until the very end. More importantly, Philadelphia has been playing some of its best hockey of the season over the past two weeks, going 4-1 in their last five games. That kind of late-season form, even for an eliminated team, speaks to the culture and compete level that the Flyers' coaching staff has instilled. The Wells Fargo Center crowd will show up and be loud for this one because Philly fans appreciate effort, and this team has given them plenty of it.

The head-to-head history between these teams is almost comically one-sided in Carolina's favor. The Hurricanes are 8-0 in their last eight meetings against Philadelphia and a mind-boggling 9-0 in their last nine road games at the Wells Fargo Center. That kind of dominance against any single opponent is rare in professional sports, and it creates a psychological edge that's hard to quantify but impossible to ignore. Even with Carolina potentially resting players and Philly riding a hot streak, the Hurricanes have owned this matchup in a way that suggests they simply have a schematic advantage over the Flyers regardless of who's in the lineup. Pyotr Kochetkov being out with a hip injury on IR is worth noting, but Carolina's goaltending depth has been a strength all season.

The 5.5 total is the joint-lowest on this 10-game slate, tied with the Kings-Kraken and Jets-Knights games. Carolina's defensive identity drives that number down, and even with some starters potentially resting, the Hurricanes' system is so well-ingrained that whoever takes the ice will play the same structured, low-event hockey that has defined this team all year. Philadelphia's recent hot streak has been driven by solid defensive play and timely scoring rather than offensive explosions, so the under has a case here. This is a game where the final score might be 3-2 or 2-1, with both teams playing tight and the Hurricanes' head-to-head hex over the Flyers ultimately being the difference in another close road win for Carolina.

Stars @ Maple Leafs

Monday, 7:30 PM ET | Scotiabank Arena, Toronto, ON
Puck Line
DAL -1.5 (+160) / TOR +1.5 (-192)
Moneyline
DAL -170 / TOR +142
Total
O/U 6.5

If you're looking for a franchise in absolute freefall, look no further than the Toronto Maple Leafs. At 32-34-14 with 78 points, Toronto has completely collapsed down the stretch, losing five consecutive games and going a horrifying 2-12 in their last 14 home games at Scotiabank Arena. Let that number sink in: 2-12 at home over nearly a month-long stretch. This is a building that used to be one of the toughest places to play in the entire NHL, and it has become a house of cards that opponents walk into with absolute confidence. The Leafs' season has been a disaster by any metric, and the fact that they were competitive enough earlier in the year to give fans hope makes the collapse even more painful to watch.

Dallas at 48-20-12 with 108 points has clinched the Central Division's second seed and is one of the most complete teams in the Western Conference. The Stars' 3.36 goals per game speaks to an offense that can beat you in multiple ways, and their 28.92% road power play, which ranks second in the entire NHL, is the kind of weapon that keeps opposing penalty kills up at night. Tyler Seguin is out for the season with a knee injury, which is a blow to Dallas's forward depth, but the Stars have more than enough talent to absorb that loss. Jamie Benn, Jason Robertson, Roope Hintz, and the rest of the Dallas attack form one of the deepest forward groups in the league, and they should feast against a Toronto defense that has been Swiss cheese for the better part of two months.

Chris Tanev being out with core surgery on IR further weakens a Toronto blue line that was already struggling to contain high-end offensive teams. Without Tanev's stabilizing presence, the Leafs have been exposed defensively, and the results speak for themselves. Toronto's collapse isn't just about a few bad bounces or some unlucky breaks. This is a systematic failure across all three zones that has left the coaching staff grasping for answers and the fan base questioning the fundamental direction of the franchise. The -170 moneyline for Dallas is steep, but it's entirely warranted when you're sending the league's second-best road power play into a building where the home team has won twice in their last 14 tries.

The under is an interesting angle here despite the 6.5 total. Toronto's last six home games against Dallas have seen the under cash in five of them, and while the Stars' offense is certainly capable of putting up a crooked number, their defensive structure under Pete DeBoer has been championship-caliber all year. Dallas doesn't need to win 6-4 to beat a team like Toronto. The Stars can win 3-1 or 4-2 and be perfectly comfortable doing it. The Leafs' offense has dried up during their losing streak, averaging just 2.2 goals per game over their last five, and against a Dallas defense that gives up very little, Toronto might struggle to find the net even at home. This feels like a game where Dallas controls the pace, scores on the power play, and cruises to a professional road victory while Toronto's fanbase counts down the days until the offseason.

MIN -125 / STL +105
Total

Minnesota at 45-23-12 with 102 points has clinched a playoff spot and can approach this game with the confidence of a team that knows it's playing in the postseason. The Wild have been one of the more complete teams in the Western Conference all season, built on the kind of defensive responsibility and goaltending depth that translates well to playoff hockey. That said, Minnesota is dealing with some health concerns heading into the final stretch, with Joel Eriksson Ek, Mats Zuccarello, Jared Spurgeon, and Zach Bogosian all listed as day-to-day. Four key contributors dealing with nagging issues isn't ideal timing, and the Wild's coaching staff has to weigh the value of getting those guys healthy for the playoffs against the desire to maintain momentum heading into the postseason.

St. Louis at 34-33-12 with 80 points has been eliminated, and the Blues' season has been defined by inconsistency and the kind of maddening hot-and-cold stretches that prevent a team from ever getting real traction in the standings. The Blues have talent. Robert Thomas, Jordan Kyrou, and the rest of the St. Louis attack can compete with anyone on any given night. But the inability to string wins together has been the story of their year, and now the Blues are relegated to playing spoiler in the final week. The +105 moneyline makes St. Louis a modest home underdog, which is a reasonable price for a team playing in front of their home fans with nothing to lose and some individual pride on the line.

Here's a fascinating scheduling trend that jumps off the page: St. Louis is 0-7 on Mondays this season, while Minnesota is 6-2. That's a dramatic split that probably doesn't have any real causal explanation, teams don't play differently based on the day of the week in any meaningful way, but it's the kind of trend that sticks out when you're handicapping a game. The Blues simply haven't been able to get it done on Mondays all year, and while there's no scientific reason to believe the 8th time will be different, it does paint a picture of a team that might have scheduling or recovery patterns that work against them on this particular day.

Minnesota's four day-to-day players create the primary uncertainty in this game. If the Wild roll out their full lineup, the -125 moneyline is probably fair to slightly generous given the quality gap between these rosters. But if Minnesota decides to sit some of those banged-up players, and that would be an entirely understandable decision with the playoffs just days away, the complexion of this game changes significantly. The 6.5 total suggests offense from both sides, and the Central Division rivalry element always adds a layer of intensity even when the standings implications are lopsided. St. Louis will play hard at home in front of their fans, and Minnesota needs to decide whether this game is about winning or about getting healthy. That decision will likely determine the outcome.

This is the most consequential game on the entire Monday slate from a playoff-race perspective, and it's not even close. Nashville at 38-32-10 with 86 points sits exactly one point behind the LA Kings for the final Wild Card 2 spot in the Western Conference, and the Predators have just two games left on the schedule to make up that ground. This is win-or-go-home territory for Nashville, and Bridgestone Arena should be absolutely rocking for what could be the most important regular season game in recent franchise history. The Predators need these two points, full stop. There is no margin for error, no room for a bad period, and no acceptable excuse for anything less than a total team effort.

San Jose at 37-34-8 with 82 points has been eliminated, and the Sharks arrive in Nashville with nothing on the line except individual pride and the professional obligation to compete. That motivation gap is enormous, and it's reflected in the -155 moneyline that makes Nashville a solid home favorite. The Sharks have been in rough form lately, going 1-4 in their last five games and 1-5 in their last six road contests. San Jose simply has not been competitive away from home down the stretch, and traveling to a hostile building where the home team is playing for its playoff life is about the worst possible scenario for a team that's already checked out mentally.

Nashville's home dominance over San Jose has been nothing short of remarkable. The Predators are 9-0 against the Sharks at Bridgestone Arena in recent years, a perfect record that speaks to a complete schematic advantage in this building. San Jose simply cannot figure out how to win in Nashville, and there's no reason to believe tonight will be any different. The Predators have gone 4-2 in their last six games, finding some late-season form at exactly the right time, and their depth scoring has picked up in a way that gives the coaching staff confidence heading into this must-win situation. Nashville knows exactly what's at stake, and against an eliminated opponent with a terrible road record, the Predators should be able to dictate the pace from the opening faceoff.

The wild card race adds layers of drama that make this game essential viewing even if you're not a fan of either team. Nashville winning here puts enormous pressure on the Kings, who play later tonight in Seattle. If the Predators take care of business and then the Kings slip up against the Kraken, the entire WC2 picture flips on its head heading into the final day of the regular season. The 6.5 total suggests the market expects both teams to find the net, and Nashville's desperation should translate into aggressive, high-pressure hockey that creates chances in both directions. But make no mistake, the Predators are the only team on this ice with a reason to play, and that should be the deciding factor in a game where Nashville simply cannot afford a letdown.

This is the biggest mismatch on the entire 10-game slate by a wide margin. Buffalo at 49-23-8 with 106 points has clinched the Atlantic Division's first seed, while Chicago at 28-38-14 with 70 points is one of the worst teams in the NHL. That's a 36-point gap in the standings, which is absolutely massive in a league where parity usually keeps teams within shouting distance of each other. The -218 moneyline for Buffalo is heavy, but it's entirely justified when you look at the quality chasm between these rosters. The Sabres have been one of the best stories in hockey this season, breaking through after years of rebuilding frustration, and their first Atlantic Division title in decades has been a well-earned reward for patient roster construction.

Chicago's season has been about development, draft positioning, and getting young players meaningful NHL ice time. Connor Bedard continues to be the centerpiece of the rebuild, and his nightly performance is about the only thing keeping Blackhawks fans engaged during what has been a long, painful season. The +180 moneyline gives Chicago some juice as a home underdog, and the United Center crowd will show up because that's what Chicago fans do, but the talent gap between these two teams is simply too large for the Blackhawks to overcome on a consistent basis. The Sabres are deeper at every position, better coached, and playing with the kind of system-wide confidence that comes from knowing you're one of the elite teams in the league.

The puck line at BUF -1.5 (+114) is actually the most compelling number on this card. Buffalo winning by two or more goals is essentially an even-money proposition, and against the league's 28th-ranked team, that feels like a number the Sabres should cover more often than not. Chicago's defensive structure has been porous all season, and their goaltending has been inconsistent at best. When a team with Buffalo's offensive talent and tempo faces a defense as permissive as Chicago's, multi-goal victories are the expected outcome rather than the exception. The Sabres' top line can create chances at will against this level of competition, and their power play should find opportunities against a Blackhawks penalty kill that ranks among the league's worst.

The only question worth asking about this game is whether Buffalo approaches it with playoff-level intensity or treats it as a glorified preseason tune-up. The Sabres have their seed locked up and the postseason starting in days, which creates a natural temptation to ease off the gas and prioritize health over results. If Buffalo's coaching staff decides to rest key players or limit minutes for their stars, Chicago's +180 price becomes more interesting. But even a B-level effort from the Sabres should be enough to handle the Blackhawks at the United Center. Buffalo has been dominant all season, and their organizational depth means the drop-off from the first line to the fourth line is smaller than almost any team in the league. This should be a comfortable Sabres victory by any reasonable projection.

Welcome to the marquee event of the evening on ESPN, and what a matchup this is on paper even with both teams dealing with significant absences. Colorado at 52-16-11 with 115 points has locked up the Presidents' Trophy as the best team in the entire NHL, and the Avalanche have been the standard-bearer for excellence all season long. Nathan MacKinnon, Mikko Rantanen, and company have been relentless, and Colorado's 6-game road winning streak entering this contest tells you everything about their ability to win in hostile environments. But with Cale Makar listed as day-to-day, there's a real possibility that the Avalanche rest their franchise defenseman with the playoffs just days away. Makar's absence would be significant, as he's the engine that drives Colorado's transition game and power play.

Edmonton at 40-30-10 with 90 points has clinched the Pacific Division's second seed, but the Oilers have their own massive absence to contend with. Leon Draisaitl is out on IR, which removes one of the top three or four players on the planet from the Edmonton lineup. The Oilers were already shut out 1-0 in their last game, and without Draisaitl feeding off Connor McDavid or creating offense on his own line, Edmonton's attack loses a dimension that's almost impossible to replace. The pick'em moneyline at COL -115 / EDM -105 tells you the market views this as essentially a toss-up, which makes sense when both teams are missing franchise-caliber players and have their playoff positions already secured.

The over on 6.5 deserves serious consideration here despite the absences on both sides. Colorado's offense doesn't revolve around a single player the way some teams' do. MacKinnon is the headliner, but Rantanen, Lehkonen, and the rest of the forward group can generate offense without Makar quarterbacking the blue line. Edmonton without Draisaitl still has Connor McDavid, and McDavid is the kind of player who can single-handedly create four or five high-danger scoring chances in a game regardless of who's skating beside him. Both teams have the talent to put up three or more goals even with their lineups diminished, and in a game where neither team is going to commit to grinding out a 2-1 victory, the potential for a wide-open, entertaining affair is very real.

The ESPN broadcast adds a layer of visibility to this game that makes it appointment television for any hockey fan. Colorado versus Edmonton is the kind of matchup that the league wants on national TV, featuring two of the biggest stars in the sport in MacKinnon and McDavid, two of the best offenses in the league, and the kind of playoff-preview atmosphere that makes the regular season feel like a postseason dress rehearsal. Whether both teams rest additional players or go all-out to build momentum, the sheer talent level on this ice should produce a game worth watching from start to finish. Rogers Place will be loud, Colorado will be confident after winning six straight on the road, and this pick'em line creates the kind of handicapping puzzle that makes hockey so fascinating to break down.

The Kings need these two points more than they've needed anything all season, and the pressure of that moment, combined with the ESPN cameras and the wild card race drama, makes this one of the most compelling games on the entire Monday slate.

The under at 5.5 combined with a Vegas win feels like the most likely outcome, and it would be a fitting way to cap a Monday slate that has delivered drama, playoff implications, and elite hockey from coast to coast.
