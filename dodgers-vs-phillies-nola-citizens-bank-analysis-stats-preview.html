Monday night these two clubs put seventeen runs on the scoreboard and Philadelphia won 10-7. Tuesday night they combined for three and Los Angeles won 2-1. Same ballpark, same rosters, forty-eight hours apart. That is baseball in the third week of July, and it is also a decent argument against reading too much into either result before tonight.

What does not swing game to game is the gap in class. The Dodgers are 64-38, the best record in the major leagues, and they have scored 526 runs while allowing 373. That plus-153 run differential is the largest of any team in either league, twenty runs clear of Milwaukee. Philadelphia is 56-46, second in the National League East and three games behind Atlanta, with a run differential of minus-11 that undersells the roster but does not lie about the season.

A 153-Run Gap And A Team Trying To Stay In A Race

The strangest thing about the Dodgers this year is where they have done their damage. Los Angeles is 33-19 on the road and 31-19 at home, and the offensive splits are wider than the records: .272 with a .359 on-base percentage and an .802 OPS away from Dodger Stadium against .245 and a .735 OPS at home. They have hit 68 of their 132 team home runs on the road. This is a club that travels better than it hosts, which is not how most teams are built and is very much how this one is.

Philadelphia is the mirror image and it is stark. The Phillies hit .253 with a .445 slugging percentage and a .767 OPS at Citizens Bank Park and collapse to .220 with a .285 on-base percentage and a .646 OPS on the road. That is a 121-point OPS swing, one of the largest home-road gaps in baseball, and 77 of their 133 team home runs have come in this building. The Phillies are 27-24 at home and 29-22 away, so the results have not followed the splits, but the bats absolutely have.

Both offenses are also fundamentally different animals. Los Angeles hits .260 as a team with a .341 on-base percentage and a .771 OPS, patient and deep. Philadelphia hits .236 with a .303 on-base percentage and a .705 OPS while striking out 904 times, and it survives on 133 team home runs and the top three spots in the order. When the Phillies do not homer, they generally do not score, which is exactly the profile that produces a 10-7 win on Monday and a 2-1 loss on Tuesday.

Rotation & Roster Notes

Los Angeles Dodgers
The Shape Of The Roster
Shohei Ohtani is the best hitter in this game and it is not close: .286 with a .393 on-base percentage, a .529 slugging percentage, a .922 OPS, 22 home runs and 60 RBI. Behind him Max Muncy carries an .865 OPS with 19 homers and Freddie Freeman is at .284 with an .840 OPS. Andy Pages leads the club with 68 RBI. The pitching staff has allowed 373 runs, fewer than any team in baseball, with a 3.53 ERA and a 1.14 WHIP, and the road ERA of 3.41 is better than the home mark of 3.66. Mookie Betts, at .234 and a .722 OPS with 13 home runs, is the one regular having a genuinely down year.
Philadelphia Phillies
The Shape Of The Roster
Kyle Schwarber has 33 home runs, a .363 on-base percentage and a .913 OPS, and he has been the offense in the stretches when nobody else has hit. Bryce Harper is at .258 with 21 homers and an .861 OPS, and Brandon Marsh has the best batting average on the club at .290 with an .804 OPS. The bottom of the order is the problem: Alec Bohm is hitting .222 with a .645 OPS, J.T. Realmuto .215 with a .643 OPS, and Trea Turner .246 with a .689 OPS despite 14 home runs. The staff has a 4.22 ERA and has allowed 454 runs, and at home that number climbs to 4.34 with 61 home runs surrendered in 464 innings.

The Pitching Match-Up: Lauer Against Nola

This is not a duel of aces. It is a duel of two starters whose seasons have been defined by the same failure mode, and the total of 9.5 reflects it honestly.

Aaron Nola has made 20 starts and thrown 103 innings with a 5.68 ERA and a 1.45 WHIP, and the number that explains all of it is 23 home runs allowed, more than any starter working tonight anywhere in baseball. The stuff has not gone away. He has struck out 108, a rate of 9.44 per nine, and his knuckle curve is still his signature and his most-used pitch at 34.3 percent and 78.2 mph, backed by a four-seamer at 23.4 percent and 92.2, a sinker at 19.8 percent and 91.6 and a changeup at 14.0 percent and 85.5. What has changed is what happens when the four-seamer catches the plate at 92. Opponents are hitting .278 against him.

Facing a Dodgers lineup that hits .265 with a .782 OPS and 96 team home runs against right-handed pitching, in the ballpark where Philadelphia pitching has already given up 61 homers this season, that is the central risk of the evening for the home side.

Eric Lauer arrives with the same disease in a smaller sample. The left-hander is 4-5 with a 4.81 ERA and 18 home runs allowed in 76.2 innings, a rate of 2.11 per nine that is worse than Nola's on a per-inning basis. He does not miss bats, 47 strikeouts and 5.52 per nine, and he leans hard on a four-seamer thrown 44.5 percent of the time at 90.9 mph, with a cutter at 19.1 percent and 87.1 and a changeup at 16.4 percent and 84.2 behind it. Ninety-one on a four-seamer from a left-hander is survivable when it is located and punished when it is not.

Philadelphia has the platoon edge on paper and does not use it well. The Phillies hit .228 with a .683 OPS against left-handed pitching, their worst split, though they have taken 38 of their 133 team home runs off lefties. Schwarber and Harper both hit from the left side, which is the specific reason Lauer's night hinges on the top four hitters rather than the lineup as a whole.

Put those two columns side by side and the shape of a 9.5 total becomes obvious. The Dodgers are the better offense in absolute terms and a considerably better one on the road. The Phillies are the better offense in this specific building. Both starters give up home runs at rates well above league average, and neither bullpen is being asked to protect a small margin by a rotation that goes deep. The counterweight is Los Angeles pitching, which has allowed fewer runs than any staff in baseball and is better away from home than in it.

Keys To The Game

Dodgers Keys
Make Nola work the four-seamer. The knuckle curve at 78 mph is still a swing-and-miss pitch. The 92 mph four-seamer is the one that has left the yard 23 times. A patient lineup with a .341 on-base percentage should be able to force the fastball into the count.
Travel like they have all year. Los Angeles hits .272 with an .802 OPS on the road. Repeating that in a small ballpark is the simplest path to a comfortable evening.
Hand the bullpen a lead. Los Angeles has allowed 373 runs, fewer than any staff in baseball, and the relief corps is the reason the road ERA sits at 3.41. Give it a margin and this game closes quickly.
Phillies Keys
Let Schwarber and Harper decide it. Thirty-three home runs and 21 home runs, both from the left side against a left-handed starter who allows 2.11 homers per nine. Philadelphia's whole season has run through those two bats.
Keep Ohtani off base. A .393 on-base percentage in front of Muncy and Freeman is how a two-run inning becomes a five-run inning. Nola walks 3.06 per nine, and he cannot afford to donate one here.
Be the home team. The 121-point OPS gap between Citizens Bank Park and everywhere else is the single largest edge Philadelphia holds tonight. The bottom of the order has to contribute in the one place it actually has.

Market Context

Los Angeles opened and sits at -131, which implies a 56.7 percent win probability, with Philadelphia at +109. For the team with the best record and the largest run differential in baseball, laying less than a third of a unit of juice is a modest ask, and it reflects both the ballpark and the fact that Eric Lauer is the least imposing name on the Dodgers staff.

The total is 9.5, with the over priced at -118 and the under at -102. That pricing is a mild lean toward runs, which is consistent with two home run prone starters in a park that has already surrendered 61 homers to visiting bats this year. The one number pointing the other way is the Dodgers bullpen, attached to a staff that has allowed fewer runs than any other in the sport.

Final Thoughts

The honest read on this game is that it is closer than the standings suggest and less close than the ballpark suggests. Los Angeles is a genuinely great team playing its best baseball away from home, and it is starting the one member of its rotation who looks beatable. Philadelphia is a good team playing in the only environment where its lineup functions, and it is starting a pitcher whose ERA has climbed past 5.60 while his strikeout rate stayed near nine and a half per nine. Somewhere in that contradiction is the game. If Nola's curve is landing, the Phillies win a 4-3 game and take the series. If it is not, the Dodgers hit three home runs by the fifth inning and this ends early. Analysis only here, no picks. The betting card is on the moneylines page and the total of the day.
