Sweet 16 - Friday

College Basketball Previews Today

The Sweet 16 continues with four massive matchups Friday night, headlined by #1 overall seed Duke facing Rick Pitino's Cinderella St. John's on CBS, #1 Michigan's 33-win juggernaut hosting Alabama at the United Center, Tom Izzo vs Dan Hurley in a pick'em thriller between Michigan State and UConn, and Iowa State sweating Joshua Jefferson's ankle against a dangerous Tennessee squad that just eliminated Virginia.

Rick Pitino's Red Storm are dancing in the Sweet 16 for the first time since 1999, and the energy surrounding this program is electric. St. John's has been one of college basketball's best stories all season, riding Zuby Ejiofor's dominant interior presence (16.3 PPG, Big East Player of the Year) and a defense that suffocates opponents in the halfcourt. The Johnnies have won 14 of their last 15 games and genuinely believe they belong on this stage. Pitino has been to the Final Four before, and he's got this roster playing with a fearlessness that's dangerous in March.

Then there's Duke. Cameron Boozer has been the most dominant freshman in the country, averaging 22.4 points and 10.3 rebounds while emerging as the Naismith Award favorite. The Blue Devils are on a 13-game winning streak, their defense ranks first nationally in adjusted efficiency, and Jon Scheyer's squad has looked borderline unbeatable since mid-February. The one concern is Caleb Foster, who's listed as a game-time decision with a nagging foot injury. Foster's court vision and perimeter shooting are critical to Duke's spacing, and his absence would force Cooper Flagg into more ball-handling duties.

The 6.5-point spread reflects Duke's clear talent advantage, but it also acknowledges that St. John's isn't some Cinderella stumbling into the second weekend. This is a 30-win team with a legitimate star in Ejiofor and a coach who's seen every possible scenario in March. The Johnnies will try to grind this into a halfcourt war, limit possessions, and keep Boozer from getting easy looks in transition. If they can control the tempo and force Duke into contested jumpers, that 6.5 shrinks in a hurry.

The 141.5 total is one of the lowest on the board, and it makes sense. Both teams thrive defensively, and St. John's will do everything possible to slow the pace. Duke's offense can overwhelm anyone when it's clicking, but the Red Storm have the defensive personnel to make life uncomfortable. This feels like it'll be decided in the final five minutes, and the question is whether St. John's can hang close enough to make their experience and toughness matter down the stretch. Neutral-court atmosphere in DC should lean blue, but the Johnnies' fanbase will travel hard for this one.

Michigan has been a buzzsaw all season, and those 33-3 numbers don't even tell the full story. Elliot Cadeau has dished out 206 assists on the year, and the Wolverines dropped a combined 196 points across their first two tournament games. This is a team that plays fast, shoots the lights out, and makes opponents pay for every single mistake. Dusty May's system is humming at peak efficiency, and Michigan's depth means they can throw waves of fresh legs at you for 40 minutes without breaking a sweat.

Alabama is the wildcard in this bracket, and that cuts both ways. Labaron Philon has been outstanding, pouring in 21.6 points per game while connecting on 39.7% of his threes. The Crimson Tide have the individual talent to beat anyone on any given night. But Nate Oats' squad is dealing with significant off-court turmoil after Aden Holloway did not travel with the team following a felony arrest. Losing a rotation player in the Sweet 16, especially under these circumstances, disrupts chemistry and focus in ways that are impossible to quantify.

The 10-point spread is massive for a Sweet 16 game, and it reflects just how dominant Michigan has been combined with Alabama's instability. The Wolverines have dismantled good teams all season with their offensive firepower and defensive versatility. Cadeau orchestrates the attack with the poise of a senior, and Michigan's ability to score in bunches can bury teams before they even realize they're in trouble. Alabama's nine losses suggest a team that can beat itself on bad nights.

That 174.5 total is the highest on the entire Sweet 16 board by a mile, and it speaks to the offensive firepower both teams possess. Michigan wants to push tempo and create easy transition buckets, and Alabama, for all their flaws, won't back down from a shootout. Philon can single-handedly keep the Tide in a game if he gets hot from deep. But the Holloway absence thins an already inconsistent bench, and Michigan's relentless pace could expose Alabama's depth issues in the second half. The United Center crowd will be interesting, with Michigan essentially playing a home game in the Midwest.

This is the game of the night, and honestly, it might be the game of the entire tournament. Tom Izzo versus Dan Hurley. Michigan State versus UConn. Two programs with championship DNA going head-to-head in what's essentially a pick'em. Jeremy Fears has been the engine driving the Spartans all season, leading the nation with 9.4 assists per game, and he's been absolutely absurd in March Madness with 27 assists through two tournament games. The kid sees passing lanes that don't exist for anyone else on the floor.

UConn won back-to-back national championships in 2023 and 2024, and while this isn't the same roster, Hurley's system continues to produce elite defensive teams. Tarris Reed Jr. has been a monster on the glass, pulling down 13.6 rebounds per game during the postseason. Alex Karaban (13.3 PPG) provides the offensive consistency UConn needs on the perimeter. The Huskies lost five games this season, which feels almost jarring after two straight uncontested title runs, but make no mistake, this is still a team built for March.

The 136.5 total is the lowest of any Sweet 16 game, and that tells you everything about how this will be played. Both teams defend at an elite level and neither is going to gift the other easy possessions. Izzo's teams always grind in March, and UConn's defensive identity means every bucket will be earned. Fears' ability to create for others against UConn's switching defense will be the chess match within the chess match. If the Spartans can generate open looks through ball movement, they have the shooters to capitalize.

The 1.5-point spread and the fact that Michigan State is actually the moneyline favorite despite being the lower seed tells you how much respect the market has for Izzo in March. The Spartans have been to eight Final Fours under Izzo, and this roster has the perfect blend of experience and young talent. UConn's championship pedigree gives them a mental edge in tight games, but Fears is the best point guard either team has, and in a game this close, the best playmaker on the floor usually decides it. This one's going down to the wire.

Iowa State has been one of the most fascinating teams in the country all season, and they just proved something with that 19-point demolition of Kentucky in the Round of 32. The Cyclones did that without Joshua Jefferson, their inside anchor, who's listed as a game-time decision tonight with an ankle injury. Milan Momcilovic leads the nation in three-point percentage and has been unconscious from beyond the arc. When ISU's perimeter shooting is clicking, there's genuinely no defensive scheme that can contain them because they'll just bomb you into submission from the outside.

Tennessee's 24-11 record doesn't scream elite, but this is a Rick Barnes team that always peaks in March. The Vols are built on defense and toughness, led by Chaz Lanier (17.9 PPG) on the offensive end and Zakai Zeigler anchoring the backcourt. Zeigler won SEC Defensive Player of the Year twice for a reason, and his ability to disrupt opposing guards in the full court is unlike anything Iowa State has faced this tournament. Tennessee's 11 losses came largely during a brutal SEC slate, and this group has hardened significantly from that grind.

The Jefferson game-time decision looms large over this entire matchup. Iowa State beat Kentucky by 19 without him, which is remarkable, but Tennessee is a completely different animal defensively. The Cyclones need Jefferson's rebounding and rim protection against a Tennessee team that will pound the ball inside when their perimeter shots aren't falling. If Jefferson can't go, Momcilovic and the guards will need to carry the offensive load entirely, and that's a lot to ask against Zeigler's pressure defense.

The 139.5 total suggests a grind-it-out affair, and that plays directly into Tennessee's hands. The Vols want this game in the 60s, a halfcourt battle where every possession matters and defensive stops win quarters. Iowa State is more comfortable playing fast and letting their shooters fire in rhythm. The late tip in Chicago creates an intense atmosphere for a game that could come down to which team imposes its style. If Tennessee can force turnovers and keep the pace slow, they have the defensive chops to pull the upset. If Iowa State pushes tempo and hits threes, their talent advantage should carry them through.
