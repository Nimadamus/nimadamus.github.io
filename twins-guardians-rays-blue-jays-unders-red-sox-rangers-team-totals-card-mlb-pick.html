Twins-Guardians Under 7.5: Joe Ryan Against An Offense In Hiding

The quietest offense on the entire Monday board plays at Progressive Field, and we are betting directly on the silence. The Guardians are hitting .230 as a team with a .678 OPS, the lowest average and lowest OPS of any club involved in tonight's card, and their 392 runs across 100 games round to under four a night. Cleveland has stayed in the race at 52-48 almost entirely on run prevention, which tells you exactly what kind of game this organization plays at home: tight, low-scoring, bullpen-heavy baseball.

Into that environment walks Joe Ryan, who has been one of the best road pitchers in the American League: a 2.47 ERA and 1.00 WHIP away from Target Field, with opponents hitting .199. His overall season line, 2.85 with 128 strikeouts against 25 walks in 110.1 innings, is front-line stuff, and his fly-ball profile plays fine in a park that has never been a launching pad in July night air. A .230-hitting lineup against a road starter allowing a .199 average is the cleanest single matchup edge on this card.

Tanner Bibee's season record of 3-9 looks ugly, but the run prevention has been fine, a 3.90 ERA with a 1.15 WHIP, and the record is largely what happens when a decent starter pitches in front of an offense scoring 3.9 a game. The Twins hit .248 with respectable pop, so Bibee is the side of this bet that has to hold, and his home-run tendencies at Progressive Field are the honest soft spot. Still, the full-game math is simple: an elite road arm, a competent home arm and the worst lineup on the slate. One unit on the Under 7.5 at -110.

Rays-Blue Jays Under 7.5: The Best Duel On The Board

This is the flagship, and it is the rare under where both starters carry sub-2.70 ERAs into the same building. Nick Martinez is 8-2 with a 2.65 ERA and just 19 walks in 105.1 innings, a strike-throwing veteran who lives on weak contact and early outs. Dylan Cease counters at 2.56 with 148 strikeouts in 98.1 innings and a .190 opponent average, and at Rogers Centre the swing-and-miss jumps another level, 14.29 strikeouts per nine at home with a .196 average against. Two pitchers this locked in, in the same game, is exactly what a 7.5 total is supposed to look like, and the books agree, which is why the price is only -115.

The lineups do their part too. The Blue Jays are hitting .242 with a .687 OPS and 397 runs in 99 games, almost exactly four a night, and now face a Martinez profile built to smother exactly this kind of middling contact offense. Tampa Bay hits .257, the better average of the two, but as a 98-homer team this is a singles-and-sequencing lineup, and sequencing three hits in an inning against Cease's strikeout stuff is a genuinely hard way to make a living. The Rays also strike out less than almost anyone, which cuts both ways: balls in play find holes, but rallies against elite stuff still require three separate events.

Add the schedule context. Both these teams are in the thick of the AL race, the 56-42 Rays chasing home-field position and the 46-53 Jays playing desperate baseball, and Monday openers of big series lean manageable and conservative: starters go deep, managers stay out of the way, and the game script tightens. Our NRFI on the other card is the first slice of this same read. The full-game version gets the bigger stake. Two units on the Under 7.5 at -115.

Red Sox Team Total Under 4.5 (-130): Boston's Quiet Bats At Fenway

Boston's offense has a reputation its 2026 numbers simply do not support. The Red Sox are hitting .244 with a .708 OPS as a 94-homer team, and their 413 runs across 98 games work out to about 4.2 a night, meaning tonight's team total of 4.5 sits above their season-long average output. For an under at -130, that baseline alone does most of the work: Boston needs an above-average offensive night just to push the number, and the arm they are facing has been much better away from home than his season line suggests.

Shane Baz on the road: a .242 opponent average, a 1.30 WHIP and a home-run rate of 0.88 per nine, all meaningfully better than his home marks. His overall 4.19 ERA and 4-9 record read mediocre, and the record genuinely is, but the road version of Baz keeps the ball in the park, and keeping the ball in the park is the entire game at Fenway, where the Green Monster turns fly-ball mistakes into crooked innings. If Baz executes his road profile, the path to five Boston runs runs through sustained singles rallies, and a .244 team average is not built for those.

The quiet accelerator here is Boston's own starter. Payton Tolle has a 3.11 ERA with 87 strikeouts in 84 innings, and at Fenway he misses bats at better than eleven per nine. A game where Tolle is dealing stays close, and close games keep Boston playing station-to-station without the garbage-time at-bats that inflate team totals in blowouts. A unit and a half on the Red Sox team total Under 4.5 at -130.

Rangers Team Total Under 4.5 (-140): A deGrom Night Script

Same idea, different building. The Rangers have scored 412 runs in 99 games, about 4.2 a night, while hitting .245 as a team, and their team total tonight is 4.5 in a game the market has hung with a 7.5 full-game number. Read those two lines together and the market is already telling you it expects a low-scoring game at Globe Life Field; we are simply isolating the half of it we trust most, because a Jacob deGrom home start is the strongest game-script anchor in Texas baseball. At home deGrom has a 2.51 ERA and an absurd 0.86 WHIP with a .178 opponent average, and when he is carving, the entire game slows down with him.

Why does the opposing pitcher matter for the Rangers' own total? Because game state drives scoring. A deGrom duel keeps Texas's dugout conservative, keeps the White Sox bullpen in normal leverage patterns, and removes the blowout innings where a team total sails past 4.5 on mop-up relief. Erick Fedde's road numbers are legitimately concerning, a 5.48 ERA and 1.45 WHIP away from Chicago, and that is the main threat to this play, so it gets the honest treatment below. But Fedde has also pitched to a 2.91 ERA in his home park this season, which says the arm still works when the command shows up, and the White Sox at 52-46 are playing sound baseball in front of him.

The bet, at bottom, is that a 4.2-runs-a-game offense in a projected pitcher's-duel environment stays at or below its average. Texas has scored the fewest runs of any team on this card except Cleveland and Toronto, the ballpark with the roof closed plays neutral at best, and the total already leans our way. A unit and a half on the Rangers team total Under 4.5 at -140.

What Can Beat It

Every under has the same enemy: one crooked inning. In Cleveland, the danger is Bibee's home-run tendency, he has allowed more than two homers per nine at Progressive Field this season, and the Twins' 123-homer lineup means two swings can put five on the board before Joe Ryan ever factors in. In Toronto, the risk is the first inning of Dylan Cease, covered in detail on the companion card, and the general truth that strikeout pitchers with 1.13 WHIPs still have blowup nights; the Rays' contact-heavy approach means balls in play, and balls in play sometimes cluster.

The Red Sox team total dies if Baz's Fenway debut against this lineup goes the way his overall 4.19 ERA suggests it can, and Boston's short porch adjacency turns his 42 walks in 111.2 innings into big innings fast. The Rangers total has the loudest single warning: Fedde's 5.48 road ERA. If road Fedde shows up instead of home Fedde, Texas can clear 4.5 by the fifth inning. Each of these is priced into stakes of 1.5 units and under. No hero exposure anywhere on this card.

The Bottom Line

Four plays, 6 units, all pointed at the same target: runs that never show up. The Twins-Guardians Under 7.5 at -110 is a unit on Joe Ryan's road dominance and the worst lineup on the slate. The Rays-Blue Jays Under 7.5 at -115 is the two-unit flagship, Martinez against Cease in the best pitching duel of the night. The Red Sox team total Under 4.5 at -130 fades a .244 lineup priced above its own season average, and the Rangers team total Under 4.5 at -140 banks on a deGrom home start dragging the whole game into the mud with it. Pitchers on form, lineups underwater, totals above season averages. That is the whole recipe, and Monday serves it four times.
