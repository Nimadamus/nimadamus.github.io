11-Game Sunday Slate

NBA February 28, 2026

Sunday delivers the biggest slate of the week with 11 games spread across three national television windows. The red-hot Spurs bring an 11-game win streak to Madison Square Garden on ABC. Identical-record Timberwolves and Nuggets collide on ABC. The 76ers visit the Celtics on NBC in the primetime window. The league-leading Pistons take their 44-14 record to Orlando. Luka Doncic and the Lakers host the last-place Kings in the nightcap.

Spurs @ Knicks

Sunday, 1:00 PM ET | Madison Square Garden, New York, NY
Spread
SAS -1.5 (-110)
Moneyline
SAS -122 / NYK +102
Total
O/U 228.5

This is the marquee game of the entire Sunday slate, and it deserves every bit of that ABC afternoon window. San Antonio at 43-16 has been the most electrifying team in basketball over the past month, riding an 11-game win streak into the most famous arena on the planet. During that streak, the Spurs are averaging a staggering 126.1 points per game, and they've won their last 10 straight to sit in the 2-seed out West. Victor Wembanyama continues to redefine what's possible from a 7'4" human being, putting up 23.7 points, 11.2 rebounds, and 2.9 blocks per game while operating as both the centerpiece of the offense and the anchor of an increasingly suffocating defense. And then there's De'Aaron Fox, who has been an absolute revelation since being traded to the Spurs from Sacramento in February 2025, contributing 15.3 points and 9.0 assists per game as the high-speed engine that makes everything go in the half court and in transition.

Keyonte George has been rock solid at 16.6 points per game, and Stephon Castle continues to develop into a legitimate two-way threat. The depth on this Spurs roster is genuinely scary. They have size, speed, shooting, and defense at every level, and the 11-game win streak isn't some product of a soft schedule. San Antonio is beating good teams, bad teams, and everyone in between because they're playing a brand of basketball that's nearly impossible to replicate. The combination of Wembanyama's rim protection and Fox's transition speed creates a game plan that opponents simply can't prepare for in a one-game setting.

New York at 38-22 is a very good basketball team, and Madison Square Garden has been a fortress all season with the Knicks going 22-8 at home. Jalen Brunson at 26.7 points and 6.1 assists per game has been one of the best point guards in the league, and Karl-Anthony Towns continues to be a perfect fit in Tom Thibodeau's system, contributing 18.3 points and 9.6 rebounds while stretching the floor with his three-point shooting. These two teams already played once this season, with San Antonio taking the January 1 meeting 134-132 in a thrilling overtime affair. That game showcased everything that makes this matchup so compelling: Wembanyama's otherworldly shot-blocking against Brunson's elite mid-range game, Fox's speed against the Knicks' physical defense, and a pace of play that turned the game into a track meet.

The atmosphere inside the Garden for a 1:00 PM ABC tipoff is going to be absolutely electric. New York's crowd lives for these nationally televised showcases, and the Knicks have the talent and home-court advantage to make this a serious test for San Antonio's win streak. But the Spurs are playing with the kind of confidence and chemistry that makes them feel nearly unstoppable right now. Every lineup combination is clicking, every role player is contributing, and the Wembanyama-Fox duo has developed a two-man chemistry that's producing some of the most aesthetically beautiful basketball in the league. Something has to give, and whether it's the Spurs' streak or the Knicks' home dominance, Sunday afternoon at the Garden is going to provide an answer.

Cleveland at 37-24 rolls into Barclays Center with the fourth-highest scoring average in the NBA at 119.4 points per game, but the Cavaliers' health situation is the story heading into Sunday. Donovan Mitchell is listed as day-to-day with a groin issue, and James Harden is day-to-day with a thumb problem, meaning Cleveland's two primary shot creators could both sit. Max Strus is already confirmed out. If Mitchell and Harden both play, Cleveland should have more than enough firepower to handle the 16-42 Nets. If either or both sit, this game gets significantly more interesting and the line will adjust dramatically before tipoff.

Brooklyn's 7-game losing streak tells you everything you need to know about where the Nets are right now. At 16-42, they're firmly in the lottery conversation, and the losses have been piling up with depressing regularity. There's young talent on this roster that will eventually matter, but right now the Nets simply don't have the depth or the experience to compete with playoff-caliber teams on a nightly basis. The defense has been particularly porous during the losing streak, and without a consistent half-court creator, Brooklyn's offense tends to stall into isolation ball that produces low-efficiency shots.

The 4th-seed Cavaliers have the kind of offensive versatility that can punish bad defenses even with key players sitting. Darius Garland, Evan Mobley, and Jarrett Allen form a core that can generate good looks against almost anyone, and Cleveland's ball movement and three-point shooting have been elite all season. Even in a worst-case scenario where both Mitchell and Harden sit, the Cavaliers should have enough talent to handle a Nets team in freefall. The more interesting subplot is whether Cleveland's coaching staff chooses to rest its banged-up stars against a lesser opponent and save them for more meaningful games later in the week.

Brooklyn's best path to making this competitive is pushing pace and forcing turnovers. The Nets have athletes on the perimeter who can run the floor, and if they can create chaos in transition, they might be able to keep the game within striking distance for a while. But sustaining that effort against a Cavaliers team that excels in the half court is incredibly difficult, and Cleveland's defensive length around the rim with Mobley and Allen makes getting quality looks at the basket a nightly struggle for opponents. This one should tilt Cleveland's way regardless of the Mitchell and Harden decisions.

You could not script a better regular season matchup than this. Minnesota at 37-23 and Denver at 37-23 have identical records, separated by tiebreaker in the Western Conference standings with the Wolves sitting 5th and the Nuggets 4th. Both teams rank among the league's elite offenses, both have legitimate MVP candidates, and both desperately need this win for seeding purposes as the stretch run heats up. This is the kind of game that ABC lives for in its Sunday afternoon window, and the intensity level should feel more like a playoff game than a regular season matinee.

Denver gets a massive blow with Aaron Gordon ruled out due to a hamstring injury, and Peyton Watson is also sidelined. Gordon's absence removes one of the Nuggets' most important defensive pieces and a key connector in their half-court offense. Without Gordon, Denver loses versatility on the wing, rim-running ability, and a defender who can guard multiple positions. That puts even more pressure on Nikola Jokic to carry the offensive load, which he's more than capable of doing, but it also means the Nuggets' defense could struggle to contain Minnesota's array of athletic wing scorers.

The Timberwolves have the kind of length and athleticism on the perimeter that gives Denver fits even when they're healthy. Anthony Edwards is one of the most explosive scorers in basketball, and his ability to get to the rim and finish through contact is something the Nuggets have struggled to contain in recent meetings. Minnesota's defense, which has been elite all season with its switching scheme and rim protection, should have extra opportunities to create turnovers and transition buckets against a shorthanded Denver rotation that will be leaning heavily on Jokic to facilitate everything.

That said, nobody in basketball is more dangerous than Jokic when his team needs him to be everything. The triple-double machine has been operating at a historic level all season, and at Ball Arena, the altitude and the familiarity of the Nuggets' offensive system give Denver an edge that raw talent alone can't explain. Jamal Murray's availability will be a massive factor here. If Murray plays, Denver has a credible secondary creator who can take pressure off Jokic. If he doesn't, the Wolves can key on the big man with traps and hard doubles knowing there's no reliable pressure release valve. At 37-23 apiece, this game carries real weight for playoff positioning, and the loser could slide to the 6-seed by Monday morning.

Chicago's 11-game losing streak is one of the most impressive feats of sustained futility in the NBA this season, and it's about to be tested against a Milwaukee team that's been surprisingly resilient despite losing Giannis Antetokounmpo since January 23 with a calf injury. The Bucks at 26-32 are nowhere near where they expected to be, but their 7-3 ATS record over the last 10 games and a 3-game road win streak suggest they've found a way to compete even without their franchise cornerstone. Reports suggest Giannis is "close" to returning, but he's not expected to suit up Sunday, meaning Milwaukee will once again lean on its depth and defensive effort to grind out a result.

The Bulls at 24-36 have been a disaster over this losing streak, going 0-10 ATS in their last 10 games. That's the kind of number that screams a team has completely checked out. Chicago's defense has been hemorrhaging points, and the offensive execution has devolved into isolation ball without any real flow or purpose. Multiple Bulls players are listed as out, further thinning a roster that was already one of the thinnest in the Eastern Conference. There's no cavalry coming for this team, and Sunday's home game against Milwaukee feels like another date with defeat for a franchise that's been sleepwalking through the second half of the season.

Milwaukee's resilience without Giannis has been genuinely impressive and worth acknowledging. The Bucks have found ways to win games through defense, ball movement, and a collective effort that speaks well of the coaching staff and the character in that locker room. When Giannis went down, most people expected Milwaukee's season to collapse entirely, but instead they've gone out and competed hard on a nightly basis. The 3-game road win streak heading into the United Center is proof that this group hasn't quit, and facing a team in the middle of an 11-game losing streak should provide plenty of opportunities to extend that road run.

Everything points toward Milwaukee in this one. The Bucks have been covering at an elite rate, they're playing with more purpose than their record suggests, and the Bulls are in complete freefall with no mechanism to stop the bleeding. Chicago's home crowd at the United Center has grown increasingly restless during this losing streak, and the energy in the building is more resigned than raucous. Milwaukee should be able to control the tempo, get stops when it matters, and put enough points on the board to cruise past a team that's been one of the easiest outs in the league for the past three weeks. The only real question is how large the margin grows.

Injury Bowl

If you're looking for star power on Sunday, this is not the game for you. Memphis at 22-36 and Indiana at 15-44 are two of the most injury-ravaged rosters in the NBA, and the combined injury report reads like a hospital census. The Grizzlies are without Ja Morant, who's been sidelined with a UCL injury in his elbow, and Zach Edey remains out with an ankle stress reaction that's kept him off the floor for an extended stretch. The Pacers, meanwhile, lost Tyrese Haliburton for the season after he underwent Achilles surgery, and Ivica Zubac is also out. This is a game between two teams' B-lineups, and the quality of play will reflect that.

Indiana at 15-44 owns the worst record in the Eastern Conference, and without Haliburton, they've lost the player who made everything on offense function. The Pacers' season effectively ended when Haliburton went down, and now they're in full development mode, giving minutes to young players and seeing who can contribute long-term. That approach is healthy for the franchise but miserable for competitive basketball, and the Pacers have been one of the league's worst teams on both ends of the floor since losing their All-Star point guard. The home crowd at Gainbridge Fieldhouse has been understandably sparse during this stretch.

Memphis has plenty of talent when healthy, but health has been the one thing this franchise can't buy, beg, or borrow. Morant's absence robs the Grizzlies of their most dynamic player and the guy who turns a pedestrian offense into a highlight reel every time he touches the ball. Without him, Memphis relies heavily on Desmond Bane to carry the scoring load, and while Bane is more than capable of getting his points, he doesn't generate the same gravity and defensive attention that Morant commands. The supporting cast has shown flashes of competence throughout the season, but consistency has been an issue without the full starting five available.

The silver lining here is that games between undermanned teams occasionally produce entertaining basketball because role players feel liberated to take chances they'd never take with the starters healthy. Memphis has a slight edge in overall roster depth and competitive spirit, and the Grizzlies should enter this game as slight favorites once the lines are released. But predicting which collection of replacement-level players will have the better shooting night is essentially a coin flip, and this game could easily go either way. If nothing else, it's an opportunity for young players on both rosters to log meaningful minutes and show they belong in an NBA rotation.

Play-In Implications

This is a game with enormous play-in implications for both franchises. Portland at 29-31 sits 9th in the West, and Atlanta at 30-31 holds the 9th spot in the East, meaning both teams are fighting tooth and nail for positioning in the final weeks of the regular season. The early moneyline of ATL -178 / POR +133 makes Atlanta the home favorite, and that's reasonable given the Hawks' advantage of playing at State Farm Arena. But the margin between these two teams is razor thin, and one win or loss in either direction could dramatically alter their postseason trajectories.

Portland has been operating without Damian Lillard, who is out for the rest of the season with an Achilles injury. Losing Lillard was devastating for the Trail Blazers, as he was the engine that drove everything offensively and the veteran leader who kept the locker room together during difficult stretches. Shaedon Sharpe is also sidelined, further thinning a backcourt that was already stretched thin. Despite those losses, Portland has managed to stay competitive at 29-31, which speaks to the development of their young players and the coaching staff's ability to maximize the remaining talent. Anfernee Simons has stepped into a larger role and been solid, but he's not Lillard, and the drop-off in playmaking and shot creation is noticeable on a nightly basis.

Atlanta's situation isn't much better on the injury front. Jalen Johnson is listed as day-to-day, and the Hawks have dealt with their own share of roster instability throughout the season. Trae Young remains the centerpiece of everything Atlanta does offensively, and his ability to create shots for himself and others from deep range gives the Hawks a reliable offensive floor even when the supporting cast fluctuates. When Young is cooking, Atlanta can hang with anyone in the league. When he's having an off night, the Hawks don't have enough secondary creation to compensate.

The play-in race gives this game a playoff-level intensity that the regular season standings don't fully capture. Neither team can afford to drop games against similar competition, and both rosters know that every single win matters for their postseason positioning. Home court gives Atlanta a slight edge, and the early moneyline reflects that. But Portland has proven all season that they can compete on the road even without their best player, and this has the feel of a game that will come down to the final few minutes. Whoever wants it more in the fourth quarter will walk away with a crucial victory.

Detroit at 44-14 owns the best record in basketball, and what this franchise has accomplished this season is nothing short of extraordinary. From lottery punchline to league-leading powerhouse in the span of 18 months, the Pistons' ascent has been the single greatest story of the 2025-26 NBA season. Cade Cunningham has evolved into a legitimate MVP candidate, averaging 25.4 points, 9.8 assists, and 5.7 rebounds per game, and his recent 42-point, 13-assist explosion was a statement performance that reminded the league he's not just having a nice year. He's having a historic one. Detroit has gone 7-1 over their last 8 games and shows zero signs of slowing down.

But here's the number that should give every Pistons backer pause: Detroit is just 2-9 in their last 11 games against Orlando historically. That's a genuinely terrible record against this specific opponent, and while historical matchup data doesn't always predict future results, it does suggest that the Magic have found ways to neutralize what Detroit does well. Orlando at 31-27 is a quality team in its own right, with the kind of length and defensive versatility that can make life uncomfortable for perimeter-oriented offenses. The Magic's defensive scheme, which prioritizes contesting threes and protecting the paint, is exactly the kind of system that has given Cunningham trouble in the past.

Orlando is dealing with a significant injury blow: Franz Wagner is out indefinitely with an ankle sprain. Losing Wagner removes the Magic's most versatile offensive weapon and a player who was averaging over 20 points per game while serving as the primary initiator in Orlando's half-court attack. Without Wagner, the Magic's offense takes a noticeable step backward, and the burden of shot creation falls to Paolo Banchero and a supporting cast that has been inconsistent as creators. The DET -355 moneyline reflects both the massive talent gap between these rosters and Detroit's dominance all season, but Wagner's absence makes the line even more lopsided.

The Kia Center crowd will be into this one from the opening tip, because home games against the league's best team always bring extra energy. Orlando's defense, even without Wagner, has the personnel to make Detroit work for every basket, and if the Magic can slow the pace and turn this into a grind-it-out affair, they have a puncher's chance. But the reality is that the Pistons are simply a better team right now, and Cunningham has been dismantling defenses with a level of precision that few players in the league can match. Detroit's depth, their defensive intensity, and their franchise player's ability to take over games whenever he wants should be enough to overcome the historical matchup woes and push toward another victory in what's been a dream season.

The NBC primetime window delivers a rivalry game with genuine postseason implications. Boston at 39-20 sits 2nd in the Eastern Conference with a 19-9 home record and an 8-2 mark over their last 10 games, and the Celtics have turned TD Garden into one of the toughest places to play in the league all season. Jaylen Brown has been otherworldly, averaging 29.1 points, 7.1 rebounds, and 4.9 assists per game while shouldering the enormous burden of replacing Jayson Tatum's production after his devastating Achilles injury. Brown hasn't just maintained the ship, he's elevated his game to a level that has him firmly in the MVP conversation, and his ability to dominate on both ends of the floor has kept Boston firmly in the mix as a championship contender.

Philadelphia at 33-26 comes into TD Garden riding a 3-game win streak and playing some of their best basketball of the season. Joel Embiid is listed as day-to-day with right side soreness, which adds a layer of uncertainty to this matchup. When Embiid plays, the 76ers have a true superstar capable of dominating any game from the post, averaging 26.6 points and 7.5 rebounds per game. But his health has been a persistent concern all season, and the 76ers have learned to operate without him during sporadic absences. Tyrese Maxey has been absolutely scorching over his last 10 games, averaging 28.5 points per game during that stretch, and his speed, shooting, and fearlessness make him one of the toughest covers in the league for any backcourt.

The season series tilts Philadelphia's way at 2-1, which is noteworthy for a team entering a hostile road environment as underdogs. The 76ers have proven they have the formula to beat Boston, and that formula starts with physical defense, transition opportunities, and Maxey's ability to get into the paint and create havoc. When Philadelphia is clicking on all cylinders with Embiid and Maxey both healthy and engaged, they're one of the five or six best teams in basketball. The question is whether Embiid plays, and if he does, whether the soreness limits his movement and aggression around the basket.

Boston's BOS -2 spread from ESPN reflects just how tight this matchup is, and that's a respectful line for a home team with the Celtics' record and home-court advantage. The small spread acknowledges that Philadelphia is a legitimate threat, especially with the season series in their favor. Brown vs. Embiid, Maxey vs. the Boston defense, and the atmosphere of a Sunday night NBC broadcast at TD Garden all combine to create one of the most anticipated games on the entire slate. This should be a physical, intense, back-and-forth affair between two teams that genuinely don't like each other, and the winner will have serious bragging rights heading into the final month of the regular season.

Oklahoma City at 46-15 owns the best record in the Western Conference, and Shai Gilgeous-Alexander has been the most dominant offensive force in basketball this season. SGA is averaging 31.9 points per game on a staggering 55.4% field goal shooting, numbers that put him firmly at the top of the MVP race. His mid-range game is essentially unguardable, his ability to finish through contact at the rim is elite, and he's added a three-point shot that keeps defenses honest from 30 feet. The Thunder already lead the season series 2-0 against Dallas, and with the Mavericks decimated by injuries, this has the makings of another lopsided OKC win.

Jalen Williams is out with a hamstring strain, which is a meaningful loss for the Thunder. Williams has been the ideal Robin to SGA's Batman, providing secondary creation, defensive versatility, and clutch scoring in big moments all season. Without him, OKC's offense becomes more SGA-dependent, but the depth around the franchise cornerstone, including Chet Holmgren's rim protection and offensive touch, Alex Caruso's defense, and the development of several young rotation pieces, gives the Thunder enough to dominate lesser opponents even without their second-best player.

Dallas at 21-38 has been completely gutted. Kyrie Irving is out for the season. Dereck Lively II is out for the season. Cooper Flagg is sidelined with a foot injury. PJ Washington is out with an ankle sprain. The roster that was supposed to contend for a Western Conference title this season barely resembles an NBA rotation at this point, and the Mavericks have been in full tank mode for weeks. There's no competitive mechanism left on this team to push back against an opponent of OKC's caliber. Dallas is playing out the string, developing young pieces, and trying to secure the best possible lottery position.

The OKC -3 spread from ESPN feels remarkably low given the talent disparity, and it's likely a reflection of the Jalen Williams absence and the general market reluctance to lay big numbers with road teams. Even without Williams, the Thunder should dominate this game from start to finish. SGA can pick apart Dallas's depleted defense at will, and the Thunder's defensive intensity, even in a game with nothing to prove, tends to stay at a high level because that's the culture Mark Daigneault has built. Oklahoma City should cruise, and the only drama will be whether the starters sit the entire fourth quarter.

Doncic's combination of size, vision, shot creation, and basketball IQ has given the Lakers an offensive ceiling they haven't had since LeBron James was in his prime, and the results have spoken for themselves.

Sacramento at 14-47 owns the worst record in the Western Conference, and the Kings' season went off a cliff after trading De'Aaron Fox to San Antonio in February 2025. That's the Kings' three most important players all gone, and what's left is a collection of young developmental pieces and roster filler trying to get through the final weeks of the season without embarrassing themselves too badly.

Doncic against his old division rivals carries a hint of extra motivation, and the Lakers' star has been known to elevate his performance in nationally visible games and against teams he has history with. His playmaking ability should shred a Sacramento defense that ranks among the worst in the league, and the supporting cast around Doncic, including Anthony Davis's dominant two-way play, gives the Lakers multiple ways to attack and multiple players who can take over a game. Davis has been a monster all season, and the Doncic-Davis pick-and-roll has quickly become one of the most devastating actions in the NBA.

Sacramento doesn't have the personnel to keep this competitive for 48 minutes, and the Kings' defense has been so porous that even their best efforts tend to result in opponents scoring at will. The Lakers should jump on this game early, build a commanding lead by halftime, and coast to a comfortable victory in front of their home crowd at Crypto.com Arena. For Sacramento, the focus is entirely on the lottery, and nights like this are simply about survival and getting through the schedule in one piece.
