5-Game Friday Slate

NBA February 27, 2026

Friday night delivers five games highlighted by the league-leading Pistons hosting a severely depleted Cleveland squad missing Mitchell. The ESPN nightcap is the headliner: Shai Gilgeous-Alexander returns from a 9-game absence as the Thunder host Jokic and the Nuggets. The Knicks take on a gutted but scrappy Bucks team in Milwaukee, and two injury-ravaged squads with identical 21-36 records clash in Dallas.

Cavaliers @ Pistons

Friday, 7:00 PM ET | Little Caesars Arena, Detroit, MI
Spread
DET -6.5
Moneyline
DET -258 / CLE +210
Total
O/U 225.5

This is going to be ugly for Cleveland, and there's really no way to sugarcoat it. The Cavaliers are walking into Little Caesars Arena without Donovan Mitchell (groin), James Harden (fractured thumb), and Evan Mobley (maintenance), which means their three best players are sitting on a Friday night against the best team in basketball. That's not a recipe for competitive basketball. That's a recipe for getting blown out of the building by halftime. Cleveland at 37-23 has been a solid team all season, but rip out the three guys who generate roughly 70% of your offensive production and you're left with a roster that would struggle against lottery teams, let alone a 43-14 juggernaut.

Detroit is the story of the NBA this season, and it's not even close. At 43-14, the Pistons own the best record in the league, and Cade Cunningham has launched himself into the MVP conversation with averages of 25.3 points, 9.8 assists, and 5.8 rebounds per game. What makes Cunningham so dangerous isn't just the counting stats, it's the way he controls the tempo and makes everyone around him better. He's become one of those rare players who can dominate a game without needing to score 40. His passing vision, his ability to get to the rim and finish through contact, and his growing comfort as a pull-up shooter have turned him into the engine of the most efficient offense-defense combination in the Eastern Conference.

Detroit's defense is what separates them from every other contender in the league right now. They suffocate opponents with length, switching, and relentless effort on every single possession. There's no weak link to attack, no mismatch to exploit, and the Pistons' ability to turn defense into transition offense has been a nightmare for opposing coaches all season. Against a Cleveland team trotting out its B-lineup, that defensive pressure could turn this into a 25-point blowout before the fourth quarter even starts. The Pistons don't need extra motivation for a national television game, but getting to embarrass a conference rival on ESPN certainly won't hurt their effort level.

The only real intrigue here is whether Cleveland's depth pieces can make this remotely watchable. Darius Garland will have the ball in his hands more than usual, and he's capable of putting up big individual numbers. But one guy scoring 30 doesn't matter when the defense on the other end is leaking points at an alarming rate. Detroit should control this game from the opening tip, and the Pistons' home crowd, which has been electric all season at Little Caesars Arena, will make life even more miserable for a Cavaliers team that already knows it doesn't have the firepower to compete tonight.

Nets @ Celtics

Friday, 7:30 PM ET | TD Garden, Boston, MA
Spread
BOS -17.5
Moneyline
BOS -1450 / BKN +850
Total
O/U 207.5

There are mismatches, and then there's whatever this game is. Brooklyn at 15-42 visiting Boston at 38-20 produces a -900 moneyline for the Celtics, which is one of the most lopsided numbers you'll see in the NBA all season. The Nets are in full tank mode, and while Michael Porter Jr. has been a bright spot since being traded to Brooklyn, averaging 25.3 points per game, he's essentially a one-man show operating without the kind of supporting cast that can compete against legitimate contenders. MPJ can get his numbers on any given night, but the Nets simply don't have enough talent around him to make those individual performances translate into wins against teams of Boston's caliber.

Jaylen Brown has been nothing short of spectacular this season, and his 29.2 points per game have placed him firmly in the MVP conversation. The loss of Jayson Tatum to an Achilles injury was supposed to be a death blow for Boston's championship aspirations, but Brown has responded by elevating every facet of his game to a level most people didn't think he was capable of reaching. He's scoring with ruthless efficiency, defending the opponent's best perimeter player every single night, and leading a Celtics team that refuses to feel sorry for itself. The depth around Brown, with Kristaps Porzingis and Jrue Holiday, gives Boston one of the most versatile rosters in basketball even without Tatum. Derrick White is sitting this one out for rest/load management, but Boston's roster is so deep that his absence barely registers against a team of Brooklyn's caliber.

The Celtics at TD Garden have been dominant all season, and the home crowd feeds energy into a team that already plays with incredible intensity on the defensive end. Boston's switching defense, anchored by the versatility of Brown and Holiday on the perimeter plus Porzingis protecting the rim, creates a wall that the Nets' limited offense will struggle mightily to penetrate. Brooklyn doesn't have the ball handlers to stress Boston's defense, doesn't have the shooters to space them out, and doesn't have the rim protection to slow down Brown's drives to the basket.

The only question worth asking is whether Boston takes its foot off the gas in the second half and lets the Nets creep back into it. Joe Mazzulla's teams have occasionally been guilty of coasting in blowouts, and the Nets have young, athletic players who will compete hard regardless of the score. But even in a scenario where Boston plays casual basketball for 20 minutes, the talent gap is so enormous that the Celtics should cruise. This is one of those games where the outcome feels predetermined before tipoff, and the real entertainment comes from watching Brown do something spectacular on national television while the Nets try to develop their young guys in a losing effort.

Knicks @ Bucks

Friday, 8:00 PM ET | Fiserv Forum, Milwaukee, WI
Spread
NYK -7.5
Moneyline
NYK -305 / MIL +245
Total
O/U 222.5

Milwaukee's season has been defined by one devastating injury after another, and at this point the Bucks are essentially a completely different team than the one anyone expected to see competing for a championship. Giannis Antetokounmpo has been out since January 23 with a calf strain, and Damian Lillard is done for the season after tearing his Achilles and being waived. Let that sink in for a moment. The two players Milwaukee built its entire identity around are both gone, and the Bucks at 26-31 are somehow still winning games. They've gone 7-3 over their last ten, which is genuinely impressive given the circumstances, and it speaks to the character and effort of a roster that refuses to pack it in despite every reason to do so.

New York at 38-22 is rolling into Fiserv Forum with the kind of roster depth that makes them a nightmare matchup for any team, let alone one missing its two best players. Jalen Brunson continues to be one of the most underappreciated point guards in the league, averaging 26.7 points per game while running an offense that maximizes every piece around him. Karl-Anthony Towns has been a revelation since coming to New York, putting up 20.1 points and 11.8 rebounds per game while providing the kind of floor spacing from the center position that opens up driving lanes for Brunson and the rest of the perimeter players. When those two are clicking, the Knicks' half-court offense is one of the hardest things in basketball to guard.

Here's what makes this game interesting despite the talent disparity: Milwaukee's recent 7-3 stretch proves they haven't quit, and playing at Fiserv Forum gives them an emotional boost that can't be quantified on paper. The Bucks have been winning with defense, effort, and a next-man-up mentality that has surprised everyone around the league. Role players who were anonymous three months ago have stepped into expanded roles and performed admirably, turning what should've been a complete teardown into a competitive roster that still gives opponents fits. That kind of energy and urgency can cover a lot of talent gaps, especially at home.

But ultimately, talent wins out in the NBA, and the Knicks have a massive advantage at virtually every position on the floor. Brunson will find mismatches all night long against a Milwaukee backcourt that lacks the personnel to contain him, and Towns' ability to stretch the floor with his three-point shooting will pull Milwaukee's rim protection away from the basket. The Knicks also have the bench depth to wear Milwaukee down over 48 minutes, which is particularly punishing against a team relying so heavily on effort and energy to compensate for its missing stars. The Bucks will compete, because that's what they've done all month, but competing and winning are two different things against a team as deep and talented as New York.

Moneyline

Two teams sitting at identical 21-36 records, both gutted by injuries and trades, both watching the lottery odds with one eye while trying to develop young talent with the other. This is the textbook definition of a "who wants it less" game, and on paper it's one of the least compelling matchups of the night. Both Dallas and Memphis are dealing with devastating injury lists that have turned what were supposed to be competitive rosters into shells of themselves, and the -5.5 spread for the Mavericks reflects home-court advantage more than any meaningful talent gap between these two decimated squads.

The Mavericks' season went sideways in a hurry after Kyrie Irving went down with a season-ending injury, and the roster has been further thinned by the losses of Cooper Flagg (left midfoot sprain, his fifth consecutive game missed), Dereck Lively II (season-ending), and PJ Washington (left ankle sprain). That's four significant rotation pieces all sitting out, leaving Dallas scrambling to cobble together lineups from a depleted roster. Khris Middleton (right shoulder stinger) and Malik Monk (right ankle soreness) are both questionable, adding even more uncertainty to an already dire situation. Dallas is essentially asking role players to carry the offensive burden, and on most nights this season that hasn't been enough.

Memphis is in a similar boat, although their path here has been arguably more painful. Ja Morant is out with a UCL sprain in his elbow, and Zach Edey has missed 15-plus consecutive games with a left ankle/foot stress reaction. Throw in Brandon Clarke (calf), Santi Aldama (knee), and Kentavious Caldwell-Pope (finger) on the injury list, and the Grizzlies are running a roster that barely resembles what they put together entering the season. Desmond Bane has shouldered the offensive burden admirably, but there's only so much one wing scorer can do when the talent around him is this thin. The Grizzlies have been competitive in stretches this season, but consistency has been elusive without Morant running the show.

The DAL -5.5 spread and -225 moneyline suggest a moderate home-court advantage, and that feels about right for a game between two rosters missing their best players. Neither team has the personnel to dominate the other, and this game will likely be decided by which supporting cast has the better shooting night and which team turns the ball over less. The 237.5 total is notably the highest on tonight's board, reflecting the expectation that neither depleted defense can consistently get stops. For anyone watching this game, the real entertainment comes from seeing which young pieces on both rosters step up in a game that lacks star power but could still produce a competitive, back-and-forth affair.

Nuggets @ Thunder

Friday, 9:30 PM ET | Paycom Center, Oklahoma City, OK
Spread
OKC -8.5
Moneyline
OKC -325 / DEN +260
Total
O/U 231.5

The late window ESPN game just got a massive upgrade: Shai Gilgeous-Alexander has been cleared to return after missing nine games with an abdominal strain, and his comeback turns this into the most compelling game on the Friday slate. SGA coming back changes everything about this matchup, which is why OKC sits at -8.5 with a -325 moneyline despite still being without Jalen Williams (hamstring strain). Nikola Jokic is putting up one of the most absurd statistical seasons in NBA history, averaging 28.8 points, 12.5 rebounds, and 10.5 assists per game, a legitimate triple-double average over a full season. That kind of production from the center position is unprecedented, and it makes Denver dangerous against any opponent regardless of who else is available.

Denver at 37-22 has injury concerns of its own. Aaron Gordon (right hamstring strain) is out, and Jamal Murray's availability is listed as questionable with an illness, which is significant because Murray is the only other player on the roster who can reliably create his own shot against elite defenses. Without Murray, Denver's offense becomes even more Jokic-centric, which works most of the time because Jokic is that good, but it also makes them more predictable and easier for elite defensive teams to game-plan against. Peyton Watson (right hamstring strain) is also out, further thinning Denver's rotation. The Nuggets' role players need to knock down open shots and make the right reads when Jokic draws doubles, or this could be a long night in OKC.

Oklahoma City at 45-15 went 7-2 during SGA's absence, a staggering accomplishment that speaks to the organizational depth and coaching that Mark Daigneault has built. But getting their franchise player back is the biggest boost this team could ask for heading into the stretch run. SGA was averaging north of 30 points per game before the injury, and his ability to create from anywhere on the floor, combined with Chet Holmgren's rim protection (Holmgren is day-to-day with a back issue), gives the Thunder a completely different ceiling. Jalen Williams remains out with a hamstring strain, and Alex Caruso is day-to-day with an ankle issue, but the SGA return more than compensates for those absences. Isaiah Hartenstein (illness) is also day-to-day.

This game has a fascinating dynamic: the best individual player in the world (Jokic) visiting a team that just got its own franchise cornerstone back. When SGA and Jokic share the floor, it's a clash of two of the three or four best players alive, and the contrasting styles make it even more compelling. Denver's Jokic-centric half-court artistry against OKC's relentless defensive pressure and transition attack. The 231.5 total suggests the market expects both offenses to produce, and with SGA back generating looks for teammates, this has the potential to be a high-scoring chess match. If Holmgren plays, the Jokic-Holmgren matchup is appointment viewing, because Holmgren's 7'1" frame and shot-blocking ability is one of the few things in the league that can genuinely bother Jokic around the rim. This is exactly the kind of game that makes late-night ESPN basketball worth staying up for.
