Expert Analysis

Brewers ML -115 @ Red Sox

Posted: 4:47 PM ET, April 6, 2026 | MLB Regular Season

Share This Pick

X Facebook Reddit

This is one of those spots where you look at the pitching matchup, look at the records, look at the trajectories of both clubs, and wonder how the line is only -115. The Milwaukee Brewers are the best team in the National League at 7-2, they're riding a wave of confidence after taking a series in Kansas City, and they're handing the ball to Brandon Woodruff, who looked sharp in his season debut. On the other side? The Boston Red Sox sit at 2-7, the worst record in the American League, and they're sending out Brayan Bello, who got absolutely demolished in his last start. Sometimes the market gives you a gift. This feels like one of those nights.

The Pitching Mismatch Is Massive

Let's start with the obvious: this pitching matchup isn't close. Brandon Woodruff went out in his first start of 2026 against the Rays and delivered exactly what Milwaukee needed. Five innings, two solo home runs allowed, six strikeouts on just 67 pitches. That's a 0.80 WHIP. That's elite command from a guy who's been through the wars with injuries and came out the other side looking dangerous. The pitch efficiency is what stands out most. Sixty-seven pitches through five innings means he was attacking hitters, working ahead in counts, and letting his defense play behind him. For a pitcher getting his sea legs back in a new season, that's about as encouraging as it gets.

Now look at Brayan Bello. His last start against Houston was an outright disaster: 4.2 innings, 8 hits, 6 runs (5 earned), 3 walks, and just 2 strikeouts. That's a 9.64 ERA and a 2.36 WHIP. Those aren't numbers that suggest bad luck or sequencing issues. That's a pitcher who couldn't locate, couldn't put hitters away, and couldn't get through the fifth inning. Bello has the raw stuff to be effective, but when he can't command his sinker, everything falls apart. He was leaving pitches middle-middle against the Astros, and a Brewers lineup that's been swinging with supreme confidence all week is going to make him pay for those mistakes.

Here's what really separates these two: Woodruff's only career start against Boston was a masterpiece. He went 6.1 innings, allowed just 1 earned run on 4 hits, struck out 9, and walked away with the win. That's the kind of historical dominance that tells you he knows how to attack this lineup's weaknesses. Bello has no such track record of success against Milwaukee, and his current form suggests he's going to be in for a long night.

Boston's Freefall Is Real

The Red Sox aren't just losing games. They're finding creative new ways to lose them. Their most recent debacle tells you everything: they took a 4-0 lead against the Padres and somehow blew it, losing 8-6. That's the kind of collapse that seeps into a clubhouse and poisons confidence. When you can't hold a four-run lead at home, something is fundamentally broken, whether it's the bullpen, the defense, or the collective belief that you can actually finish games.

At 2-7, Boston has lost seven of their first nine games. This isn't a slow start. This is a tailspin. The pitching staff has been battered, the offense has been inconsistent, and the defense has let them down in critical moments. There's a reason the Red Sox are the worst team in the American League right now, and it's not just one thing. It's everything. The rotation outside of a handful of arms has been leaking runs at an alarming rate, and the bullpen hasn't been reliable enough to cover for short starts. When Bello can't get through five innings, this team is in serious trouble, because there's nobody behind him you trust in a tight game.

The morale factor matters in April more than people think. When you're 2-7 and you just blew a 4-0 lead at home, the energy in the dugout isn't exactly electric. Now you're facing a Brewers team that's been winning with authority, riding high at 7-2, and bringing a legitimate ace to the mound. That's a mental edge that shows up in small ways: tighter at-bats from Milwaukee's hitters, more aggressive pitching from Woodruff, and a general sense that the Brewers expect to win and the Red Sox are just hoping something goes right.

Milwaukee's Hot Start Has Substance

Look, plenty of teams get hot in the first week and a half of the season. What makes Milwaukee's 7-2 start different is the way they're winning. This isn't one guy carrying the offense or the pitching staff stealing games. This is a complete team firing on all cylinders. They just went into Kansas City and took the series, winning 8-5 on Sunday with the kind of balanced offensive attack that makes you a nightmare to pitch against.

The Brewers are chasing their fourth consecutive NL Central title, and the front office constructed this roster to contend from Opening Day. The lineup has depth at every position, the rotation has legitimate arms at the top, and the bullpen is stacked with high-leverage options. When you combine that organizational readiness with a 7-2 record and the confidence that comes with it, you get a team that knows how to close out games and doesn't panic when things get tight.

Milwaukee's offensive approach has been particularly impressive. They're not just swinging for the fences and hoping for the best. They're working counts, taking quality at-bats, and punishing mistakes. That's exactly the kind of disciplined lineup that eats a pitcher like Bello alive, because when he starts nibbling and falling behind in counts, the Brewers hitters are going to sit on his fastball and drive it. The patient, professional at-bats that have defined Milwaukee's early-season success are perfectly suited to exploit Bello's command issues.

Woodruff Owns This Type of Matchup

Brandon Woodruff has always been at his best when the matchup tilts in his favor, and this one couldn't tilt much further. He's facing a lineup that's been struggling to score consistently, managed by a club that's lost all confidence, and he's got the full support of a pitching staff that can cover the late innings if he gives them five or six strong frames. That 9-strikeout performance against Boston in his career? That wasn't an accident. Woodruff's mix of a mid-90s fastball, sharp curveball, and devastating changeup is built to exploit lineups that chase out of the zone, and a struggling Boston offense is going to be pressing at the plate.

The 67-pitch count from his last start is also significant here. Woodruff wasn't stretched deep into that game, which means he's had a full, normal rest and should be built up to go six, maybe seven innings tonight. If he can replicate the command and efficiency he showed against Tampa Bay, the Brewers are going to have a significant pitching advantage for the entire game, not just the first few innings. Compare that to Bello, who couldn't even get through 4.2 innings in his last outing, and the gap between these two starters becomes a canyon.

There's also the experience factor. Woodruff has been through playoff battles, high-leverage situations, and the kind of pressure that forges elite pitchers. Bello is talented but still finding his way, and when things went sideways against Houston, he had no answer. In a road start at Fenway where the home crowd is restless and frustrated with a 2-7 start, Woodruff is the kind of veteran presence who thrives in hostile environments. He doesn't get rattled. He gets sharper.

The Numbers Tell the Story

Let's put it all on the table. Woodruff: 1-0, 3.60 ERA, 0.80 WHIP, 6 strikeouts in 5 innings. Bello: 0-1, 9.64 ERA, 2.36 WHIP, 2 strikeouts in 4.2 innings. Milwaukee: 7-2, best record in the NL. Boston: 2-7, worst record in the AL. The Brewers just took a series in KC with authority. The Red Sox just blew a 4-0 lead and lost at home. The moneyline is sitting at Milwaukee -115, Boston +104. The run line has Milwaukee at -1.5 (+129) and the total is set at 8.

Here's the thing about that -115 price: it's practically a coin-flip price for a matchup that is anything but a coin flip. You're getting a pitcher with a 0.80 WHIP and 9 career strikeouts against this lineup at basically even money against a pitcher with a 9.64 ERA and a 2.36 WHIP. That's not a fair fight. That's a lopsided mismatch being priced like a competitive game, and those are exactly the spots you want to be aggressive with.

The over/under at 8 is interesting but secondary to the moneyline play here. This is about backing the right side in a clear pitching mismatch, and the right side is Milwaukee. When you combine the starting pitching edge, the record disparity, the confidence gap, and the historical data showing Woodruff owns Boston, there's really only one play here.

The Bottom Line

Everything points in one direction tonight. You've got the better pitcher on the mound in Woodruff, a Brewers lineup that's been clicking all season, a Red Sox squad in genuine freefall at 2-7, and a Boston starter who got torched in his last outing and is walking into a buzz saw. Milwaukee at -115 is one of the best values on Sunday's board. The pitching mismatch alone justifies the play, and when you stack the record disparity, the momentum, and Woodruff's career dominance against this lineup on top of it, this becomes an easy play. We're riding with the Brewers and Woodruff with full confidence.

The Pick

Brewers ML -115
