This is the biggest friendly the United States has played on home soil in years, and the setting could not be more fitting. Mercedes-Benz Stadium in Atlanta will be one of the 2026 World Cup host venues, which means this is genuinely a dress rehearsal on the exact stage where the tournament will unfold. Pochettino's squad arrives with a point to prove. The USA's recent form shows wins mixed with real results (W-W-D-W-L), and Folarin Balogun has been in red-hot club form, scoring seven goals in seven appearances. If Balogun fires in front of a raucous Atlanta crowd, the national confidence around this team goes to a completely different place.

Belgium arrive unbeaten in nine consecutive internationals. Kevin De Bruyne has been the engine of it all, contributing six goals in World Cup qualifying, which is a ridiculous return for a midfielder. His influence extends far beyond the stat line, and the USA's midfield will have to be disciplined and physical to prevent him from dictating the game's tempo. Belgium's unbeaten run includes victories against genuine European competition, which makes the +118 line feel like it carries real value.

The injury news shapes the USA's potential significantly. Tyler Adams is out, which removes the defensive midfield anchor. Sergino Dest is also unavailable, limiting options at right back. These absences matter because Belgium will absolutely target the areas Adams would normally patrol. De Bruyne probing into those spaces with his movement and vision is how Belgium will try to control the game. Whoever fills Adams' role faces an enormous test against one of the best creative midfielders in the world.

Both nations are competing at the 2026 World Cup, and every minute of preparation in tournament-adjacent venues carries added weight. Belgium's tactical discipline and De Bruyne's relentless form make them the slight favorite. But the USA at home, in a World Cup venue, with Balogun flying and the crowd behind them, is not a team to dismiss. The atmosphere in Atlanta will be tremendous, and the quality on the pitch should deliver something worth watching.

Scotland's World Cup qualification after a 28-year absence is one of international football's genuinely heartwarming stories of 2025-26. Hampden Park will be an emotional cauldron on Saturday, with a nation that spent decades watching tournaments from their couches finally getting to celebrate. Steve Clarke has built a team with real tactical structure and spirit, and this friendly against Japan is the first major test of where Scotland actually stand relative to the elite nations they'll face at the World Cup.

Japan are one of the most underrated sides in international football. The Samurai Blue have lost only two of their last 23 competitive internationals, which is a record that many European nations would envy. Under Hajime Moriyasu, Japan have developed pressing intensity and technical quality that is genuinely world-class. Players like Kubo, Mitoma, and Doan have all flourished in top European club football, and that experience elevates Japan's level in ways previous generations couldn't match. They're the rightful slight favorite at +114 despite playing away.

Scotland under Clarke tend to be organized and disciplined, sitting in a solid shape and relying on set pieces and individual quality from McTominay and Robertson to create their best chances. Japan's pressing game is the natural antagonist to that approach: they want to force errors in buildup, win the ball high up the pitch, and attack in transition. Whether Scotland can maintain composure under sustained Japanese pressure for 90 minutes is the central question.

The odds with Scotland and Japan both at +230 for the home win and draw respectively reflects genuine parity in outcome probability. The first competitive test after qualifying is always revealing. Scotland will learn a great deal about where they need to improve. Japan will use this as one more data point in their ongoing World Cup preparation. Hampden in full voice is a formidable environment. Don't sleep on the home side here.

Before we even get to the football: the Azteca is back. After a two-year, comprehensive renovation, the legendary Estadio Azteca reopens its doors on Saturday. This is the stadium where Pele received a standing ovation in 1970, where Maradona's Hand of God and Goal of the Century both happened in 1986. The renovation has brought modern infrastructure while preserving the soul of a place that matters more than almost any other sporting venue on the planet. Mexico is co-hosting the 2026 World Cup, and the Azteca will host the final. Getting it back up and running ahead of the tournament is significant in ways that transcend a March friendly.

Both squads are heavily depleted. Cristiano Ronaldo is out with a hamstring issue, removing the most famous player in the world from what would have been his probable farewell international tour. Portugal without Ronaldo still has Bruno Fernandes and quality depth, but the motivation and energy Ronaldo brings cannot be replicated. Mexico is similarly missing several key players from their club circuit, which means this is not a best-on-best showcase.

Portugal as the slight favorite at -118 reflects the quality advantage they hold even without Ronaldo. Bruno Fernandes running the attack is a combination that can hurt any defense, and Portugal's tactical discipline has been consistent. Mexico at home in the Azteca will have the crowd advantage amplified enormously by the emotional weight of the stadium's reopening, but they're facing a European squad that is systematically preparing for a major tournament.

This game matters most as context, not result. Mexico's coaches will evaluate combinations for the World Cup. Portugal will rotate through their squad and test different approaches without Ronaldo as the focal point. The Azteca's reopening is the real story here, and the atmosphere it generates will be something to witness even on television. One of football's great venues is back, on the right side of history, ready for the biggest tournament in the world.

Canada's World Cup preparations are rolling at full pace, and BMO Field provides one of the better home atmospheres in CONCACAF. The Canadians have been excellent recently, winning four of their last five and recording four consecutive clean sheets. That defensive solidity has been a genuine development from a team that used to rely almost exclusively on Alphonso Davies' brilliance in transition. Even without Davies, who is unavailable for this window, Canada have developed multiple attacking threats and a defensive structure that is difficult to break down.

Iceland present a challenging but manageable test. Their football model of physical commitment, set-piece threat, and hard defensive structure has always made them difficult opponents regardless of the quality gap. They don't have the star power Canada will face at the World Cup, but they provide something valuable: a tough, organized side that won't give cheap goals and will make Canada work for everything. Iceland's direct style and aerial threat at set pieces sharpen defensive discipline in ways that matter when June arrives.

The -203 home win price reflects the quality gap honestly. Canada are the better team, at home, in excellent form, and with more top-level club players. The question for Jesse Marsch is less about winning and more about building specific combinations. Without Davies, the left side needs to be organized around different personnel, and this friendly provides the opportunity to develop that. Jonathan David and Tajon Buchanan both bring elite-level club form into the international window.

Canada won't be caught cold in front of their own fans with the World Cup on the horizon. Four consecutive clean sheets gives real confidence at the back, and if David is sharp and Buchanan is dangerous in behind, this should be a comfortable home performance that sends the Toronto crowd home satisfied and World Cup anticipation building.
