Bulls @ Wizards

Spread
CHI -5.5
Moneyline
CHI -230 / WAS +190
Total
O/U 248.5

This is one of those late-season games that means absolutely nothing in the standings but could be wildly entertaining if you appreciate two teams just playing loose and letting it fly. The 248.5 total is one of the highest on the entire Tuesday board, and it tells you exactly what to expect: zero defense, a ton of transition buckets, and both teams pushing the pace like they're playing in a summer league showcase. Chicago and Washington are both firmly planted in the lottery, and the only thing left to play for at this point is individual pride, young player development, and the kind of audition minutes that determine who sticks around on next year's roster and who gets shipped out.

Chicago being a 5.5-point road favorite says more about how bad Washington has been than how good the Bulls are. The Bulls have had a rough season by any measure, but they've at least shown flashes of competence on offense, and their younger players have started to figure out their roles as the year has wound down. The backcourt has shown some promise in recent weeks, and the offense has been generating decent looks even if the defense remains a disaster. The trade that sent Zach LaVine to the Kings earlier in the year was always about clearing the deck and seeing what the kids could do, and that experiment has produced mixed results but genuine optimism about a few pieces moving forward.

Washington's season has been a masterclass in how to lose basketball games, and at this point the Wizards are practically sprinting toward the best possible draft position. The defense has been historically bad, surrendering points at a rate that would make a high school coach wince, and the offense hasn't been much better outside of a few individual performances. That said, the Wizards' young players have shown some fight at home, and Capital One Arena has actually produced a few surprising results against teams that came in expecting an easy night. The 248.5 total suggests this game is going to be a track meet, and both teams have the pace and the defensive deficiencies to deliver on that promise.

The 5.5-point spread feels about right for a game between two teams that are essentially equal in their mediocrity, with the slight edge going to Chicago because the Bulls have been marginally less terrible in recent weeks. This isn't a game that's going to change anyone's opinion about either franchise, but it could be genuinely fun to watch if you enjoy high-scoring affairs with very little defensive resistance. Expect a lot of points, a lot of transition layups, and a final score that makes both coaching staffs cringe when they watch the film tomorrow. The 248.5 total is the real story here, because both of these teams have the pace and the porousness to blow right past that number without breaking a sweat.

Timberwolves @ Pacers

Tuesday, 7:00 PM ET | Gainbridge Fieldhouse, Indianapolis, IN
Spread
MIN -12.5
Moneyline
MIN -800 / IND +550
Total
O/U 231.5

Minnesota being a 12.5-point road favorite is a number that screams blowout, and honestly, this Timberwolves team has earned every bit of that respect. Anthony Edwards has taken another massive leap this season, and the combination of his scoring explosiveness with the defensive infrastructure that Rudy Gobert anchors has turned Minnesota into one of the most complete teams in the league. The Wolves are a legitimate contender, and they're coming into the final stretch of the regular season looking to lock down the best possible seeding for what promises to be a deep playoff run. Edwards has been posting numbers that put him firmly in the MVP conversation, and his ability to take over games in the fourth quarter has been the defining characteristic of Minnesota's season.

Indiana's season has gone sideways in a way that nobody predicted, and the Pacers are now firmly in tank mode with nothing left to play for except draft lottery positioning. The loss of Tyrese Haliburton to an Achilles injury during the 2025 Finals was supposed to be a temporary setback, but the recovery has been longer and more complicated than anyone anticipated, and his absence has gutted the Pacers' identity. Without Haliburton running the offense, Indiana has looked lost and directionless, and the supporting cast that thrived alongside him has been unable to replicate that chemistry with anyone else at the controls. The -800 moneyline tells you everything you need to know about the market's confidence that this game is over before it starts.

The 12.5-point spread is massive for a road team, and it reflects the enormous talent gap between these two rosters right now. Minnesota has the kind of suffocating defense that can hold depleted teams to 95 points without breaking a sweat, and when the Wolves pair that with Edwards' ability to score 30 on any given night, the math gets ugly fast for opponents who don't have the firepower to keep pace. Indiana's offense without Haliburton has been one of the least efficient units in the league, and their defense has been equally bad, creating a double-edged sword of incompetence that turns most games into non-competitive affairs by halftime.

The 231.5 total is interesting because it's lower than you'd expect for a game involving two teams that can both play at a fast pace. The market is clearly factoring in Minnesota's elite defense, which should be able to slow this game down and turn it into a grind that favors the Wolves. Gobert's rim protection will make life miserable for Indiana's drivers, and the Wolves' perimeter defenders are long and active enough to disrupt passing lanes and force turnovers. This has all the makings of a game where Minnesota builds a 20-point lead by the third quarter and then coasts home with the starters on the bench for the entire fourth. The Pacers' young players will compete, because that's what young players do when they're trying to prove they belong, but competing and winning are two very different things against a team this good.

Milwaukee being only 2.5-point road favorites in Brooklyn tells you that the market has significant doubts about where the Bucks are right now, and honestly, those doubts are justified. This has been a season of frustration for Milwaukee, with injuries, inconsistency, and stretches of basketball that looked nothing like a championship-caliber team. Giannis Antetokounmpo remains an absolute force when healthy and engaged, but the supporting cast around him has been a revolving door of players trying to figure out their roles. The Bucks need wins down the stretch to solidify their playoff positioning, and a trip to Brooklyn should be exactly the kind of game where they take care of business, but "should be" and "will be" are two very different things with this version of Milwaukee.

Brooklyn is in the middle of what most people would charitably call a rebuilding year, but the Nets have actually been more competitive than their record suggests, especially at home. The Barclays Center crowd has been surprisingly engaged for a team that isn't going anywhere in the standings, and the Nets' young core has played with an energy and effort that has produced some genuinely impressive performances against better teams. The +110 moneyline tells you that the market thinks Brooklyn has a real shot at winning this game outright, and that's not crazy at all given how the Nets have played on their home floor recently.

The 220.5 total is the lowest number on tonight's board, and it suggests the market expects a grinding, defensive-oriented game that doesn't produce a ton of highlights. That makes sense when you consider Milwaukee's length and defensive potential, especially when Giannis is locked in on that end of the floor. The Bucks have the kind of versatile defenders who can switch everything and make life difficult for opposing ball-handlers, and when that defense is clicking, they can hold teams to the mid-90s without much difficulty. Brooklyn's offense has been inconsistent at best this season, and their ability to generate quality looks in the half-court against good defenses has been one of their biggest weaknesses.

The 2.5-point spread feels like it should be bigger given the talent gap between these rosters, but the market is clearly accounting for Milwaukee's inconsistency and the Nets' home court fight. Giannis is going to be the best player on the floor by a wide margin, and his ability to impose his will in the paint should be the difference in a close game. But the Bucks have a maddening tendency to play down to their competition, and games that should be comfortable victories have a way of turning into nail-biters when Milwaukee takes their foot off the gas. If the Bucks come out focused and aggressive, they should win this by 8 or more. If they sleepwalk through the first half, Brooklyn has the scrappiness to make this a fight to the finish.

This is about as close to a coin-flip as you'll find on tonight's board, and the moneyline tells the story better than anything else. Miami at -108 and Toronto at -112 is essentially the market saying it has no idea who wins this game, and the 1.5-point spread is about as thin as it gets for a road favorite. Miami's season has been defined by toughness and the kind of Heat Culture grit that Pat Riley has built over decades, and even without Jimmy Butler, who was traded to the Warriors earlier this season, Miami has found ways to compete and remain relevant in the Eastern Conference playoff picture. Bam Adebayo has shouldered an enormous load as the team's primary option, and his versatility on both ends of the floor has kept Miami in the hunt.

Toronto has Brandon Ingram as their centerpiece, and the Raptors have been an interesting team to watch since that move. Ingram gives Toronto a go-to scorer who can create his own shot and carry the offense during stretches when nothing else is working, and his ability to get to the mid-range and the free throw line has given the Raptors an offensive identity they were sorely lacking. The Scotiabank Arena crowd has been vocal all season, and Toronto has been better at home than their overall record suggests, with several impressive wins against playoff-caliber opponents in front of their fans.

The 238.5 total is relatively high for a game that features two teams with at least some defensive identity, and it suggests the market expects both offenses to be cooking tonight. Miami's pace has picked up in recent weeks, and the Heat have been generating more transition opportunities than they did earlier in the season, which has pushed their scoring averages up. Toronto has been even more uptempo, with Ingram's ability to push the ball in transition and the Raptors' athletic wings running the floor creating a style of play that naturally produces more possessions and more points. This game has the potential to be one of the most entertaining matchups of the night if both teams bring the energy and effort they're capable of.

The pick-em nature of this game makes it a fascinating watch from a pure basketball perspective. Neither team has a clear advantage, and it's going to come down to which group executes better in crunch time and which star, Adebayo or Ingram, has the bigger individual performance. Miami has the edge in experience and coaching, with Erik Spoelstra being one of the best in the business at preparing his team for close games. But Toronto has the home crowd and the hunger of a team that's still trying to prove it belongs in the playoff conversation. This is the kind of game that could go either way, and the margin between winning and losing might be a single play in the final minute.

Boston at home as a 5.5-point favorite is the kind of number that feels like it should be higher, and the relatively modest spread is likely a reflection of the Celtics' strange season rather than any real respect for Charlotte. The Celtics have been navigating a strange season that saw Jayson Tatum miss months with a ruptured Achilles before returning to action in early March. Tatum is back on the court but playing on a minutes restriction, and while having him in the lineup transforms Boston's ceiling, the Celtics are still finding the right rhythm with their star back in the fold. Jaylen Brown carried the load brilliantly during Tatum's absence and hasn't slowed down since his return, giving Boston a devastating one-two punch when both are healthy and engaged.

Charlotte has had the kind of season that makes you wonder what this franchise's plan actually is. The Hornets have young talent, but translating that talent into consistent wins has been an elusive goal, and the roster construction has left them stuck in a no-man's land between competing and rebuilding. LaMelo Ball's health has been a constant concern, and when he's out, the Hornets' offense loses its primary creator and playmaker. Charlotte's defense has been one of the worst in the league for most of the season, and asking a bad defensive team to go into TD Garden and keep things close against a proud Celtics squad is a tall order no matter how you slice it.

The 219.5 total is the second-lowest on tonight's board, and it reflects Boston's defensive identity even in a down year. The Celtics have always been built on the defensive end first, and that hasn't changed despite the turbulence of this season. Boston's perimeter defense remains long and active, and their team defense communicates and rotates at a level that makes it genuinely difficult for opposing offenses to get clean looks. Charlotte's offensive struggles compound the problem, because a team that already has trouble scoring is going to have an even harder time against defenders this committed and this well-coached.

The national broadcast on NBC/Peacock gives this game a platform it might not entirely deserve from a competitiveness standpoint, but the Celtics are always a compelling watch at TD Garden, and the crowd will be engaged regardless of the opponent. Brown is the kind of player who rises to the occasion on big stages, and a nationally televised game is exactly the kind of environment where he goes off for 35 points on efficient shooting. Charlotte will need to shoot the lights out from three to have any chance of keeping this close, and the Hornets' three-point shooting has been wildly inconsistent all season. Boston's defense, Boston's crowd, and Boston's talent advantage should all converge to produce a comfortable Celtics victory, though the Hornets have just enough offensive firepower to keep the final margin under double digits if their shots are falling.

But here's the thing: even at their worst, the Pelicans have more talent on their roster than what Utah is bringing into the Smoothie King Center tonight. When Zion Williamson is on the floor and engaged, his combination of power and explosiveness at the rim still creates problems that defenses don't have easy answers for, and at home against one of the league's worst teams, New Orleans has every reason to play with energy.

The Jazz are deep in rebuild mode, and the roster has been constructed with an eye toward the future rather than the present. Both of these teams rank near the bottom of the league defensively, and when you put two bad defenses on the floor together, the scoring tends to pile up quickly. This is likely to be a sloppy, up-and-down affair where both teams get easy looks and the scoreboard moves fast. Not exactly appointment television, but there will be plenty of points.

The 11.5-point spread is large for a game between two lottery-bound teams, but the home court advantage and the talent gap between even a disappointing Pelicans roster and a deliberately tanking Jazz squad justifies the number. Golden State being a 15.5-point home favorite over a divisional rival is a staggering number, and it speaks volumes about where these two franchises stand right now. The Warriors at 36-42 are fighting for their lives in the play-in tournament picture, and every single game from here through the end of the regular season is a must-win situation. Stephen Curry remains one of the most electrifying players in basketball, and his ability to single-handedly transform a game with a barrage of three-pointers makes Golden State dangerous in any matchup. The Warriors brought in Jimmy Butler from the Heat last season, though Butler's torn ACL ended his season and left Golden State without the co-star they envisioned alongside Curry. That injury has defined the Warriors' campaign, forcing them to scrap and claw for play-in positioning rather than contending for a top seed.

Sacramento has had an absolutely brutal season, and the Kings' record reflects a team that lost its identity when De'Aaron Fox was traded to the Spurs. Fox was the engine that made everything in Sacramento work, and without him, the Kings have been searching for a replacement-level point guard who can run the offense and create for others. That search has been largely unsuccessful, and the result has been an offense that stalls out in the half-court and a team that struggles to generate the kind of fast-break opportunities that defined their best basketball under Fox's leadership. The +675 moneyline tells you that the market views this as essentially a guaranteed Warriors win, and it's hard to argue with that assessment given the state of Sacramento's roster.

The -1050 moneyline is one of the most extreme numbers on tonight's board, and it reflects the enormous gap between a Warriors team that has championship pedigree and a desperate need to win and a Kings team that has essentially checked out of the season. Golden State's home record at Chase Center has been solid despite the team's struggles overall, and the crowd will be fully invested knowing that every game matters for play-in positioning. Curry in a meaningful game at home in April is a terrifying prospect for any opponent, and Sacramento simply doesn't have the talent or the depth to contain him for 48 minutes, especially without a defensive stopper at the point of attack.

The 15.5-point spread is enormous, and covering a number that large requires a true blowout, but the Warriors are absolutely capable of delivering one against this opponent. When Curry gets hot from three, Golden State's offense becomes an avalanche that buries teams before they have a chance to respond. Sacramento's defense has been porous all season, ranking near the bottom of the league in defensive efficiency, and their inability to protect the rim or contest shots on the perimeter is going to give Golden State open look after open look. The 234.5 total reflects the offensive firepower Golden State brings to the table and the expectation that Sacramento's defense won't provide much resistance. This has blowout written all over it, and the only real question is whether the Warriors win by 15 or 25.

Thunder @ Lakers

O/U 222.5

Oklahoma City at 62-16 is on pace for 65 wins, and that number alone should make you stop and appreciate what Shai Gilgeous-Alexander and this Thunder roster have accomplished this season. This isn't just the best team in the Western Conference. This is one of the best regular season teams the NBA has seen in years, a relentless, suffocating machine that grinds opponents into dust with defensive intensity and offensive precision that very few teams in league history have been able to sustain for an entire 82-game campaign. SGA has been the best player in basketball this season, a two-way force who scores with terrifying efficiency and defends with a ferocity that belies his smooth, almost effortless style of play. The Thunder are not just winning games. They are dominating them.

The Lakers at 50-28 would normally be a formidable opponent, but without Luka Doncic, Los Angeles is a fundamentally different team, and the -2100 moneyline reflects that reality in brutal terms. Luka suffered a Grade 2 left hamstring strain on April 2 and is currently in Spain receiving specialized treatment, which means he's done for the regular season and his playoff availability is a genuine question mark. When Luka is on the floor, the Lakers are a legitimate contender with the offensive firepower to hang with anyone in the league. Without him, they're a collection of good players without a true offensive orchestrator, and the dropoff from a healthy Luka Doncic to whoever is running the offense in his absence is one of the steepest in the NBA.

The 16.5-point spread is one of the largest on the entire Tuesday board, and it reflects a convergence of factors that all point in Oklahoma City's direction. The Thunder have the best defense in the league, and without Luka's ability to break down that defense with his size, skill, and basketball IQ, the Lakers are going to struggle to generate quality offense against OKC's switchy, athletic perimeter defenders and their rim protection. Oklahoma City also has the kind of depth that allows them to maintain intensity for all 48 minutes, with their second unit often being as good or better than opposing starters. The Lakers' supporting cast is talented, but asking role players to suddenly become the primary options against the league's best defense is a recipe for a long, ugly night at Crypto.com Arena.

Here's what makes this game fascinating despite the lopsided spread: the Lakers still have 50 wins and a proud group of veterans who aren't going to roll over just because the best player isn't in the lineup. There's a real chance Los Angeles comes out with extra energy and fight, determined to prove they're not a one-man team, and that kind of desperation can keep games closer than the talent suggests, at least for a half. But the Thunder are the ultimate closer team, with SGA's fourth-quarter dominance being one of the most statistically impressive stretches of late-game basketball in recent memory. Even if the Lakers hang tough early, OKC has the personnel and the poise to slowly pull away in the second half and turn a competitive game into a comfortable double-digit victory. The 222.5 total is low because both defenses are elite, and this game could easily turn into a grinding, physical affair where possessions are precious and points are hard to come by.

Flagg's rookie season has been a rollercoaster of incredible highs and the kind of growing pains that every young player goes through, but the overall trajectory has been overwhelmingly positive, and Mavericks fans have every reason to be excited about the future of their franchise. The problem is that the future isn't the present, and right now, Dallas is a young team that doesn't have the depth or the experience to compete consistently against established opponents.

The Clippers at home in the Intuit Dome have been a different animal this season, and the new arena has clearly energized both the team and the fanbase in ways that have translated directly to wins. LA's roster is deep and versatile, with multiple players who can create their own shot and defend at a high level. The Clippers have been one of the more balanced teams in the Western Conference, capable of beating you with offense, defense, or both, and their home record has been outstanding. The -535 moneyline is steep, but it reflects the reality that this is a playoff-caliber team facing a rebuilding squad, and the Clippers are going to be motivated to take care of business at home against a team they should beat handily.

The 11.5-point spread is large, and it's a testament to how much the Clippers have improved their home court identity since moving into the Intuit Dome. The arena's atmosphere has been electric, and opposing teams have found it genuinely difficult to play there, with the crowd noise and the energy creating a hostile environment that rattles young players in particular. For Cooper Flagg and the Mavericks' other young players, a road game in a loud, hostile building against a team fighting for playoff seeding is exactly the kind of experience that builds character and reveals who's ready for the next level. But building character and winning basketball games are two different things, and Dallas doesn't have the roster to overcome an 11.5-point deficit on the road.

The 237.5 total is relatively high, and it reflects both the Clippers' offensive capabilities and the expectation that Dallas's defense isn't going to provide much resistance. The Mavericks have been one of the weaker defensive teams in the league this season, which is expected from a young roster that's still learning how to execute schemes and communicate on that end of the floor. LA's offense is going to find easy baskets against a defense that's still figuring things out, and the Clippers' ability to space the floor and attack mismatches should produce a steady stream of open looks throughout the game. Dallas will score their share of points because Flagg can get to his spots and the Mavericks aren't devoid of offensive talent, but the quality and consistency of LA's attack should create a comfortable separation that holds throughout the game.

KD returns to his former home arena tonight, and how he responds to the hostility of the crowd could define the outcome of this game. Devin Booker is the undisputed franchise cornerstone for the home team, and Book has responded with one of the best individual seasons of his career. Booker's numbers have kept his squad competitive in a loaded Western Conference, and the -108 moneyline at home tells you the market respects what this group has built. Booker going up against his former co-star's new team adds an emotional layer that makes this feel like a playoff game in April.

The 219.5 total is the lowest on tonight's entire board, and it reflects the expectation that this is going to be a physical, defensive-minded battle where both teams grind for every point. Houston's defense has been one of the biggest stories in the NBA this season, with the Rockets' young, athletic perimeter defenders making life miserable for opposing ball-handlers and their rim protection deterring drives at the basket. But if the Rockets' defense can make him work for every bucket and force the supporting cast to beat them, the visitors have the edge in a grind-it-out battle. This is must-watch basketball, and the NBC/Peacock broadcast is going to deliver one of the best games of the regular season's final week.
