Western Conference FinalGame 3Vegas leads 2-0

The marquee NHL game on the board tonight is Colorado at Vegas for Western Conference Final Game 3, and the price tells a strange story for a team trailing 2-0. The Golden Knights took both games in Denver, a 4-2 win in Game 1 and a 3-1 win in Game 2, and now bring the lead home to T-Mobile Arena, yet the Avalanche are still the road favorite at minus-144 on the moneyline with the puck line at minus-1.5 priced at plus-172 and the total at 6.5. Puck drop is 8:00 PM ET on ESPN. Colorado is 7-2 in these playoffs after sweeping the Kings and beating the Wild in five, while Vegas is 8-4 after getting through Utah and Anaheim. The market is leaning on Colorado's underlying play even as the scoreboard says the Avalanche are two losses from elimination, and the reason it can hold that line is that Colorado has driven the run of play in both losses while failing to get the saves or the timely finish.

The Makar Hole

The defining variable is Cale Makar, who is ruled out again for Game 3 with an undisclosed upper-body injury and remains officially day-to-day. He has now missed both games of this series, and the splits are stark: Colorado went 51-14-10 with Makar in the lineup during the regular season and 4-4-1 without him, and the Avalanche are 0-2 in this series without him. Makar is the team's primary transition driver and the quarterback of its power play, and NHL EDGE clocked him with the fastest max skating speed in the league this postseason at 23.92 miles per hour before the injury. Without him, Colorado is leaning on deeper defensive rotations and heavier minutes from secondary pairings, and through two games that has shown up as breakdowns in its own zone that Vegas has cashed in the third period.

The Hart Wall

Carter Hart has been the swing factor and the best reason Vegas is up 2-0. He stopped 36 shots in the Game 1 win and posted a .967 save percentage with 29 saves in Game 2, when Vegas trailed after one period and scored all three of its goals in the third to flip the game. NHL EDGE has Hart fifth in the league in high-danger save percentage at .877 this postseason. Colorado has countered with Scott Wedgewood, who started both games and carried a .914 save percentage through the earlier rounds, with Mackenzie Blackwood available as the change-of-pace option if Wedgewood falters the way he briefly did against Minnesota. Wedgewood's high-danger save percentage sits at .852, tenth in the field, a clear notch below Hart, and that gap is the cleanest single explanation for the series score.

The Possession Paradox And Special Teams

Here is why Colorado is still favored. At five-on-five the Avalanche own this series: their shot-attempt share is 56.0 percent this postseason against Vegas at 48.5 percent, they lead the NHL at roughly 33.7 shots per game, and they held the even-strength scoring edge 23-13 across their first nine games. They fired 26 and 30 shots in the two losses and still came up short because Hart kept the high-danger looks out. The swing area is special teams. Colorado's power play has clicked at 25.0 percent and scored in five of its last six playoff games, but it loses a layer without Makar feeding from the point. Vegas has killed at 86.8 percent under head coach John Tortorella and has even generated short-handed offense, with Mitch Marner among the league leaders in short-handed points. If Colorado cracks that Vegas kill, the possession edge finally turns into goals; if it does not, the Avalanche keep out-shooting Vegas and keep losing.

Marner, Eichel And The MacKinnon Counter

Mitch Marner has been the best player in the entire postseason, leading the NHL with around 18 playoff points and producing in every situation. Jack Eichel leads the playoffs with 16 assists and remains the engine of the Vegas attack, and the two wins were built on that duo plus opportunistic depth scoring from Ivan Barbashev, who had two goals in the Game 2 comeback, along with Pavel Dorofeyev and Brett Howden. Colorado's answer is Nathan MacKinnon, who entered the conference final with 13 playoff points and leads the NHL with 10 skating bursts at 22-plus miles per hour and 65 bursts at 20-plus. MacKinnon is the one player who can tilt a game inside the first ten minutes by himself, and with Makar out he is carrying an even larger share of Colorado's offensive burden. Three checkpoints tonight: Hart's first period, the special-teams exchange between Colorado's 25.0 percent power play and Vegas's 86.8 percent kill, and whether MacKinnon's line can finally convert Colorado's shot volume into goals before this series slips to 3-0.
