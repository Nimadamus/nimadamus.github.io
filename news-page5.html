49ers Hire Raheem Morris as Defensive Coordinator: Super Bowl Champion Reunites With Kyle Shanahan to Replace Robert Saleh

Posted: February 1, 2026, 3:10 PM ET

Raheem Morris joins the San Francisco 49ers as defensive coordinator after being fired by the Atlanta Falcons | Photo: USA Today Sports

Falcons HC (16-18)

→

49ers DC

THE HIRE: Raheem Morris, 49, becomes San Francisco's fifth defensive coordinator in five seasons after his two-year stint as Falcons head coach

THE CONNECTION: Morris and Kyle Shanahan share deep ties from Tampa Bay (2004-05), Washington (2012-13), and Atlanta (2015-16)

The San Francisco 49ers have found their man. After Robert Saleh departed to become the Tennessee Titans' head coach just 10 days ago, Kyle Shanahan has turned to a familiar face to lead his defense: Raheem Morris, the former Atlanta Falcons head coach who was fired last month after back-to-back 8-9 seasons. The hire was first reported by ESPN's Adam Schefter on Saturday afternoon, and it represents a reunion nearly two decades in the making. Morris, 49, becomes San Francisco's fifth defensive coordinator in five seasons, following DeMeco Ryans (who left to become Houston's head coach), Steve Wilks, Nick Sorensen, and Saleh himself. It's been a revolving door on that side of the ball, but Shanahan believes Morris has the experience, scheme flexibility, and personal chemistry to finally provide some stability.

Here's the thing about Morris: the man has been around the block. He's seen the highest highs and the lowest lows this league has to offer. He was a defensive quality control coach for the Tampa Bay Buccaneers when they won Super Bowl XXXVII in 2002, earning his first championship ring before he'd turned 27. He was the Rams' defensive coordinator when Los Angeles won Super Bowl LVI in 2021, earning his second ring. In between, he's been a head coach twice, an interim head coach once, a position coach, and seemingly everything else you can be in this league. That breadth of experience matters when you're trying to rebuild a defense that lost All-Pro defensive lineman Nick Bosa to a torn ACL and All-Pro linebacker Fred Warner to a fractured ankle last season.

The Shanahan-Morris Connection: A Friendship Forged Over Two Decades

Morris and Shanahan aren't just colleagues. They're friends who have been running in the same circles since they were both young assistants trying to make names for themselves. Their first intersection came in Tampa Bay, where both were on Jon Gruden's staff from 2004 to 2005. Shanahan was a 25-year-old offensive quality control coach; Morris was a 28-year-old assistant defensive backs coach. They were bottom-of-the-totem-pole guys, grinding film at all hours, learning from one of the most demanding coaches in football. That shared experience created a bond that has survived through multiple coaching stops, career setbacks, and Super Bowl runs.

Their paths crossed again in Washington from 2012 to 2013, when Shanahan's father Mike was the head coach. Kyle served as offensive coordinator while Morris coached the defensive backs. Then came Atlanta, where Morris was the assistant head coach and wide receivers coach under Dan Quinn from 2015 to 2016, the same years Shanahan was calling plays as offensive coordinator. That Falcons offense, with Matt Ryan at quarterback, was one of the most explosive units the league had ever seen. They reached Super Bowl LI against the New England Patriots and led 28-3 in the third quarter before the greatest collapse in championship game history. Shanahan and Morris both carry scars from that night. Now they'll try to heal them together in San Francisco.

Why Morris Over the In-House Candidate?

When Shanahan spoke at his end-of-season press conference after the 49ers' divisional round loss to Seattle, he indicated that defensive assistant Gus Bradley was the primary in-house candidate to replace Saleh. Bradley has been around even longer than Morris, with previous stints as the Seahawks' defensive coordinator during the Legion of Boom era and as the Jaguars' head coach. He interviewed for the job, and there was reportedly strong support within the building for promoting from within. But then Morris's situation changed. He had been considered a finalist for the Arizona Cardinals' head coaching vacancy, but that job went to Mike LaFleur, the Rams' offensive coordinator. Once Morris was no longer in contention for a head coaching gig, he jumped to the top of Shanahan's list.

The decision makes sense when you consider what the 49ers are asking their next DC to do. This isn't a situation where you're inheriting a fully healthy, championship-caliber defense. Nick Bosa is rehabbing from a torn ACL. George Kittle suffered a torn Achilles in the divisional round. Fred Warner's ankle injury ended his season prematurely. The roster is depleted, the salary cap is tight, and there are legitimate questions about whether San Francisco can reload quickly enough to remain competitive. Morris has experience building defenses from less-than-ideal starting points. When he took over as Rams DC in 2021, that defense had ranked 22nd the previous year. They finished 15th in his first season and won the Super Bowl. The following two seasons, they ranked 6th and 7th in scoring defense. The man can coach.

The Atlanta Experience: Learning From Failure

Let's address the elephant in the room: Morris just failed as a head coach. His Atlanta Falcons went 8-9 in 2024 and 8-9 again in 2025, missing the playoffs both seasons. He was fired after the season finale in January, becoming the latest in a long line of coaches who couldn't turn that franchise around. On the surface, that doesn't look great for a guy who's about to be entrusted with a crucial coordinator role. But context matters. The Falcons' roster was a mess when Morris took over. They had cap problems, quarterback questions with Kirk Cousins' massive contract, and a defense that couldn't stop anyone. Morris never had the pieces to compete, and his 16-18 record probably reflects the talent level more than his coaching ability.

There's also an argument that failing as a head coach can make you a better coordinator. You learn humility. You learn what it's like to have the weight of an entire organization on your shoulders. You learn that managing egos, dealing with media scrutiny, and handling the day-to-day grind of being the face of a franchise is fundamentally different from calling plays. Morris has said publicly that he misses the tactical side of football, the chess match of scheming against opposing offenses. As a head coach, you delegate that work. As a coordinator, it's your entire job. Morris is ready to get back to what he does best.

What Morris Brings Schematically

Morris cut his teeth in the Tampa 2 defense under Monte Kiffin, one of the architects of that scheme in the modern NFL. But he's evolved significantly since then. His Rams defenses were aggressive, pressure-oriented units that relied on Aaron Donald as a one-man wrecking crew but also featured creative blitz packages and varied coverages. He's not locked into one system. He adapts to his personnel. In San Francisco, that means figuring out how to maximize whatever version of Nick Bosa returns from his ACL injury, developing younger players like defensive tackle Javon Hargrave, and finding ways to generate pressure without the luxury of a fully healthy roster.

One thing that stood out in Morris's Los Angeles tenure was his ability to get the most out of his secondary. Jalen Ramsey, when healthy, was arguably the best cornerback in football during those years. But Morris also developed lesser-known players like safety Nick Scott and cornerback David Long Jr. into reliable contributors. The 49ers have some pieces in the secondary, including cornerback Charvarius Ward and safety Talanoa Hufanga (when healthy), but they'll need Morris to work his developmental magic on younger players as well. The 49ers' defensive coordinator job has become a bit of a head coaching launchpad in recent years, with both DeMeco Ryans and now Robert Saleh parlaying the role into top jobs. Morris has already been a head coach twice. This time, he's focused solely on building a defense that can get San Francisco back to the Super Bowl.

The Robert Saleh Factor: Replacing a Friend

It's worth noting that Morris isn't just replacing any coordinator. He's replacing Robert Saleh, who returned to San Francisco last year after his disastrous stint as New York Jets head coach. Saleh's single season back with the 49ers was a minor miracle. He took a defense that ranked 29th in scoring in 2024 and transformed it into the league's 13th-ranked unit in 2025, despite catastrophic injuries to Bosa and Warner. He built trust with players, commanded respect in the locker room, and proved that his time in New York hadn't diminished his coordinator skills. When the Titans came calling with a five-year head coaching contract, Saleh couldn't say no. Now he's in Nashville with quarterback Cam Ward, and Morris is left to fill very large shoes.

The comparison between Morris and Saleh will be inevitable, and it's not entirely fair. Saleh had the benefit of familiarity with Shanahan's system from his previous 49ers tenure (2017-2020). Morris is learning the organizational structure for the first time. Saleh had a full offseason to implement his scheme. Morris is taking over in early February, just weeks before free agency begins. The circumstances are different. But if there's one thing we've learned about Morris, it's that he's adaptable. He's been in enough buildings, worked under enough head coaches, and seen enough schemes to know how to adjust quickly. Shanahan trusts him. The players will too.

What This Means for the 49ers' Super Bowl Window

San Francisco's championship window isn't closed, but it's definitely narrower than it was two years ago. Brock Purdy is still under a rookie contract for one more season, which provides salary cap flexibility. But the injuries are piling up. The defense needs reinforcements. And the NFC West just got a lot more competitive with Seattle's Super Bowl run and the Rams' continued excellence. Morris understands the urgency. He's 49 years old. He's already had two head coaching jobs. This isn't a stepping stone for him; it's an opportunity to prove he can be part of something special. If the 49ers can get healthy and Morris can scheme up a competent defense, there's no reason they can't be right back in Super Bowl contention next season.

The hire also says something about Shanahan's leadership style. He values relationships. He values experience. He values people who have been through adversity and come out the other side. Morris checks all those boxes. They've known each other for 22 years. They've won together and lost together. They've shared late nights in film rooms and early mornings on practice fields. When Shanahan called Morris about this job, it wasn't a formal interview. It was a conversation between friends. Sometimes, that's enough. The 49ers are betting that the personal connection, combined with Morris's extensive resume, will translate into defensive success. Given the alternative, that's a bet worth making.
