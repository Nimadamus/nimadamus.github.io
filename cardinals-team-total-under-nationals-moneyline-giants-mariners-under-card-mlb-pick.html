Cardinals Team Total Under 4.5, Nationals Moneyline and Giants-Mariners Under 7.5: A Three-Play MLB Card

July 19, 2026|8 min read|BetLegend

On Saturday afternoon in Seattle, the Giants and Mariners played the exact game the ballpark always promises: a 4-3 Mariners win, seven runs total, decided late, nobody blown out. Twenty-four hours later the same two teams walk back into T-Mobile Park and hand the baseballs to two starters who are better than the pair that just combined for that seven-run afternoon. That is the whole card today, really. Sunday getaway games are where the market gets a little lazy about starting pitching, and all three of these plays are bets on the same idea from three different angles: when the gap between the two arms on the mound is this wide, or the environment this hostile to scoring, take the pitching side of the argument and do not overthink it. Total exposure is 6 units.

Cardinals Team Total Under 4.5: Eduardo Rodriguez At Home Is A Different Pitcher

Start with the number that carries this entire play: Eduardo Rodriguez has a 1.89 ERA at Chase Field this season. Not for a month, not over a hot stretch, but across 66.2 home innings in which opponents are hitting .197 against him with a 0.95 WHIP. His overall line is excellent on its own, an 8-3 record and a 2.29 ERA over 114 innings, but the home and road split tells you where the real weapon lives. On the road he is a very good pitcher at 2.85. At home he is close to unhittable, and Sunday afternoon he gets the ball in his own building with the roof closed and the desert heat locked outside.

The lineup he is facing is the right one to pick on. The Cardinals are a solid 51-46 club, but the offense is not the engine. St. Louis is hitting .242 as a team with a .707 OPS, and the run production, 437 runs across 97 games, works out to about four and a half a game, which means the team total of 4.5 sits almost exactly on their season average against average pitching. Eduardo Rodriguez at home is not average pitching. When a lineup's median outcome against a neutral arm is the number itself, and the arm on the mound has been holding home opponents to a batting average under .200 all season, the under side of that total is carrying real margin.

Andre Pallante matters here too, in a quieter way. The Cardinals starter is 10-6 with a 3.96 ERA, a workhorse who keeps his team in games, and that shapes how this one is likely to be managed. A close, low-scoring game keeps St. Louis playing station-to-station baseball rather than piling on against a broken bullpen in garbage time. The blowout path, the one where the Cardinals total sails over because the game gets out of hand in their favor, requires Rodriguez to have his worst home start of the year. A unit and a half on the Cardinals team total Under 4.5 at -138.

Nationals Moneyline: The Widest Pitching Gap On The Board

Sometimes handicapping is complicated. This is not one of those times. Foster Griffin is 10-2 with a 2.77 ERA, a 1.02 WHIP and 109 strikeouts against just 26 walks in 110.1 innings. Jacob Lopez, the arm the Athletics send out against him, carries a 6.83 ERA and a 1.79 WHIP, with opponents hitting .293 off him and nearly as many walks issued, 34, as strikeouts recorded, 43, across his 55.1 innings. That is a four-run gap in ERA and the widest starting pitching mismatch on the entire Sunday board, and it is priced at -143, a number that would look thin for a matchup half this lopsided.

What makes Griffin especially trustworthy in this spot is that the road does not touch him. His away split this season is a 2.79 ERA with a 0.93 WHIP and a .193 opponent average across 58 road innings, essentially identical to his home work and in some ways sharper. Sutter Health Park, the minor league yard in West Sacramento the Athletics call home, is a genuine hitting environment, and a road under would make me nervous here. A road moneyline behind a command artist who does not walk anyone is a different bet entirely. Walks are what turn a hitter-friendly park into a disaster, and Griffin simply does not issue them.

The lineups point the same direction. Washington has quietly scored 540 runs in 99 games with 142 home runs as a team, comfortably the most productive offense involved in any game on this card, and it gets to work against a pitcher who puts nearly two runners on per inning. The Athletics sit at 42-56 and have lost fourteen more games than they have won for a reason. You are laying a modest price for the best pitcher on the field, the better offense, and a bad team on the other side. Two units on the Nationals moneyline at -143.

Giants-Mariners Under 7.5: Ray, Gilbert And A Park That Swallows Runs

Back to Seattle, where Saturday's seven-run game would have already cashed this number, and where Sunday's pitching matchup is an upgrade on both sides. Logan Gilbert is having a genuinely elite season: a 3.32 ERA with a 0.99 WHIP and 119 strikeouts against only 24 walks in 114 innings. Opponents are hitting .213 off him. He is the kind of strike-throwing, bat-missing right-hander who makes innings disappear in twelve pitches, and he works in a park that has been suppressing offense for two decades. Robbie Ray, opposite him, brings a 3.38 ERA and a .217 opponent average of his own, and the veteran left-hander knows this mound as well as anyone in the building after his years pitching for the home side.

Then look at the two offenses being asked to clear eight runs. The Mariners are hitting .227 as a team with a .684 OPS, the lowest batting average of any club on this card, and their 396 runs across 99 games is barely four a game. The Giants are only marginally better at .256 with 405 runs in 98 games. Neither of these lineups strings together big innings with any regularity, and both now face a starter holding opponents well under .220. The total of 7.5 asks two bottom-third offenses to produce an above-average scoring game against two above-average arms in one of the toughest run environments in baseball. That is a lot of asks stacked on top of each other.

The shape of Saturday's game is the template. Seven runs felt like a lot in that one, and it took a late Mariners push to get there against lesser stuff than Gilbert will show today. With both starters capable of handing a lead directly to the back of the bullpen, the seven-inning version of this game is likely a 3-2 or 4-2 affair, leaving the total needing a messy ninth just to threaten the number. Two and a half units on the Under 7.5 at -120, the biggest play on the card.

What Can Beat It

Honest accounting, because every one of these has a real failure path. The Cardinals team total dies if Rodriguez's home dominance finally has its off day, and no split, however gaudy, is immune to one bad afternoon. St. Louis did put up eight runs in a game earlier this month, and a couple of early walks plus one mistake over the middle gets them to five before the desert crowd settles in.

The Nationals moneyline carries the loudest warning of all, and it happened yesterday: the Athletics beat this same Washington team 15-1 on Saturday in the very ballpark where today's game will be played. Baseball does not care about ERA gaps on any single afternoon, Sutter Health Park inflates offense, and a Nationals club that just got embarrassed has to show up and flush it. The bet is that Griffin, who has been the most consistent thing about this team all season, is precisely the arm you want handed the ball after a night like that. And the Seattle under dies the way Saturday nearly died it: a late bullpen inning that turns 4-2 into 6-3 with runners still on. Ray's road numbers, a 4.53 ERA away from San Francisco against 2.44 at home, are the soft spot in the matchup, and if he leaks early the Mariners only need one crooked inning to put the total in play. Priced in, all of it. The reads stand.

The Bottom Line

Three plays, one through-line: on a Sunday, back the mound. The Cardinals team total Under 4.5 at -138 is a unit and a half on Eduardo Rodriguez and a 1.89 home ERA against a .242 lineup whose average day is the number itself. The Nationals moneyline at -143 is two units on the widest starter gap on the board, Foster Griffin at 10-2 and 2.77 against Jacob Lopez at 6.83, with the better offense hitting behind him. And the Giants-Mariners Under 7.5 at -120 is the flagship at two and a half units, Logan Gilbert's 0.99 WHIP and Robbie Ray in a park that swallowed seven runs whole just yesterday. Six units, no hero swings required, and every leg leans on the one thing that travels best in July baseball: a starting pitcher who has been doing it all year.
