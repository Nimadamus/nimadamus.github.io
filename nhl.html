Hurricanes @ Maple Leafs

Friday, 7:00 PM ET | Scotiabank Arena, Toronto, ON
Puck Line
CAR -1.5
Moneyline
CAR -220 / TOR +180
Total
O/U 6.5

This is the marquee matchup on a Friday night, and it's a game that perfectly illustrates two franchises heading in completely opposite directions. The Carolina Hurricanes (43-19-6, 92 points) are one of the best teams in the Eastern Conference and a legitimate Stanley Cup contender, sitting comfortably in a top-three spot in the Metro Division. The Toronto Maple Leafs (29-28-12, 70 points), meanwhile, have been one of the most disappointing teams in the NHL this season. A franchise with this much payroll and this much pressure sitting at 29-28-12 in late March is a disaster, and the frustration inside that locker room and among the fanbase has to be palpable. The -220 moneyline on Carolina tells you everything about the talent gap between these two clubs right now.

Carolina's system under Rod Brind'Amour continues to produce elite results year after year. The Hurricanes play a relentless brand of hockey that wears opponents down with their forecheck, their transition speed, and their depth scoring. Sebastian Aho has been the offensive engine with elite production all season, and Seth Jarvis has taken another step forward as a legitimate top-line scorer. The defensive corps, anchored by Jaccob Slavin, remains one of the best units in the league. Carolina's penalty kill has been among the NHL's best, and their ability to suppress high-danger chances makes them incredibly difficult to beat. With 92 points through 68 games, they're on pace for a franchise-best regular season, and this is a team that knows how to win on the road.

Toronto's season has been a slow-motion unraveling. The 12 overtime losses are particularly painful because they represent points that slipped away in winnable games, and that's the kind of thing that eats at a team's psyche over the course of a long season. Auston Matthews has still been productive when healthy, but the supporting cast hasn't delivered consistently enough. William Nylander has had his moments, and John Tavares continues to provide veteran leadership, but the overall roster construction has too many holes for a team with playoff aspirations. The goaltending has been inconsistent, and the defensive structure that coach Craig Berube has tried to implement hasn't fully taken hold. At 70 points, Toronto is fighting for their playoff lives, but doing it against a team like Carolina feels like trying to climb a mountain in a rainstorm.

The 6.5 total is an interesting number for this one. Carolina's defensive structure could keep this game tighter than the moneyline suggests, but the Hurricanes also have the offensive firepower to light up a Toronto team that has struggled to keep pucks out of the net. If the Leafs come out desperate and push the pace early, this could open up into a high-event affair. But if Carolina grabs an early lead and settles into their structure, Toronto might not have the horses to fight back. The puck line at CAR -1.5 is worth a long look, because when the Hurricanes are rolling, they don't just beat inferior teams, they bury them. Toronto's home crowd could provide some energy, but energy alone doesn't solve the fundamental problems this roster has been dealing with all season long.

Devils @ Capitals

Friday, 7:00 PM ET | Capital One Arena, Washington, DC
Puck Line
WSH -1.5 (+185)
Moneyline
WSH -130 / NJ +110
Total
O/U 6.0

This is a game with legitimate postseason implications for both teams, and the intensity should reflect that. The Washington Capitals (34-27-8, 76 points) are sitting in a wild card spot and trying to hold on, while the New Jersey Devils (35-31-2, 72 points) are four points back and running out of runway to make a push. For New Jersey, every game from here on out is essentially an elimination game, and the pressure of needing points in a hostile building like Capital One Arena is the kind of test that separates playoff teams from pretenders. Washington being a slight -130 home favorite is the right number for this matchup, because the Capitals have been more consistent at home and they've got a crowd behind them that smells a postseason berth.

Washington's offense has been solid if unspectacular this season, but the storyline that transcends the standings is Alex Ovechkin's chase of Wayne Gretzky's all-time goal record. Every game Ovechkin plays is another chance for history, and the electricity in Capital One Arena when he's on the ice with a chance to score is unlike anything else in the sport. Beyond Ovechkin, the Capitals have gotten strong contributions from their supporting cast. Jakob Chychrun has been a revelation from the blue line, providing offense from the back end that Washington desperately needed. Tom Wilson has continued his evolution from enforcer to legitimate top-six forward, and his production has been critical. The Capitals' 76 points through 69 games puts them on pace for roughly 90 points, which should be enough for a playoff spot, but nothing is guaranteed in a tightly packed Eastern Conference.

New Jersey's season has been a rollercoaster of inconsistency. When the Devils are playing their best hockey, with Jack Hughes driving the offense and the speed game clicking, they look like a team that can beat anyone. But those stretches have been too few and far between, and the 35-31-2 record reflects a team that simply hasn't been able to string together enough wins to separate from the pack. Hughes has been electric when healthy, but injuries have limited his impact at various points. Jesper Bratt has been the team's most consistent offensive threat, and Nico Hischier brings the two-way reliability that every championship team needs. The Devils' problem isn't a lack of talent. It's the inability to play a complete 60-minute game on a nightly basis. Too many letdowns after strong stretches have cost them dearly.

The 6.0 total is a tighter number than the 6.5 we see on a lot of games tonight, and it reflects the defensive capabilities both teams possess when they're locked in. This is a game where special teams could be the deciding factor. Washington's power play needs to be better than it has been for stretches this season, while New Jersey's ability to draw penalties and capitalize on man advantages could swing the game in their favor. The WSH -1.5 at +185 offers nice value if you believe the Capitals' home-ice advantage and the desperation to hold their playoff spot will lead to a multi-goal victory. But the Devils at +110 on the moneyline aren't going away quietly, and this has the makings of a one-goal game decided in the final five minutes of the third period.

Avalanche @ Blackhawks

Friday, 8:30 PM ET | United Center, Chicago, IL
Puck Line
COL -1.5
Moneyline
COL -258 / CHI +210
Total
O/U 6.5

The Colorado Avalanche (44-13-10, 98 points) are the best team in the NHL and it's not particularly close. They roll into the United Center on Friday night to face a Chicago Blackhawks team (26-30-12, 64 points) that is firmly in the bottom third of the league and playing out the string. The -258 moneyline on Colorado is the heaviest favorite on the board tonight, and honestly, it probably should be even steeper given the talent disparity between these rosters. Colorado has been a buzzsaw all season, and they don't tend to take nights off against weaker opponents. This is a team that plays with an intensity and a hunger that suggests they know something special is brewing in Denver.

Nathan MacKinnon has been putting together what might be the best season of his career, and when you consider the seasons he's already had, that's saying something extraordinary. His combination of speed, vision, and finishing ability makes him the most dangerous player on the planet right now, and the linemates around him have elevated their play as a result. Cale Makar continues to redefine what a defenseman can do in the modern NHL, contributing offensively at a historic pace while also being one of the best defenders in the league. The Avalanche's depth is what truly separates them from the pack, though. This isn't a one-line team. They can roll four lines at you with legitimate scoring threats on each one, and that kind of depth is what wins championships. Their 98 points through 67 games puts them on pace for a 120-point season, which would be a franchise record.

Chicago, to their credit, has shown more fight than their record suggests at various points this season. Connor Bedard continues to develop into the franchise cornerstone the Blackhawks hoped he'd be, and his flashes of brilliance give the fanbase something to be excited about even in a losing season. The problem is that Bedard is surrounded by a roster that simply isn't equipped to compete with elite teams on a nightly basis. The 12 overtime losses tell you this is a team that competes hard but doesn't have the closing ability to finish games, and that's a function of roster depth more than effort. The Blackhawks are in the middle of a rebuild, and while the light at the end of the tunnel is visible with Bedard leading the charge, tonight's matchup against the Avalanche is a stark reminder of how far they still have to go.

The 6.5 total is the key number here. Colorado's offense is capable of putting up four or five goals against anyone, and Chicago's defense has struggled to contain elite teams all season. If the Avalanche come out firing, which they usually do, this game could get out of hand early. The Blackhawks' best hope is to keep this game low-event, slow the pace, and hope their goaltender steals them a period or two. But against a Colorado team that generates chances at the rate they do, that's an incredibly tall order. The COL -1.5 puck line is going to attract a lot of attention, and rightfully so. When the Avalanche are healthy and locked in, they don't just beat teams like Chicago, they dominate them from start to finish.

Panthers @ Flames

Friday, 9:00 PM ET | Scotiabank Saddledome, Calgary, AB
Puck Line
FLA -1.5 / CGY +1.5
Moneyline
FLA -115 / CGY -105
Total
O/U 6.0

Here's a fascinating spot for the defending back-to-back Stanley Cup champion Panthers. Florida (34-31-3, 71 points) has had a deeply underwhelming season by their lofty standards, sitting outside the playoff picture as of late March. A team that won it all in both 2024 and 2025 now finds itself scrambling for points and staring at the very real possibility of missing the postseason entirely. They head into the Scotiabank Saddledome to face a Calgary Flames team (27-34-7, 61 points) that is firmly in rebuild mode and has been one of the weaker teams in the Western Conference all year. The nearly even moneyline, with Florida at -115 and Calgary at -105, tells you the market doesn't have a ton of confidence in either side, and that's entirely justified given how both teams have performed this season.

Florida's fall from grace has been one of the most shocking stories in the NHL this year. This is a roster that featured enough talent to win two consecutive championships, but the combination of key departures, fatigue from deep playoff runs, and the inevitable regression that comes from being at the top for so long has caught up with them. Aleksander Barkov remains one of the most complete players in the sport, providing elite two-way play night after night. Matthew Tkachuk has been productive but hasn't quite replicated the dominant form he showed during their title runs. Sam Reinhart has been solid offensively, but the depth that made Florida so dangerous in the postseason has thinned considerably. At 71 points through 68 games, the Panthers need to go on an absolute tear just to have a chance at the playoffs, and trips to western Canada aren't typically where teams find easy wins.

Calgary is in full-on sell mode after shipping Nazem Kadri to Colorado at the trade deadline earlier this month. With their leading scorer gone, the Flames are essentially playing meaningless games from a standings perspective, which can be a double-edged sword. On one hand, there's no pressure and young players get extended opportunities. On the other, there's often a lack of urgency that can lead to sloppy play. Mikael Backlund has shouldered the top-line burden admirably, and the young players in the lineup are getting valuable reps that will serve the franchise well down the road. But the bottom line is that this is a team with 61 points, and they're not constructed to compete with teams that have championship pedigree, even if that pedigree has faded considerably this season.

The 6.0 total on this game reflects the offensive limitations of both teams. Calgary has struggled to score consistently all season, and Florida, while more talented offensively, hasn't been the same dynamic attacking team that scored seemingly at will during their Cup runs. This feels like a game that could go either way, and the close moneyline confirms that. If the Panthers treat this like a must-win, which it absolutely is at this stage, they should have enough talent to pull away in the third period. But if Florida comes out flat on the road in the second game of what could be a western trip, Calgary's home crowd could provide the energy for an upset. The value here might be on the Flames at -105, simply because the Panthers haven't earned the benefit of the doubt this season, championship rings or not.

Ducks @ Utah Mammoth

Friday, 10:00 PM ET | Delta Center, Salt Lake City, UT
Puck Line
UTA -1.5 (+205)
Moneyline
UTA -122 / ANA +102
Total
O/U 5.5

The nightcap on Friday's slate is a Pacific Division clash that carries enormous playoff implications for both teams. The Anaheim Ducks (37-27-4, 78 points) and the Utah Mammoth (36-27-6, 78 points) are deadlocked at 78 points in the standings, making this a de facto four-point game in the race for playoff positioning. Utah being a slight -122 home favorite reflects the home-ice advantage at the Delta Center, where the Mammoth's passionate fanbase has made life difficult for visiting teams in their inaugural season. But the Ducks at +102 are essentially a pick'em, and that's because Anaheim's young core has proven all season that they can compete with anyone, anywhere.

Anaheim's emergence as a Pacific Division contender has been one of the best storylines in the NHL this year. Cutter Gauthier has been absolutely sensational, establishing himself as one of the premier goal scorers in the Western Conference with a powerful combination of speed, shot, and instincts around the net. Leo Carlsson has been excellent as a dynamic young center, and Beckett Sennecke has provided legitimate offensive production as a rookie. The veteran addition of Chris Kreider has given this young group a calming presence and a proven playoff performer. The Ducks play with a fearless, attacking style that's a product of their youth, and that energy can be incredibly difficult to contain, especially for a Utah team that is still figuring out their identity in their first season. Anaheim's 78 points through 68 games puts them right on pace for a playoff berth, and they're not going to let a road trip to Salt Lake City derail their momentum.

Utah's first season has been a remarkable success by any measure. Taking on the former Arizona Coyotes' roster and transforming it into a playoff contender in Year 1 was no small feat, and the Delta Center atmosphere has become one of the best in the league. Clayton Keller has been the offensive catalyst, providing the kind of elite playmaking ability that every team needs from their top forward. Logan Cooley has shown flashes of being a future franchise center, and the blue line has been competitive. The Mammoth's home record has been strong, and the energy of a city experiencing NHL hockey for the first time cannot be overstated. That kind of atmosphere creates a tangible home-ice advantage that goes beyond what the numbers show, and visiting teams have learned to respect the Delta Center this season.

The 5.5 total is the lowest on the board tonight, and it makes sense when you consider the stakes. Playoff-type games tend to tighten up defensively, with both teams playing a more conservative, structured style to avoid costly mistakes. Both goaltending situations will be critical, because in a game this tight, one bad goal can be the difference. The moneyline being so close, UTA -122 to ANA +102, tells you this game is genuinely 50/50 in the market's eyes. Home ice is essentially the tiebreaker. This is the kind of late-night Pacific Division battle that casual fans might miss but hardcore hockey fans live for, because every shift matters, every puck battle is contested, and the winner walks away with a massive two points in a race where neither team can afford to lose ground.
