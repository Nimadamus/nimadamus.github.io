8-Game Friday Slate

NBA March 13, 2026

Friday's eight-game slate is headlined by a Prime Video doubleheader, with the Cleveland Cavaliers (40-26) visiting the rebuilding Dallas Mavericks at 7:30 PM ET and the Minnesota Timberwolves (40-26) traveling to Golden State at 10 PM ET with Stephen Curry sidelined. The night features an absurd amount of lopsided spreads, with six games carrying double-digit lines, led by Detroit's dominant 47-18 squad hosting Memphis as 15.5-point favorites and Portland laying 14.5 against Utah's tanking Jazz.

Cavaliers @ Mavericks

Friday, 7:30 PM ET | American Airlines Center, Dallas, TX | Prime Video
Spread
CLE -13.5
Moneyline
CLE -900 / DAL +600
Total
O/U 222.5

Here's your Prime Video headliner, and frankly, it's not much of a contest on paper. The Cleveland Cavaliers at 40-26 have been one of the steadiest teams in the Eastern Conference all season, and they're walking into American Airlines Center against a Dallas Mavericks team that's 22-44 and firmly in the lottery conversation. Cleveland's balanced attack, led by Donovan Mitchell's scoring punch and a defense that ranks among the top ten in the league, makes them a nightmare matchup for a Dallas roster that's been gutted since the Luka Doncic trade to the Lakers in February 2025. The 13.5-point spread is massive for a nationally televised game, but it reflects the canyon between these two rosters.

Dallas has leaned heavily on rookie Cooper Flagg this season, and to his credit, the kid has shown flashes of brilliance. But flashes don't win games in March, and the Mavericks' supporting cast simply doesn't have the depth or experience to hang with a playoff-caliber team for 48 minutes. Flagg's development is the story in Dallas right now, not wins and losses, and that's exactly the kind of environment where a team like Cleveland can blow the doors off early and cruise to the finish. The Mavs are giving up points at an alarming rate, and their half-court offense stalls when defenses can key in on Flagg as the primary option.

The 222.5 total feels about right for a game where Cleveland should be able to score at will. The Cavaliers' offensive efficiency has been excellent in recent weeks, and Dallas's defense doesn't have the personnel to slow them down. The question is whether Dallas can generate enough offense to push this toward the over, or if Cleveland's defense clamps down and turns this into a 115-100 type game. Either way, this feels like a Cleveland blowout. The -900 moneyline is prohibitive, but the spread tells you everything you need to know about the market's expectations. This is a team playing for seeding against a team playing for ping pong balls, and that gap shows up on the court every single night.

One thing to watch: Cleveland's road form has been solid but not spectacular, and there's always a letdown risk when you're a massive favorite in what feels like a meaningless game. If the Cavs come out flat in the first quarter, Dallas could hang around long enough to make that 13.5 interesting. But the talent disparity is so severe that even a sluggish Cleveland should pull away by the third quarter. The national TV stage might give Flagg some extra motivation, but motivation doesn't close a 13.5-point talent gap.

Timberwolves @ Warriors

The late-night Prime Video offering is significantly more interesting than the early game, even if the storyline is somewhat depressing for Warriors fans. Stephen Curry is OUT with a knee injury, and that absence transforms this from a competitive matchup into a game where Golden State has to figure out how to generate offense without the greatest shooter in basketball history. The Warriors at 32-33 are already on a three-game skid, and losing Curry takes the floor general out of an offense that desperately needs his gravity and playmaking. Minnesota at 40-26 is the clearly superior team here, even though they've dropped three straight themselves.

Anthony Edwards has been a force of nature all season, and he's exactly the kind of player who thrives in these nationally televised spots. Edwards brings the highlight-reel dunks, the deep threes, and the competitive fire that makes him appointment television. Without Curry on the other side, there's no comparable star power to match Edwards, and Minnesota's defensive length and athleticism should be able to suffocate Golden State's secondary options. The Timberwolves' three-game losing streak is a concern, but the talent gap without Curry tips this one decisively in Minnesota's direction.

The 213.5 total is the lowest on the board, and that tells you the market expects this to be a grind. Without Curry, Golden State's offensive ceiling drops dramatically, and Minnesota's defense is built to make life miserable for opponents. The Timberwolves can turn games into rock fights when they lock in defensively, and a Curry-less Warriors team is exactly the kind of opponent that gets smothered. If Golden State can't hit threes at a decent clip without their maestro creating looks, this could easily stay under.

The fascinating subplot here is the battle of streaks. Both teams come in having lost three straight, and somebody's slump has to end. Minnesota's losing streak feels more like bad luck and a tough schedule, while Golden State's feels more systemic, especially now with the Curry injury. The Warriors at home with their crowd behind them could put up a fight early, but the second half is where the talent difference usually shows up in these spots. Minnesota -6 is a big number, but the Curry absence is a game-changer that justifies every point of it.

Grizzlies @ Pistons

Spread

O/U 224.5

Let's talk about the best story in the NBA this season. The Detroit Pistons at 47-18 have gone from league laughingstock to the most dominant team in basketball in the span of a single offseason. Cade Cunningham has taken a massive leap, transforming from a promising young guard into a legitimate MVP candidate who's orchestrating one of the most efficient offenses in the league. Detroit's 47-18 record is the best in the Eastern Conference, and they've been doing it with a style of play that's both aesthetically pleasing and ruthlessly effective. The transformation at Little Caesars Arena has been nothing short of remarkable.

Memphis at 23-42 is living in the opposite reality. The Grizzlies' rebuild has them firmly in the lottery, and they don't have the firepower to compete with Detroit's starting five, let alone their depth. The 15.5-point spread is eye-popping, but when you watch these two teams play, it actually feels light. Detroit has been blowing out inferior opponents at home all season, and their defensive intensity doesn't drop even when they build big leads. The Pistons play with a chip on their shoulder, like a team that remembers being terrible and refuses to ever go back to that place.

The -1200 moneyline is completely untouchable, so don't even bother looking at it straight up. The real question is whether Detroit covers that 15.5. The Pistons have been a strong ATS team at home this season, and Memphis doesn't have the defensive ability to slow down Cunningham and the Detroit offense. The 224.5 total reflects the potential for Detroit to push this into the 120s on their end while Memphis scrapes together enough points to keep the total in play. This should be over by halftime, with the only drama being whether the Pistons' bench can keep the foot on the gas in the fourth quarter.

Indiana at 15-51 is the worst team in the NBA, and it's not even close. The Pacers have been absolutely decimated by the loss of Tyrese Haliburton to an Achilles injury suffered during the 2025 Finals, and without their franchise point guard, this roster has completely fallen apart. The talent simply isn't there to compete on a nightly basis, and the results reflect that brutal reality. New York at 42-25 is the second-best team in the East, and Jalen Brunson has been playing at an All-NBA level all season. This is a mismatch in every sense of the word, and the 13.5-point spread barely scratches the surface of the talent gap.

Brunson's ability to control the tempo and pick apart defenses is going to be on full display against a Pacers defense that simply can't guard anyone. New York's physicality, depth, and defensive toughness make them a nightmare for a team as thin as Indiana, and the Knicks have shown no mercy against bottom-feeders this season. They play hard for 48 minutes regardless of the score, which is a testament to their coaching staff and the culture they've built. The Knicks aren't a team that takes their foot off the gas when they get up big, and that's bad news for Indiana backers hoping for a garbage-time comeback.

The 227.5 total is the highest on the board, and it reflects New York's offensive firepower more than any expectation that Indiana will keep pace. The Knicks could realistically score 125+ in this one, and Indiana's defense gives up enough points to push the total over even with a limited offensive attack. The Pacers' home crowd has been understandably thin this season, and Gainbridge Fieldhouse won't provide much of a home-court advantage. This has "Knicks cruise to a 30-point win" written all over it, and the only suspense will be whether the starters play enough minutes for the spread to matter.

Suns @ Raptors

Spread
TOR -4.5
Moneyline
TOR -190 / PHX +160

O/U 228.5

This is the closest game on Friday's board, and it's the one that deserves the most attention from a pure basketball standpoint. Phoenix at 39-27 is having a solid season, but they've lost Kevin Durant, who was traded to Houston in July 2025, and the Suns have had to reconfigure their identity around Devin Booker and Bradley Beal as the primary duo. Booker has been spectacular this year, carrying a heavier load and responding with some of the best basketball of his career. Beal has been the steady second option Phoenix hoped he'd be, but the question is whether this duo can match up with Toronto's new-look roster on the road.

Toronto at 36-29 has been one of the more pleasant surprises in the Eastern Conference, and a lot of that has to do with Brandon Ingram, who has given the Raptors a legitimate go-to scorer since arriving via trade. Ingram's ability to create his own shot in isolation, combined with his improved playmaking, has elevated Toronto's offensive ceiling significantly. Scotiabank Arena has been a hostile environment for visiting teams this season, and the Raptors' home record has been a strength all year. The -4.5 spread reflects Toronto's home-court advantage and their overall consistency at home.

The 228.5 total is the highest individual game total on the board, and for good reason. Both of these teams can flat-out score. Phoenix's offensive approach under Booker generates a ton of points in a hurry, and Toronto's Ingram-led attack is similarly explosive. Neither team is a defensive juggernaut, which means possessions should be plentiful and points should flow freely. This has the makings of a 118-112 type game where both offenses are clicking, and the entertainment value should be high.

The playoff implications make this one even more compelling. Phoenix is fighting for positioning in the Western Conference playoff picture, and every road win matters. Toronto is trying to solidify their spot in the East and potentially move up in the standings. Both teams have real motivation, which should translate to a high-effort, competitive game from start to finish. The TOR -4.5 feels like a fair number, and this could easily go either way depending on which team's stars have the hotter hand.

Houston at 40-25 is third in the Western Conference, and the Kevin Durant trade from Phoenix in July 2025 has transformed this franchise from a rebuilding project into a legitimate contender. Durant brings championship pedigree, scoring ability from every spot on the floor, and a mentoring presence that's accelerated the development of Houston's young core. The Rockets have been one of the most improved teams in the NBA, and Toyota Center has become one of the toughest buildings for opponents this season. At home against a rebuilding Pelicans squad, Houston should be in complete control from the opening tip.

The Pelicans at 22-45 have had a miserable season. The roster lost its second-best player in the Brandon Ingram trade, and they've been scrambling to find offensive identity ever since. Zion Williamson's health is always the elephant in the room with this franchise, and when he's not at full strength, New Orleans simply doesn't have enough talent to compete with the league's upper tier. The Pelicans are playing out the string, and road games against playoff teams are where the losses pile up fastest.

The -7 spread is actually modest for Houston at home against a team this weak. The Rockets could easily win by 15-20 if they come out focused, and Durant has shown a tendency to dominate against inferior competition, putting games away early with his otherworldly scoring efficiency. The 219.5 total reflects Houston's defensive improvement this season, with the Rockets playing a more disciplined brand of basketball that limits opponent scoring opportunities. New Orleans' offense has been inconsistent enough to keep the total in check, even if Houston puts up their usual 110-115 points.

This is the kind of game where the in-state rivalry angle might inject some extra energy. Houston and New Orleans are close geographically, and Pelicans players won't want to get embarrassed on the road against a team in their own division. But wanting to compete and actually being able to are two different things, and the talent gap here is significant. Durant and the Rockets should handle business without too much drama, and the focus will quickly shift to bigger matchups down the road.

Utah at 20-46 is in full tank mode, and there's no sugarcoating it. The Jazz have been one of the worst teams in the NBA this season, and they're playing with a roster designed to lose games and accumulate draft capital. Portland at 31-35 isn't exactly lighting the world on fire either, but the Blazers are a significantly better basketball team, and at home, they should be able to handle a Jazz squad that's essentially waving the white flag every night. The 14.5-point spread is enormous, but it reflects the complete lack of competitive talent on Utah's roster.

Portland has been inconsistent this season, hovering around .500 and unable to string together enough wins to really make a push for the playoff picture. But inconsistency against playoff teams is one thing, and playing the Jazz is another matter entirely. Utah doesn't defend, doesn't rebound with enough physicality, and doesn't execute in the half court with any consistency. The Blazers' home crowd at Moda Center should see a stress-free Friday night where Portland builds a big lead early and coasts to the finish.

The 218.5 total is on the lower end of the board, suggesting the market expects this to be a lower-scoring affair. That makes sense when you consider that Utah's offense is one of the least efficient in the league, and Portland's defense at home has been solid enough to limit bad teams. The Jazz might struggle to crack 100 in this one, which would put significant pressure on the over. Portland putting up 115-118 while holding Utah to 95-100 is the most likely game script, and that would keep this under the total. This is a game for Portland fans to enjoy a blowout and for everyone else to move on to the next one.

The nightcap of Friday's slate sends the Chicago Bulls into the Intuit Dome for a game that looks bleak on paper. Chicago at 27-39 has been drifting through the season without much direction, and the Zach LaVine trade to the Kings stripped away their most dynamic offensive weapon. The Bulls are a team without a clear identity, playing out the string with a roster that isn't good enough to make the playoffs and isn't bad enough to guarantee a top lottery pick. It's the worst place to be in the NBA, and the results on the court reflect that limbo.

LA at 33-32 is fighting for their playoff lives, and that desperation should translate to a high-energy performance at home. James Harden is still putting up numbers and orchestrating the Clippers' offense with the kind of crafty playmaking that's defined his career. The Clippers need every win they can get to stay in the play-in picture, and a home game against the struggling Bulls is exactly the kind of opportunity they can't afford to waste. The Intuit Dome crowd should be into it from the start, and the Clippers' depth gives them a significant advantage over Chicago's thin rotation.

The -13 spread is steep, but it's driven by the Bulls' complete inability to compete on the road against motivated teams. Chicago's road record has been dreadful, and their defense gives up too many easy points to contain a Clippers offense that can attack from multiple angles. Harden, in particular, feasts on weak defensive teams, and the Bulls' backcourt defense has been among the worst in the league. The 216.5 total is on the lower end, but the Clippers' defensive effort at home should keep Chicago in the low 90s while the Clippers push toward 115.

This is one of those late-night West Coast games that most casual fans will miss, and honestly, they won't be missing much in terms of competitive drama. The Clippers should control this wire to wire, and the biggest question is whether they take their foot off the gas late and let Chicago backdoor the spread. LA's motivation as a team fighting for playoff positioning should prevent that from happening, making this a comfortable Clippers win in front of their home fans to close out the Friday night slate.
