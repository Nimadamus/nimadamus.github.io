Quarter Kelly vs Full Kelly

Understanding Fractional Kelly for Smarter Bankroll Management

The Kelly Criterion tells you the mathematically optimal bet size to maximize long-term bankroll growth. But "optimal" doesn't always mean "best for you." This guide explains why most successful bettors use fractional Kelly and how to choose the right fraction for your situation.

Kelly Fractions Comparison

Metric Quarter Kelly (25%) Half Kelly (50%) Full Kelly (100%)
Growth Rate ~50% of optimal ~75% of optimal Maximum
Volatility Very Low Moderate Very High
Max Drawdown Risk ~25% ~50% ~85%+
Edge Estimation Error Tolerance High Medium Low
Best For New bettors, Props Most bettors Proven edges only

Why Full Kelly Is Rarely Used

Full Kelly sounds great in theory: maximum growth rate means maximum profits over time. But there are serious practical problems:

Edge Estimation Errors: Kelly assumes you know your exact win probability. In reality, you're estimating. If you overestimate your edge by just 2-3%, full Kelly can lead to overbetting and significant losses.

Extreme Volatility: Full Kelly can result in 50-85% drawdowns even when you have a genuine edge. Most bettors cannot psychologically or financially handle watching their bankroll drop by half.

No Margin for Error: One bad month of results (which happens to everyone) can devastate a full Kelly bankroll. The math works out long-term, but "long-term" might be thousands of bets.

The Case for Half Kelly

Why Half Kelly Is the Industry Standard

Half Kelly gives you 75% of full Kelly's expected growth rate while cutting variance roughly in half. This is the sweet spot where you capture most of the upside while protecting yourself from estimation errors and natural variance.

Most professional sports bettors and investment managers use half Kelly or similar fractions. It's battle-tested across decades of real-world application.

When to Use Quarter Kelly

Quarter Kelly is not "too conservative" - it's smart risk management in these situations:

New to Bankroll Management: If you're just starting to track your bets systematically, use quarter Kelly until you have 100+ graded bets proving your edge.

Betting Props or Parlays: These markets have higher variance and your edge estimation is less reliable. Quarter Kelly protects against the additional uncertainty.

Correlated Bets: If you're betting multiple games on the same slate that might be correlated (weather, referee tendencies), reduce to quarter Kelly.

Capital Preservation Priority: If protecting your bankroll is more important than maximizing growth, quarter Kelly provides steady, sustainable returns.

Practical Example: Same Edge, Different Fractions

Scenario: $10,000 Bankroll, 4% Edge at -110 Odds

Full Kelly suggests: 8.8% = $880 bet

Half Kelly suggests: 4.4% = $440 bet

Quarter Kelly suggests: 2.2% = $220 bet

After 100 bets (assuming edge holds):

Full Kelly expected bankroll: ~$24,000 (but could range from $4,000 to $60,000+)

Half Kelly expected bankroll: ~$18,000 (more likely range: $10,000 to $35,000)

Quarter Kelly expected bankroll: ~$14,500 (tight range: $11,000 to $20,000)

Which Fraction Should You Use?

Start Conservative: Begin with quarter Kelly. After 6 months of proven results with positive CLV (closing line value), consider moving to half Kelly. Only move to full Kelly if you have years of documented edge and can handle extreme volatility.

For NFL/NCAAF: Half Kelly maximum due to small sample sizes and high game variance. Quarter Kelly for player props.

For NBA/MLB: Half Kelly for sides and totals once you've proven your edge over 100+ bets. Quarter Kelly for player props.

For Parlays: Quarter Kelly or less. The compounding of edge estimation errors makes larger fractions dangerous.

General Rule: When in doubt, use a smaller fraction. The growth rate sacrifice is minimal, and the protection against ruin is significant.
