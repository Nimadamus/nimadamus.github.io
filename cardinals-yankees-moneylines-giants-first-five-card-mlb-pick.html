Six units across three tickets, and every one of them is grounded in who takes the ball.

Giants First Five: Logan Webb Against An 8.23 ERA

This is the flagship, and the reason it is a first five innings bet rather than a full-game one matters. San Francisco is 42-60 and Los Angeles is 41-62, two teams going nowhere, and the back of both bullpens is exactly the sort of coin flip you want no part of. So we isolate the piece of the game that is not a coin flip: the starters. Over the first five innings you are simply betting Logan Webb against Grayson Rodriguez, and that is about as one-sided as a pitching matchup gets.

Webb is the Giants' workhorse and one of the steadiest arms in the National League. He carries a 3.87 ERA and a 1.11 WHIP across 107 innings and 17 starts, with 85 strikeouts and only nine home runs allowed. He goes deep, he limits baserunners, and he keeps the ball in the yard at pitcher-friendly Oracle Park, which is exactly the profile you want holding a lead through five. His record sits at 5-7, but that is a function of the offense behind him, not the arm.

Grayson Rodriguez, acquired by the Angels over the offseason, has been the opposite. He owns an 8.23 ERA and a 1.77 WHIP over eight starts and 35 innings, having allowed five home runs and walked far too many. A pitcher giving up baserunners at that clip against a Webb start is the entire thesis: the Giants do not need much offense in the first five innings, they need Webb to do his job while Rodriguez does his. At -175 for the first five, this is the three-unit play on the card.

Cardinals Moneyline: The Better Arm At A Fair Home Price

St. Louis is 52-50 and hosts a Cincinnati club that is 47-54 with a run differential of minus 63, one of the worst in the National League. The Reds have won two in a row, but the season-long picture is a team that has been outscored 486 to 423, and now they walk into Busch Stadium with the wrong man on the mound.

Cincinnati starts Rhett Lowder, who has been the softest starter in this game by a wide margin: a 5.75 ERA and a 1.64 WHIP across 72 innings and 14 starts, with a 3-7 record. A 1.64 WHIP means baserunners in bunches, and against a St. Louis lineup at home that is a recipe for early trouble. The Cardinals counter with Dustin May, who at a 4.78 ERA and a 1.32 WHIP over 98 innings is far from perfect but is comfortably the more reliable of the two, and he throws in front of a staff whose 4.18 team ERA is better than Cincinnati's 4.56.

This is not a bet on a dominant favorite. It is a bet on the better team, at home, throwing the better starter against a club that is minus 63 on the season and running a 5.75 ERA arm out there. At -159 the price is fair for that combination, and two units is the right weight.

Yankees Moneyline: The Best Staff In The League At Plus Money

The value here is simple: you rarely get the American League's best run-prevention team at a plus price, and Friday you do. New York is 57-45 with a plus 88 run differential, has allowed a league-low 392 runs, and owns the AL's best staff ERA at 3.38. On the mound is Cam Schlittler, who has been outstanding at 9-6 with a 2.20 ERA and a 0.96 WHIP over 123 innings and 145 strikeouts. That is an ace-level line, and the Yankees are getting it as a road underdog.

Philadelphia is a very good team too, 56-47 with Jesus Luzardo on the mound, which is why this is one unit and not more, and why it lives on this card rather than as a headline. But the Phillies staff ERA of 4.27 is nearly a run worse than New York's, and getting the better run-prevention club at +122 in a game the market itself sees as close to a coin flip is a clean spot. What Can Beat It

The Giants first five is the safest of the three because it removes the bullpens, but Webb still has to be Webb. If he has a rare clunker and Rodriguez steals five league-average innings, the ticket flips, though the ERAs say that is the exception rather than the rule. The Cardinals play carries the ordinary risk of a mid-tier favorite: Lowder has had scattered good starts even in a rough year, and the Reds are hot enough right now to steal one. And the Yankees, for all their pitching, are on the road against a legitimate contender in a hitter-friendly park, so a single Luzardo gem or a Schlittler off night is enough to lose a one-unit dog.

None of these are locks, and they are not priced as locks. The connective logic is that all three lean on the same thing: the arm we want is meaningfully better than the arm across from it, and the price still gives us room. That is the spot to bet.

The Bottom Line

Six units on three sides, all anchored to starting pitching. The Giants first five at -175 is the flagship because Logan Webb against an 8.23 ERA is the cleanest starter edge on the board once you take the bullpens out of it. The Cardinals at -159 are the better team throwing the better arm at home against a minus-63 club. And the Yankees at +122 are the best run-prevention team in the league catching a plus price on the road. Take the arms and let the standings do the rest.
