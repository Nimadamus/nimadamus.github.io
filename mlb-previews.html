Final Day of Spring Training

MLB Previews Today

The last day of exhibition baseball before the 2026 season opens tomorrow with the Yankees at Giants. Today's spring training finales feature Shohei Ohtani's final pitching tuneup, Roki Sasaki's troubled spring, Gerrit Cole's Tommy John rehab, Justin Verlander back in Tigers stripes, and the Spencer Strider injury blow for the Braves.

Last Updated: March 24, 2026

Season Eve: What to Watch on Opening DayYankees at Giants | March 25 | 4:05 PM ET | ESPN

The 2026 MLB season kicks off tomorrow afternoon in San Francisco, and the storylines could not be richer. The Yankees, coming off a disappointing 2025 campaign that ended with a first-round exit, have retooled around Aaron Judge and a revamped pitching staff. The Giants, meanwhile, are banking on a healthy rotation and a lineup anchored by their offseason acquisitions to compete in a loaded NL West.

This is just the second time since 2000 that the season opener features an interleague matchup, and the first time the Giants have hosted Opening Day since 2016. Oracle Park will be rocking, and with ESPN carrying the broadcast nationally, it sets the stage for one of the most anticipated first pitches in recent memory. The pitching matchup hasn't been officially announced by either club yet, but all signs point to the aces getting the ball.

For bettors, Opening Day brings unique challenges. Spring training stats are largely meaningless for projecting regular-season performance, and roster decisions are still being finalized across the league. The smart play is to sit back and watch how teams look in live action before committing real money. That said, the over/under in Oracle Park tends to play low early in the season when the marine layer is at its heaviest, something worth noting if totals markets open aggressively.

Beyond Yankees-Giants, the rest of the league opens on March 26 with a full slate. Keep an eye on the Dodgers, who are chasing a third consecutive World Series title, something no franchise has accomplished since the 1998-2000 Yankees dynasty. The Cubs, with Alex Bregman now anchoring the infield, enter 2026 as legitimate contenders. And the Tigers, with Justin Verlander back in Detroit, have a feel-good story that could translate into early-season success if the rotation holds.

Shohei Ohtani's Final Pitching TuneupDodgers Spring Training | March 24

Shohei Ohtani's return to the mound has been the biggest story of spring training, and today's outing represents his final dress rehearsal before the regular season. After missing the entire 2025 pitching season recovering from UCL surgery, Ohtani has looked sharp in his Cactus League appearances, sitting 96-98 mph with his fastball and flashing the devastating sweeper that made him one of baseball's most unhittable arms before the injury.

The Dodgers have been cautious with his workload, gradually building his pitch count over four spring starts. He's expected to throw around 75-80 pitches today, simulating what would be a typical five-inning regular-season outing. His command has been the biggest question mark, as he walked seven batters across his first three starts, but his most recent outing saw just one walk over four innings with six strikeouts.

For the Dodgers, having Ohtani as a two-way player again is a game-changer on multiple levels. Not only do they get an elite arm in the rotation, but his presence at DH on days he doesn't pitch gives LA the deepest lineup in baseball. Manager Dave Roberts has indicated Ohtani will start on a five-day rotation with no pitch count restrictions once the season begins, though the team will monitor his innings carefully through the first half.

The advanced metrics from spring are encouraging. Ohtani's spin rates are tracking at or above his pre-surgery levels, and his velocity hasn't dropped at all. The sweeper is generating a 45% whiff rate in spring, which would rank among the best single-pitch whiff rates in baseball if sustained. If Ohtani is truly all the way back, the Dodgers' three-peat bid just got a massive boost.

Roki Sasaki's Rocky Transition to MLBDodgers Spring Training | March 24

While Ohtani's comeback has been the feel-good story, Roki Sasaki's spring has been anything but smooth. The 24-year-old Japanese phenom, who signed with the Dodgers in January, has struggled with consistency in his Cactus League outings, posting a 6.75 ERA across four starts with control issues that have raised legitimate concerns about his readiness for the majors.

Sasaki's stuff remains elite when he's right. His fastball touches 101 mph and his splitter is a devastating put-away pitch. But the adjustment to MLB-caliber hitters, different ball texture, and larger mound dimensions has been apparent. He's been overthrowing his fastball and leaving his splitter up in the zone, two tendencies that NPB hitters rarely punished but MLB lineups will feast on.

The Dodgers' brass has downplayed the struggles publicly, but privately there are discussions about whether Sasaki should start the season in Triple-A Oklahoma City to continue his development. The organization has the rotation depth to absorb that decision, with Ohtani, Yoshinobu Yamamoto, Tyler Glasnow, and Bobby Miller already locked into spots. Rushing Sasaki into a fifth-starter role when he's clearly not comfortable could do more harm than good.

For fantasy managers who drafted Sasaki expecting an immediate impact, the spring has been a cold shower. His average draft position was in the top 50 pitchers, but his command issues suggest he may need several weeks to find his footing. The talent is undeniable, but the transition from NPB to MLB is rarely seamless, and Sasaki's spring has been a textbook example of that adjustment period.

Gerrit Cole's Tommy John Comeback: Cubs Rotation CenterpieceCubs Spring Training | March 24

Gerrit Cole's journey from the Yankees to the Cubs was one of the biggest offseason shocks, and his Tommy John rehab has been the central storyline of Mesa this spring. The former Cy Young winner, who underwent surgery in June 2025, has been throwing bullpen sessions and simulated games throughout camp, and the Cubs are targeting an early April activation if his final spring outings go smoothly.

Cole's velocity has been the most-watched metric in all of spring training. He sat 94-95 mph in his most recent live batting practice session, down from his pre-surgery average of 97.1 mph. That drop is typical for Tommy John recoveries at this stage, and the Cubs' medical staff expects him to regain 1-2 mph as the season progresses. More encouraging is his breaking ball command, which has been crisp throughout the rehab process.

The Cubs traded significant prospect capital to acquire Cole's rights from the Yankees, then signed him to a three-year extension. It was a bold bet on a pitcher coming off major surgery, but if Cole returns to even 80% of his pre-injury form, the Cubs suddenly have a legitimate ace atop a rotation that also features Shota Imanaga and Justin Steele. Combined with the Bregman signing, Chicago is all-in on 2026.

The biggest concern isn't Cole's arm but rather his pitch mix. Tommy John recoveries often affect a pitcher's ability to throw certain pitches, particularly cutters and sliders that put stress on the repaired ligament. Cole has historically relied heavily on his four-seam/knuckle-curve combination, which should translate well post-surgery, but his slider usage may need to be dialed back initially. The Cubs are reportedly developing a new changeup grip for him as an alternative secondary offering.

Verlander Returns to Detroit: A Storybook Ending?Tigers vs. Rockies | Spring Training | March 24

Justin Verlander in a Tigers uniform again might be the most sentimental story in baseball this spring. The 43-year-old right-hander, who left Detroit via trade in 2017, signed a one-year deal in February to return to the franchise where he became a superstar. Today's spring finale against the Rockies will be his last tune-up before taking the ball on Opening Day, a moment that will bring Comerica Park to its feet.

Age is the obvious elephant in the room. Verlander posted a 4.48 ERA with the Astros in 2025 before becoming a free agent, and his fastball velocity has settled into the 91-93 mph range, well below the 95+ he carried through his prime. But Verlander has always been more than velocity. His curve remains a weapon, his pitch sequencing is masterful, and his competitive fire hasn't dimmed one bit.

The Tigers aren't expecting Verlander to be an ace. They need him to be a reliable innings eater who can mentor a young rotation that includes Tarik Skubal, Jackson Jobe, and Keider Montero. If Verlander can give Detroit 28-30 starts of league-average pitching, the signing is a success. Anything more is gravy. His spring numbers, a 3.60 ERA across five starts, suggest he can deliver on that modest expectation.

Beyond the on-field production, Verlander's presence has transformed the Tigers' clubhouse. Young pitchers have raved about his preparation and routine, and Skubal has credited Verlander with helping him refine his slider grip. It's the kind of veteran leadership that doesn't show up in box scores but can elevate an entire pitching staff. If the Tigers surprise people in the AL Central this year, Verlander's influence will be a significant reason why.

Spencer Strider's Injury Blow Rocks the BravesBraves Spring Training | March 24

The Atlanta Braves received devastating news this week when Spencer Strider was shut down with a setback in his recovery from UCL surgery, pushing his expected return to mid-summer at the earliest. The 27-year-old flamethrower, who struck out 281 batters in 2023 before the injury derailed his 2024 and 2025 seasons, was expected to rejoin the rotation in April but experienced elbow soreness during a live batting practice session.

For a Braves organization that was already thin on frontline pitching, the Strider news is a gut punch. Atlanta's rotation now leans heavily on Chris Sale, Reynaldo Lopez, and Charlie Morton, with Max Fried having departed via free agency after the 2024 season. The club will need AJ Smith-Shawver and Hurston Waldrep to take significant steps forward if they want to compete in the NL East.

The injury also raises uncomfortable long-term questions about Strider's durability. He's now missed significant time in back-to-back-to-back seasons, and there's growing concern among medical experts that his extreme arm speed and violent delivery create inherent injury risk that can't be fully mitigated. The Braves have him under team control through 2028, but the contract extension they signed him to before the initial surgery now looks increasingly risky.

From a betting perspective, the Braves' World Series odds have drifted significantly since the Strider news broke. Atlanta was +1200 entering spring training and has ballooned to +1800 at most sportsbooks. The NL East is now a two-horse race between the Mets and Phillies, with the Braves' ceiling significantly capped without their strikeout machine. If you had Braves futures, this is the kind of news that forces a painful reassessment.

Final Spring Training Standings SnapshotCactus League & Grapefruit League | March 24

Spring training records are notoriously meaningless for projecting regular-season success, but they do offer a window into which teams are sharp and which are still figuring things out. The Cactus League has been dominated by the Dodgers and Padres, both of whom have used their depth to overwhelm opponents. The Grapefruit League has seen the Orioles and Yankees post strong records, though neither team played their regulars as consistently as some competitors.

The teams that should concern their fanbases aren't necessarily the ones with losing spring records, but rather the ones showing troubling trends. The White Sox, widely projected as the worst team in baseball, have done nothing to dispel that notion, posting historically bad pitching numbers across camp. The Angels have also struggled mightily, particularly with bullpen depth, which was already their Achilles' heel entering the offseason.

Conversely, some under-the-radar teams have impressed. The Royals' young pitching staff has been excellent, and the Reds' offense has mashed throughout camp. Neither team is considered a serious contender, but both could be spoilers in their respective divisions if the spring momentum carries into April. The Royals in particular have a sneaky-good roster that could push for a wild card spot if everything breaks right.

As rosters finalize today, the most interesting storylines center on fringe roster battles. Several high-profile prospects across the league are waiting to learn whether they've made the Opening Day roster, and the final round of cuts will shape how teams look in the early going. Keep an eye on waiver wire activity over the next 48 hours, as teams release players who didn't make the cut and rival organizations scoop up potential bargains.
