Coors Field Does What Coors Field Does

The 10.5 total is the highest on Saturday's entire board, and if you've watched baseball for more than five minutes, you know exactly why. Coors Field at 5,280 feet above sea level turns routine fly balls into doubles, doubles into triples, and warning-track outs into home runs. The thin Denver air reduces pitch movement and allows the ball to carry farther than any other stadium in baseball, and it's been doing this for three decades without anybody figuring out how to consistently pitch well there as a visiting team. The Phillies send Jesus Luzardo to the mound, and the left-hander's arsenal theoretically plays well at altitude because his slider generates enough movement to survive the thin air, but Coors Field has humbled better pitchers than Luzardo.

Philadelphia at -246 is a massive favorite, and that price reflects both the talent gap between these two rosters and the Phillies' lineup being perfectly built to exploit Coors Field's dimensions. Bryce Harper, Kyle Schwarber, Trea Turner, and the rest of Philly's order can turn any game into a laugher when the ball is jumping, and Coors gives them the extra boost that makes their already-dangerous offense terrifying. Bryan Bernardino draws the start for Colorado, and pitching for the Rockies at home is one of the most thankless jobs in professional sports. Even good outings can get inflated by the altitude, and the margin for error is essentially zero.

The Rockies are a tough sell at +200 because while Coors Field keeps them in games, the overall roster gap between Colorado and Philadelphia is enormous. The Phillies have World Series aspirations, and the Rockies are in the early stages of what looks like a multi-year rebuild. That said, Coors Field games are inherently chaotic, and the altitude can compress talent gaps in a way that no other ballpark in baseball can. If you're looking at the over, the 10.5 at Coors feels like it's going to need a truly excellent pitching performance from one side to stay under, and the historical data at this venue suggests that's a tough ask.
