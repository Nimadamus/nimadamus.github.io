Brewers, Rockies and Diamondbacks Moneylines Plus a Rays-Blue Jays NRFI: A Four-Play MLB Card

July 20, 2026|9 min read|BetLegend

Monday brings a full fifteen-game board, and the shape of this card came together fast. Three of the four plays are sides, and every one of them is really a bet on the same gap: the difference between the arm on the mound for our team and the arm on the mound against it. The fourth play is a first-inning bet on two starters who have made a habit of clean opening frames. There is a heavy favorite here, a plus-money home dog at altitude, a mid-priced favorite against the worst starter on the board, and an NRFI in Toronto. Total exposure is 8.5 units, the biggest card of the week, because Monday's board offered the widest set of pitching mismatches we have seen in a while.

Brewers Moneyline: Misiorowski Against A Lineup That Cannot Touch Him

Start with the best pitcher on the entire Monday board. Jacob Misiorowski is 10-4 with a 1.62 ERA and a 0.76 WHIP, and the strikeout line reads like a video game: 167 punchouts against 27 walks across 111 innings, with opponents hitting a microscopic .148 off him. At American Family Field the numbers somehow get better, a 1.59 home ERA with a .153 opponent average and better than fourteen strikeouts per nine. When he takes the mound at home, run scoring basically stops being a realistic activity for the other dugout.

The other dugout tonight belongs to the 42-58 Mets, who arrive with the weakest batting average of any lineup on this card at .234 with a .686 team OPS. This is an offense averaging barely four runs a game for the season, and it now has to solve a right-hander holding hitters nearly 90 points below that team average. Meanwhile the 62-37 Brewers have quietly scored 502 runs behind a lineup hitting .253 with a .337 on-base percentage, patient at-bats stacked all the way down the order.

Freddy Peralta gets the ball for New York, and the road version of him is the problem: a 5.58 ERA and 1.54 WHIP away from home, with opponents hitting .283 and homers leaving at nearly 1.8 per nine. His overall 4.66 ERA is survivable against average offenses, but the Brewers' on-base machine against a road starter allowing that much traffic is exactly the profile that produces crooked innings. Laying -190 is never comfortable. Laying it with a three-run-per-game gap in current form and the biggest starter mismatch in baseball is different. Three units on the Brewers moneyline at -190.

Rockies Moneyline: Plus Money At Coors In A Coin-Flip Game

Now the flyer, and hear the whole argument out before judging the record. Yes, the Rockies are 40-61. But tonight's game is at Coors Field, the total on the board is sitting at a monstrous 12, and the market is pricing a coin flip: Washington around -123 with Colorado getting plus money back. In a projected slugfest at altitude, the sides essentially become a lineup-versus-lineup bet, and that is where this gets interesting, because the Rockies are hitting .255 with a .747 OPS and 484 runs, a genuinely respectable offense that plays way up at home, while the Nationals hand the ball to a rookie with real command questions.

Andrew Alvarez has thrown 49 big-league innings with a 4.04 ERA, a 1.45 WHIP and 22 walks. Free passes are survivable at sea level. At Coors Field they are gasoline, because every walk becomes a runner in scoring position the moment anyone doubles into the gap, and Colorado's lineup doubles into the gap constantly at home. Washington sits at exactly 50-50 on the season, a coin-flip team playing at the hardest road venue in the sport, asked to win behind six-plus innings of rookie command baseball or hand things to a bullpen in the thin air.

Kyle Freeland is no ace, and we will get to him in the risk section, but the veteran lefty has spent his whole career navigating this ballpark and his splits at least point the right way, a 6.38 ERA at home against 8.28 on the road where he is 0-6. In a game the market itself projects as a twelve-run demolition derby, getting +107 on the home team in the sport's most extreme run environment is a price worth taking at a measured stake. A unit and a half on the Rockies moneyline at +107.

Diamondbacks Moneyline: Jeffrey Springs Is The Play

Some bets are about backing your own starter. This one is about attacking theirs. Jeffrey Springs is 3-9 with a 6.08 ERA and a 1.42 WHIP across 97.2 innings, and the underlying numbers are worse than the surface: he has allowed home runs at 2.65 per nine at home and 2.08 on the road, some of the most extreme gopher-ball rates of any regular starter in the league. Now he walks into Chase Field, a hitter-friendly building, dragging a 42-57 Athletics club that has lost fifteen more games than it has won.

Arizona counters with an unusual profile, rookie left-hander Mitch Bratt making just his third big-league appearance, which means the Diamondbacks are effectively running a bullpen-led game plan behind a short opening stint. Normally that would scare me off a -154 price. It does not here, because the bet is not that Arizona's pitching dominates, it is that the 51-48 Diamondbacks at home outscore a Springs start, and Springs starts have been reliably generous all season. Nine of his twelve decisions are losses for a reason.

The offensive gap is thinner than the other sides on this card, Arizona hitting .237 to the Athletics' .246, so let me be precise about the shape of the edge: it is Springs' home-run problem meeting a ballpark that rewards fly balls, backed by home field and a bullpen that Arizona can deploy aggressively behind Bratt on a night where the manager has no reason to stretch anyone. The market has this at -163 in places, so -154 is the right side of the number. Two and a half units on the Diamondbacks moneyline at -154.

Rays-Blue Jays NRFI: Martinez Owns The First Inning

The fourth play needs exactly one inning of good pitching from two arms having excellent seasons. Start with the Tampa Bay side of the equation, because it is close to automatic: Nick Martinez has a 1.00 ERA in the first inning this season, allowing two runs total across 18 first frames with a 0.89 WHIP and a .185 opponent average in the opening inning. The veteran right-hander is 8-2 with a 2.65 ERA on the season, a strike-thrower who has walked 19 men all year, and his entire profile is built on getting ahead early and letting a contact-heavy defense work.

Toronto's first inning belongs to Dylan Cease, who is striking out 14.29 batters per nine at Rogers Centre with a .196 home opponent average and a 2.56 ERA on the season. The swing-and-miss stuff plays especially well against a Rays lineup that, for all its contact skills, still has to open the game against a right-hander whose fastball-slider combination has generated 148 strikeouts in 98.1 innings. On the other side of the opening frame, the Blue Jays are hitting .242 as a team with a .687 OPS, an offense that has scored barely four runs a game all season, and they get first crack at a Martinez first inning that has been shut down almost all season.

Cease's first innings have been the shakier of the two, and the risk section will put numbers on that. But at -134, this NRFI asks for one zero from a pitcher who has been historically clean in the first, and one zero from a strikeout monster at home against a below-average offense. A unit and a half on the Rays-Blue Jays NRFI at -134.

What Can Beat It

Honest accounting, play by play. The Brewers bet dies if Misiorowski has his first genuinely bad home start of the season or if Peralta's home-road split flips for one night, and at -190 a single upset stings twice as much. The Rockies play carries the most obvious flaw on the card: Kyle Freeland is 2-8 with a 7.36 ERA, and if the Nationals' lineup, which has scored 545 runs with 144 homers and is comfortably the more productive offense this season, jumps him early, the plus-money price will not matter. That is precisely why the stake is 1.5 units and not three.

The Diamondbacks play can lose two ways: Springs pitches his best game in months, or Arizona's bullpen-led plan behind a third-appearance rookie unravels before the offense gets to Springs. And the NRFI has a real pressure point, because Cease has allowed seven first-inning runs across 17 first frames, a 3.18 first-inning ERA with two homers. His stuff misses bats, but when he leaks early it tends to leak loud. Tampa Bay's .257 team average is the best of any lineup in that game, and one first-pitch ambush ends the bet in four minutes. Every number above is priced into the stakes. The reads stand.

The Bottom Line

Four plays, 8.5 units, one organizing idea: bet the mound gaps. The Brewers moneyline at -190 is the flagship at three units, Misiorowski's 1.62 ERA against a .234 lineup. The Rockies moneyline at +107 is a unit and a half of calculated chaos at Coors, where a 12-run total turns the game into a lineup fight Colorado can win at home. The Diamondbacks moneyline at -154 is two and a half units against Jeffrey Springs and his 6.08 ERA. And the Rays-Blue Jays NRFI at -134 is a unit and a half on Nick Martinez's 1.00 first-inning ERA and Dylan Cease's home strikeout stuff buying one clean opening frame. Monday boards this deep do not come around often. When the mismatches line up, bet them.
