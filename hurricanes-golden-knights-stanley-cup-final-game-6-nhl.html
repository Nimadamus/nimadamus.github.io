Hurricanes At Golden Knights, Game 6: Carolina One Win From The Cup

Carolina pushed its Stanley Cup Final lead to 3-2 and now heads back to Las Vegas with a chance to clinch the franchise's second championship. Vegas, behind Mitch Marner, faces elimination on home ice and must win to force a Game 7. Puck drop is 8:00 PM ET on Sunday at T-Mobile Arena. Analysis and breakdown below, no picks.

The 2026 Stanley Cup Final reaches a potential closeout in Las Vegas, with the Carolina Hurricanes holding a 3-2 series lead and one win from raising the Cup. Carolina won Game 5 to take control of a Final that has swung back and forth all spring, and now the Hurricanes get a chance to finish it on the road at T-Mobile Arena. Vegas faces elimination on home ice and has to win to force a Game 7 back in Raleigh. The Hurricanes are listed near a slim road favorite at minus-115 with the total set at 5.5, a number this offense-heavy series has repeatedly tested.

How Carolina Took Control

This Final has been a wild, high-event affair from the opening puck. Vegas took Game 1 by a 5-4 score, Carolina answered with a 4-3 overtime win in Game 2 on a Seth Jarvis power-play goal, and the Golden Knights reclaimed the edge with a 5-4 double-overtime epic in Game 3. Facing the threat of a 3-1 hole, Carolina flipped the script in Game 4 with a 5-3 win to even the series, then seized the lead with a Game 5 victory to move ahead 3-2. Through five games the teams have traded blown leads, overtime swings, and special-teams haymakers, and the offense has rarely stopped. Now Carolina carries that momentum into a building where it can clinch.

Marner And The Vegas Engine

The story of the series remains Mitch Marner, who left Toronto for Vegas and has authored the best postseason of his life. Marner scored the fastest hat trick in Stanley Cup Final history in Game 3, three goals in just over six minutes, and he has driven the Golden Knights offense all spring. He has help: Jack Eichel has been a setup machine down the middle, Shea Theodore has produced from the back end including the Game 3 double-overtime winner, Pavel Dorofeyev has been a consistent finisher, and Tomas Hertl has chipped in big goals. Facing elimination at home, Vegas will lean on that group to generate the offense it needs to extend the series.

Carolina's Depth Carries A Quiet Top Line

The fascinating wrinkle for Carolina is that its biggest names have been relatively quiet and the Hurricanes are a win from the Cup anyway. The top line of Sebastian Aho, Jarvis, and Andrei Svechnikov has been checked hard by Vegas, yet Carolina has stayed in front because its depth keeps producing. Taylor Hall, Jackson Blake, and Logan Stankoven have all been factors throughout the postseason, and Carolina's bottom six has been a difference-maker. If the Hurricanes are going to close this out, the formula is the same one that built the lead: secondary scoring keeps coming, and the Aho line breaking through against a Vegas defense that has smothered it would be the finishing touch.

The Carolina Crease

Goaltending was the lingering question for Carolina earlier in the Final after Frederik Andersen was pulled in back-to-back games, but the Hurricanes steadied the position to take Games 4 and 5. Andersen has been Carolina's man all postseason, and the recent wins bought the crease real confidence heading into a closeout spot. Vegas has ridden Carter Hart through the entire playoff run, and Hart owns strong overall numbers, but this Carolina attack has tested him. In a series with a 5.5 total, the goalie who makes the timely save in the third period likely decides whether Carolina lifts the Cup or the series heads to a Game 7.

Special Teams And The Margins

In a series this tight, special teams are the difference. Carolina has leaned on one of the best penalty kills among the Final teams, a unit that has repeatedly bailed the Hurricanes out, but the power play has been the glaring weakness, and a club that cannot score with the man advantage in a one-goal series is leaving wins on the table. Vegas owns the more dangerous power play and has cashed big man-advantage goals in this series, though its penalty kill has been stretched as Carolina's chances have piled up. Jarvis's Game 2 overtime winner came on the power play, proof that even a cold unit can swing a game in a single moment. Discipline and finishing on the man advantage may decide Game 6.

How They Got To The Final

These two arrived very differently. Vegas was a wrecking ball through the first three rounds before reaching the Final, the kind of dominant run that builds a roster's belief. Carolina took the harder road, surviving a grind of an Eastern Conference Final to reach its first Stanley Cup Final since the franchise's lone championship in 2006. Now Carolina is the team holding the lead and the Golden Knights are the ones facing elimination, a reversal from the way the series began. The Hurricanes have earned the chance to close it out on the road, and a young, desperate Vegas group has to defend home ice to keep its season breathing.

Final Thoughts

Game 6 is a potential Cup-clincher for Carolina and a must-win for Vegas, and everything about the first five games, all decided by a goal or two with overtime swings and traded leads, points to another tight, high-event affair. The slim moneyline and a 5.5 total reflect a series that has been close and goal-heavy throughout. Carolina has the lead, the momentum, and a depth that keeps producing; Vegas has Marner and the desperation of an elimination game on home ice. Puck drop is 8:00 PM ET on Sunday at T-Mobile Arena, and there is no reason to expect anything other than another thriller.
