11-Game Sunday Slate

NBA March 1, 2026

Sunday delivers a loaded 11-game slate headlined by three national TV showdowns. The Spurs bring an 11-game win streak into Madison Square Garden on ABC. Identical-record Timberwolves and Nuggets collide in Denver on ABC. The 76ers visit the Celtics on NBC for Sunday Night Basketball. The league-leading Pistons carry their five-game road winning streak into Orlando. SGA and the Thunder lay a massive number in Dallas. Luka and the Lakers host the league's worst team in Sacramento.

Spurs @ Knicks

Sunday, 1:00 PM ET | Madison Square Garden, New York, NY
Spread
SA -1.5
Moneyline
SA -124 / NYK +106
Total
O/U 228.5

This is the game of the day, and it's not particularly close. San Antonio at 43-16 rolls into Madison Square Garden riding an 11-game win streak and a perfect 11-0 February, and the Spurs look like the most unstoppable force in the NBA right now. Victor Wembanyama has been absolutely absurd, averaging 25.5 points, 10.8 rebounds, and 3.9 blocks per game this season, and his two-way dominance has turned San Antonio from a fun young team into a legitimate title contender that nobody wants to see in a seven-game series. But it's the addition of De'Aaron Fox that has truly unlocked this roster. Fox is averaging 25.2 points and 6.2 assists per game since arriving from Sacramento, and his speed and aggression in the pick-and-roll with Wemby has created the most terrifying offensive combination in basketball.

New York at 38-22 is a genuinely good team, but the Knicks are dealing with some key injury concerns heading into this one. OG Anunoby is doubtful with a hip issue, and Mitchell Robinson remains out with a foot injury, which significantly weakens their interior defense and rebounding. That's a problem when you're going up against Wembanyama, who will exploit any weakness at the rim with his 7'4" frame and otherworldly touch around the basket. Jalen Brunson continues to be spectacular, averaging 26.7 points per game, and Karl-Anthony Towns has been a revelation at center with his 20.1 points and 11.8 rebounds. Those two give New York the offensive firepower to hang with anyone, and the MSG crowd on a Sunday afternoon ABC game will be rocking.

Here's what makes this matchup so fascinating: the Spurs have the best defense in the league during their win streak, and their ability to switch everything with Wembanyama lurking as the help defender creates a wall that most offenses can't penetrate. Brunson has been brilliant at navigating tough defenses all season, but San Antonio's length and athleticism present a unique challenge that's different from anything else he faces on a nightly basis. The Knicks can't afford to go small against Wemby, and they can't afford to stay big against Fox's speed. It's a tactical nightmare for Tom Thibodeau.

The 228.5 total suggests a competitive, well-paced game, and the SA -1.5 spread tells you Vegas sees this as essentially a coin flip with a slight edge to the team riding the hottest streak in basketball. Devin Vassell is day-to-day with a knee issue for the Spurs, which could thin their perimeter rotation slightly, but this roster is so deep right now that they've been able to absorb absences without missing a beat all month. The Knicks need Anunoby to play to have any shot at matching San Antonio's defensive versatility, and even then, stopping a Fox-Wembanyama pick-and-roll combination is something that no team in basketball has figured out over the last 11 games.

Cavaliers @ Nets

Sunday, 3:30 PM ET | Barclays Center, Brooklyn, NY
Spread
CLE -11.5
Moneyline
CLE -538 / BKN +398
Total
O/U 222.5

Cleveland at 37-24 has been one of the more interesting teams in the league since the James Harden acquisition from the Clippers, and Sunday's trip to Barclays Center should give the Cavaliers a chance to continue building chemistry in what amounts to a glorified scrimmage against the 15-44 Nets. Harden has given Cleveland a secondary playmaker alongside Donovan Mitchell that they desperately needed, and his ability to run the pick-and-roll with Evan Mobley has added a dimension to Cleveland's offense that makes them significantly more dangerous in half-court sets. Mitchell can now play off the ball more, which has actually improved his efficiency because he's getting cleaner looks rather than having to manufacture offense from scratch every possession.

Brooklyn is in full tear-down mode, and the arrival of Michael Porter Jr. from Denver hasn't changed the trajectory of what is clearly a rebuilding season. MPJ can score, and he's shown flashes of the talent that made him a franchise-altering prospect coming out of high school, but he's essentially operating as a volume scorer on a team that has no infrastructure around him to make those individual performances meaningful. The Nets' defense is a sieve, their ball movement is nonexistent, and their best young players are still trying to figure out how to compete at this level. It's a development year for Brooklyn, and games like this one against a legitimately good Cavaliers team are just part of the process.

The CLE -11.5 spread and -538 moneyline reflect the massive talent gap between these two rosters, and it's hard to argue against the number. Cleveland should control this game from start to finish, and the only question is whether they come out with enough energy and focus in an early afternoon game to cover a big spread against a team that everyone knows is tanking. The 222.5 total is on the lower side, which makes sense given Brooklyn's tendency to play at a crawling pace when they don't have the talent to run with better teams. Cleveland's defense, anchored by Mobley's incredible versatility and rim protection, should keep the Nets well below their season average in scoring.

The real story for Cleveland is how quickly Harden has integrated into this roster. He's not the James Harden of 2018, but he doesn't need to be. What he gives this team is a steadying hand, a guy who can run the offense for 12-15 minutes when Mitchell sits, and a threat from three that opens up driving lanes for everyone else on the floor. Against a Nets team that can't defend the pick-and-roll, Harden should feast, and the Cavaliers' depth advantage is going to wear Brooklyn down as the game goes on. This one could get ugly early.

O/U 228.5

Two of the most ravaged rosters in basketball meet in Chicago on Sunday afternoon, and it's wild that Milwaukee at 26-32 is somehow a 3.5-point favorite on the road despite not having either of its franchise cornerstones. Giannis Antetokounmpo has been out since January 23 with a calf strain, and Damian Lillard is done for the entire season after tearing his Achilles and subsequently being waived. That's both of Milwaukee's stars gone, which should've turned the Bucks into one of the worst teams in the league. Instead, they've gone 7-3 over their last 10 games, a stretch that defies every logical expectation and speaks to the effort and coaching that has kept this team competitive against all odds.

Chicago at 24-36 is in a similar boat, albeit for different reasons. The Bulls shipped Nikola Vucevic to Boston at the trade deadline and have committed fully to a rebuild centered around developing their young core. Coby White has become the de facto leader of this group, and he's playing with a freedom and confidence that he never showed when he was sharing touches with more established veterans. But White isn't a franchise player, and the rest of Chicago's roster is a collection of young, inconsistent pieces who are still learning how to play winning basketball at this level. The United Center hasn't had much to cheer about this season, and Sunday's matinee against a Bucks team missing its stars isn't exactly must-see TV.

What makes this game interesting is Milwaukee's remarkable resilience. The Bucks have found production from unexpected sources during this stretch, with role players stepping into expanded roles and performing well beyond what anyone anticipated. Their defense has been the driving force behind the 7-3 run, as the collective effort and energy on that end has compensated for the massive talent void left by Giannis and Lillard. Against a Chicago team that doesn't have a dominant scorer who can punish that kind of effort-based defense, Milwaukee's approach should continue to work.

The 228.5 total is surprisingly high for a game between two decimated rosters, but both teams play at a relatively quick pace and neither has the defensive personnel to consistently generate stops in the half court. Milwaukee's MIL -3.5 spread feels about right, reflecting the road-team disadvantage against a home squad that, talent-wise, isn't meaningfully better. This game is going to come down to which coaching staff does a better job of maximizing limited resources, and right now Milwaukee's track record over the last 10 games suggests they've figured out something that's working.

Timberwolves @ Nuggets

Sunday, 3:30 PM ET | Ball Arena, Denver, CO
Spread
DEN -2.5
Moneyline
DEN -150 / MIN +125

O/U 237.5

This is must-watch basketball. Minnesota at 37-23 and Denver at 37-23 have identical records entering Sunday's ABC showdown at Ball Arena, and the implications of this game extend well beyond a single regular-season win. These two teams are battling for seeding in a loaded Western Conference where the difference between the 3-seed and the 6-seed could mean facing the Pistons or Thunder in the second round instead of a more manageable first-round opponent. Nikola Jokic continues to put up numbers that look like a video game glitch, averaging over 28 points, 12 rebounds, and 10 assists per game, which means he's posting a legitimate triple-double average across an entire NBA season. That's not something that human beings are supposed to be able to do.

Anthony Edwards has been the driving force behind Minnesota's ascent into serious contender status, averaging north of 27 points per game with the kind of explosive athleticism and swagger that makes him appointment television every single night. Edwards' ability to get to the rim and finish through contact, combined with his improving three-point shot, has turned him into one of the five or six best players in basketball. Julius Randle has given the Wolves a secondary scoring option who can operate in the mid-range and post, and Rudy Gobert continues to be the defensive anchor who turns Minnesota's scheme into something that opponents dread facing. The Wolves' defense, built around Gobert's rim protection and the team's length on the perimeter, is one of the best in the league and will be tested by Jokic's passing wizardry.

Denver's home-court advantage at altitude is a real factor that doesn't show up in the box score. Teams consistently struggle in the thin air of Ball Arena, especially in the second half when fatigue sets in and legs get heavy. Jamal Murray's health is always a question mark, but when he's on the floor, his ability to create off the dribble and hit contested shots in the mid-range gives Denver a second creator who can punish defenses that collapse on Jokic. The Nuggets' offense when Jokic is orchestrating is a thing of beauty, with constant motion, back-cuts, and the threat of Jokic's no-look passes keeping defenses on a string.

The DEN -2.5 spread essentially prices in home-court advantage, which means the market sees these two teams as dead even on a neutral floor. That feels exactly right. The 237.5 total is the highest on the board and reflects two offenses that are capable of putting up 120+ on any given night. Edwards vs. Jokic is one of the premier individual matchups in the entire NBA, a clash between the league's most athletic wing and its most skilled center, and the national TV audience on ABC is in for a treat. This game has playoff intensity written all over it, and the outcome could ripple through the Western Conference standings for the rest of the season.

O/U 237.5

If you were looking for a game featuring two of the most injury-devastated rosters in basketball, congratulations, you found it. Memphis at 22-36 is playing without Ja Morant, who's been out with a UCL sprain in his elbow, and they shipped Desmond Bane to Orlando at the trade deadline, which removed their other primary scoring option from the equation entirely. Indiana at 15-45 owns the worst record in the Eastern Conference and has been without Tyrese Haliburton since his season-ending Achilles injury. These are two teams that entered the year with legitimate playoff aspirations and have watched those dreams evaporate through a combination of devastating injuries and unfortunate timing.

The Grizzlies are basically running a roster of role players and developmental pieces at this point, and the results have been predictably inconsistent. Without Morant's ability to create offense out of nothing and Bane's shot-making from the perimeter, Memphis' offense has become stagnant and predictable. They can still compete on the defensive end because effort and toughness have always been embedded in the Grizzlies' DNA, but generating enough points to win games against anybody has been a nightly struggle. Zach Edey has missed significant time as well with a foot issue, further thinning an already depleted rotation.

Indiana's situation is arguably even more dire. The Pacers are on pace for one of the worst seasons in recent Eastern Conference history, and Haliburton's Achilles injury during the 2025 Finals was the domino that set everything in motion. Without their franchise point guard, Indiana's offense has no reliable creator, and the supporting cast that looked so dynamic around Haliburton has been exposed as one-dimensional without his playmaking. The Pacers are playing hard because that's what professional athletes do, but the talent simply isn't there to compete against anyone consistently.

The MEM -1 spread is essentially a pick'em with a slight nod to the visitors, and the -113/+113 moneyline confirms that this game is about as close to a toss-up as you'll find on any NBA slate. The 237.5 total is notably high, which suggests the market expects zero defense from either team, and frankly that feels about right. Neither Memphis nor Indiana has the personnel to slow anyone down consistently, and games between depleted rosters tend to produce a lot of sloppy, fast-paced possessions that inflate scoring. This is a battle of two broken teams, and the entertainment value here comes from watching which young players step up and make a name for themselves in a game with zero pressure.

Atlanta at 30-31 and Portland at 29-31 are two teams sitting right on the bubble of the play-in tournament, and this game has real implications for both franchises' postseason hopes. The Hawks made the seismic decision to trade Trae Young to Washington, fundamentally altering the identity of this team from a Young-centric spread offense to a more balanced, defense-first approach. Jalen Johnson has emerged as the go-to option in Young's absence, and the Hawks have actually been better defensively since the trade, with Dyson Daniels providing the kind of perimeter stopper that Atlanta hasn't had in years. The question is whether the Hawks' new identity generates enough offense to beat teams consistently.

Portland's season has been a mixed bag of encouraging developmental growth and frustrating inconsistency. Scoot Henderson is the centerpiece of everything Portland is building toward, and his development has been the most important storyline of the Blazers' season. Henderson's court vision and ability to get to the rim have improved noticeably, though his shooting from three remains a work in progress. Anfernee Simons provides the perimeter scoring punch that keeps defenses honest, and Deandre Ayton gives Portland a physical presence inside who can score in the post and grab boards. It's a roster that has talent, but it doesn't always play together with the consistency you need to string wins together.

The ATL -6 spread is notable because it suggests the market sees Atlanta as meaningfully better at home despite the near-identical records. That's likely a reflection of the Hawks' home-court advantage at State Farm Arena, where their energy and defensive effort tend to spike in front of their own crowd. Daniels' defense will make life difficult for whoever he's guarding on Portland's perimeter, and Atlanta's overall length and athleticism should create problems for a Trail Blazers team that relies heavily on mid-range scoring and driving to the basket.

The 238.5 total is the second-highest on Sunday's board, and it paints a picture of two teams that are going to get up and down the floor and trade baskets in a game that could easily turn into a shootout. Neither team has elite defensive personnel, and both offenses are built around attacking the rim and generating easy looks in transition. Portland's ability to keep pace with Atlanta's athleticism will determine whether the Blazers can stay within the number, but on the road against a team that's playing with renewed purpose and identity since the Young trade, covering 6 points is a tall order for a Blazers team that's been inconsistent on the road all season.

The best team in basketball takes its show on the road to Orlando, and the Pistons at 44-14 just keep finding ways to remind everyone that this season's transformation isn't a fluke. Detroit owns the NBA's best record, carries a five-game road winning streak into this one, and has been playing the kind of suffocating defense that makes them a nightmare for every team on the schedule. Cade Cunningham has cemented himself as a genuine MVP candidate, averaging 25.3 points, 9.8 assists, and 5.8 rebounds per game, and his growth from promising young talent to franchise cornerstone has been the best individual development story in basketball this season. Cunningham doesn't just score, he orchestrates, he defends, and he elevates everyone around him with his basketball IQ and court vision.

Orlando at 31-27 has been rejuvenated by the acquisition of Desmond Bane from Memphis, giving the Magic an elite perimeter scorer to pair alongside Franz Wagner and Paolo Banchero. Bane's ability to shoot off the catch, create off the dribble, and play winning basketball on both ends of the floor has added a dimension to Orlando's offense that they were desperately missing. Wagner and Banchero continue to be the foundation of everything the Magic do, but adding Bane gives them a third legitimate scoring option who can punish teams for over-helping on drives to the basket. It's a roster that on paper should be competing for home-court advantage in the playoffs.

The challenge for Orlando is that Detroit's defense is built to shut down exactly the kind of offense the Magic run. The Pistons switch everything, contest every three-pointer, and protect the rim at an elite level, and their defensive versatility means there's no single matchup that opposing teams can exploit. Wagner's drives to the basket will meet resistance at the rim, Banchero's post-ups will be contested by length and physicality, and Bane's catch-and-shoot opportunities will come with a hand in his face. Detroit makes every shot difficult, and that consistency on the defensive end is the primary reason they've been able to maintain the league's best record despite not having a transcendent superstar in the way that teams like OKC or San Antonio do.

The DET -6 spread on the road says a lot about how the market views these two teams. It's unusual for a road team to be laying 6 points against a team with a winning record, but that's the respect Detroit has earned with its 44-14 season. The 220.5 total is the lowest on Sunday's board, reflecting two defense-oriented teams that are going to grind this game out in the half court. Detroit's pace of play has been deliberately controlled all season, and the Pistons are at their best when they force opponents into a slower, more physical style that neutralizes transition scoring and fast-break points. Orlando will need to find offense in the half court against one of the league's best defenses, and that's a challenge that even good teams have struggled to meet this year.

Thunder @ Mavericks

Sunday, 8:00 PM ET | American Airlines Center, Dallas, TX
Spread
OKC -15.5
Moneyline
OKC -1111 / DAL +740

O/U 231.5

Fifteen-and-a-half points. That's the number the market has attached to this game, and honestly, it might not be enough. Oklahoma City at 46-15 has been the best team in the Western Conference all season, and Shai Gilgeous-Alexander has been playing at an MVP level that deserves to be discussed alongside the very best individual seasons in recent NBA history. SGA is averaging north of 33 points per game, and his combination of mid-range mastery, driving ability, and newfound three-point consistency has made him virtually unguardable. When SGA decides he's going to score, there isn't a defender in the league who can stop him one-on-one, and the Thunder's supporting cast has been good enough to ensure that double teams come with a heavy price.

Dallas at 21-38 has been gutted by injuries, trades, and bad luck to the point where the roster is nearly unrecognizable from the team that was supposed to contend this season. Kyrie Irving is done for the year with a knee injury, Cooper Flagg, the number one overall pick, is out with a foot issue, and Anthony Davis, who was acquired from the Lakers, has been limited or injured since arriving. The Mavericks are essentially playing out the string with a roster of replacement-level players and developmental pieces, and the nightly results have been about what you'd expect from a team missing its three best players. Dallas has been competitive in spots this season, but the talent gap against a fully healthy Thunder squad is enormous.

Chet Holmgren continues to evolve alongside SGA into one of the most dangerous two-man combinations in the NBA. Holmgren's shot-blocking and rim protection give the Thunder an elite defensive anchor, and his ability to step out to the three-point line on offense creates spacing that opens up driving lanes for SGA and the rest of OKC's perimeter players. The Thunder's defense, even during stretches when they've been missing key rotation pieces, has remained among the best in the league because the system Mark Daigneault has built is bigger than any individual player. Everyone buys in, everyone switches, everyone helps, and the result is a defensive machine that smothers opponents.

The OKC -15.5 spread and -1111 moneyline are some of the most lopsided numbers you'll see in any NBA regular season game, and they're entirely justified by the disparity between these two rosters. The 231.5 total suggests a game where Oklahoma City scores at will while Dallas manages to put up some respectable numbers in garbage time. There's really no competitive angle to this game unless the Thunder decide to rest their starters early and give the bench extended run, in which case Dallas' scrappy group might keep things within 20. But if SGA and Holmgren play their normal minutes, this could get out of hand by halftime, and the only drama will be whether OKC covers the massive number.

Sunday Night Basketball delivers one of the NBA's oldest and most heated rivalries, and even with both teams missing their franchise players, the 76ers at Celtics still carries weight. Philadelphia at 33-26 will once again be without Joel Embiid, who's dealing with an oblique issue that's kept him sidelined, meaning Tyrese Maxey continues to carry the entire offensive burden for the Sixers. And Maxey has been absolutely phenomenal in that role, averaging north of 30 points per game this season and playing with the kind of fearless aggression that has elevated him from elite secondary option to legitimate franchise player. He attacks the rim with reckless abandon, his three-point shot has become one of the most reliable in the league, and his ability to draw fouls keeps him at the free-throw line where he's been nearly automatic.

Boston at 39-20 has navigated the devastating loss of Jayson Tatum to a season-ending Achilles injury with remarkable composure, and Jaylen Brown deserves an enormous amount of credit for the way he's responded. Brown is averaging over 29 points per game and has been the best two-way wing in basketball this season, defending the opponent's best perimeter threat every night while carrying the offensive load that used to be split between him and Tatum. Kristaps Porzingis has been the ideal complement to Brown, providing floor spacing with his three-point shooting and rim protection on the defensive end, and Jrue Holiday's steady hand as the secondary ball handler has kept Boston's offense humming despite the absence of their biggest star. Nikola Vucevic, acquired from Chicago at the deadline, has added veteran depth at the center position.

The BOS -8.5 spread is significant for a rivalry game, but it reflects the reality that Philadelphia without Embiid simply doesn't have the interior presence to match up with what Boston brings to the table. Maxey can score 35 and Philly could still lose by double digits because the Celtics' defense is designed to funnel everything to the rim, where Porzingis is waiting as the help defender. Without Embiid to punish Boston's bigs in the post and demand double teams, the Celtics can commit more resources to slowing down Maxey, which makes Philly's offense even more one-dimensional than it already is without their MVP-caliber center.

TD Garden on a Sunday night is one of the toughest environments in the NBA, and the Celtics' home crowd has been especially intense this season as they've rallied around a team that could've packed it in after the Tatum injury but instead chose to compete for a championship. The 221.5 total is on the lower side, which makes sense given two teams that prioritize defense and play at a controlled pace. Boston's defensive identity hasn't changed despite the roster turnover, and Philadelphia's defense, while not elite, has been solid enough this season to keep games from turning into shootouts. This is going to be a physical, grind-it-out rivalry game where every possession matters, and the national TV audience on NBC is getting a contest that should feel a lot closer than the 8.5-point spread might suggest, at least until the fourth quarter.

Ivica Zubac anchors the middle with his rebounding and screen-setting, but the overall question for LA is whether this collection of solid-but-not-spectacular players can generate enough offense to be competitive down the stretch of the season without a true lead creator.

New Orleans at 18-42 has had one of the most disappointing seasons in the NBA, and at this point, the Pelicans are just trying to get to the offseason without further catastrophe. Zion Williamson's availability has been a season-long concern, and CJ McCollum has been the steadiest hand on a roster that has lacked consistent health and production from its highest-paid players. The Pelicans have the talent, on paper, to be much better than their record suggests, but injuries and a lack of cohesion have turned what was supposed to be a playoff push into a lottery-bound campaign that's been painful for everyone involved.

The LAC -8 spread reflects the Clippers' home-court advantage at Intuit Dome and the gap between a middling team with solid pieces and a team that's been one of the worst in the league all season. The Clippers' defense has been their calling card, and their ability to switch and contest shots on the perimeter makes life difficult for teams that rely on perimeter creation. New Orleans' offense has sputtered for most of the season, and their inability to consistently get stops on the other end means they're often playing from behind and forced to press offensively, which leads to turnovers and easy transition points for the opposition.

The 225.5 total is on the lower end of Sunday's board, reflecting two teams that don't play at a particularly fast pace and tend to grind games out in the half court. The Clippers' defense will be the difference here, and Powell's ability to get buckets in isolation and off screens gives LA a reliable scoring option that New Orleans doesn't have an answer for defensively. This game isn't going to draw much attention on a Sunday night filled with better matchups, but the Clippers need every win they can get in the play-in race, and their desperation should translate into effort that overwhelms a Pelicans team playing out the clock on a lost season.

Sacramento's season has been nothing short of catastrophic. At 14-47, the Kings own the worst record in the entire NBA, and it's hard to overstate how thoroughly this roster has been dismantled. De'Aaron Fox was traded to San Antonio, where he's become an All-Star alongside Wembanyama. Domantas Sabonis is done for the season with a torn meniscus. Zach LaVine, who came over from Chicago, is out for the season after hand surgery. That's three players who were supposed to be the core of this team, and all three are either gone or watching from the sideline. What's left in Sacramento is a collection of young players and replacement-level veterans who are simply trying to develop and improve in the midst of what has been a historically bad season.

Los Angeles at 34-24 is in a completely different universe, and the Luka Doncic experience continues to be the most entertaining show in the Western Conference. Doncic is averaging 32.8 points per game since being traded from Dallas in February 2025, and his ability to dominate games from the perimeter with his step-back three, his post-ups against smaller defenders, and his vision in the pick-and-roll has transformed the Lakers into a legitimate contender. LeBron James, at age 41, continues to defy every law of nature and biology, providing production and leadership that would be remarkable for a player in his prime, let alone someone in his 23rd NBA season. The combination of Doncic's individual brilliance and LeBron's basketball IQ gives the Lakers two of the smartest offensive players in the history of the sport.

The LAL -13 spread is enormous, and the -837 moneyline means you'd have to risk $837 just to win $100 on the Lakers, which tells you everything about how the market views this matchup. Sacramento doesn't have a single player who can credibly guard Doncic one-on-one, doesn't have the rim protection to deter his drives to the basket, and doesn't have the offensive talent to keep pace with a Lakers team that can score from every level of the floor. This is going to be a mismatch from the opening tip, and the Kings' best hope is that the Lakers get bored and take their foot off the gas in the second half.

The 233.5 total indicates the market expects a lot of scoring, which makes sense when one team has two of the best offensive players in basketball and the other team doesn't have the defensive personnel to stop anyone. The Lakers' offense with Doncic running the show is a machine that generates open looks for shooters, creates driving lanes to the basket, and punishes teams for every mistake with ruthless efficiency. Sacramento, to their credit, plays with effort and competitiveness despite the record, but effort doesn't close a talent gap this wide. The Kings' young players will get their opportunities to impress in what amounts to a developmental exercise against one of the best offenses in the league, but the final score is going to reflect the reality that this is the worst team in basketball visiting one of the better ones.
