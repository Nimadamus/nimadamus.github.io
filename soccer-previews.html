Thomas Tuchel's England ran the table in World Cup qualifying, winning all eight matches in UEFA Group A to stamp their ticket to the 2026 tournament in emphatic fashion. Now comes the fine-tuning. This friendly at Wembley is the first real opportunity for Tuchel to experiment with his squad depth, and that's exactly what he plans to do. Harry Kane, Bukayo Saka, and Declan Rice are all being rested, which opens the door for fringe players to state their case for a World Cup roster spot. Harry Maguire and Ben White are back in the fold, giving Tuchel a chance to evaluate defensive combinations that could prove crucial when the real pressure arrives next summer.

Uruguay present a legitimate test despite this being a friendly. Marcelo Bielsa's side sits 17th in the FIFA rankings and boasts a squad brimming with elite club talent. Darwin Nunez continues to terrorize defenders at Liverpool, Federico Valverde remains one of the most complete midfielders in world football at Real Madrid, and Ronald Araujo anchors Barcelona's backline when fit. La Celeste always bring intensity to international fixtures, and Bielsa's tactical blueprint guarantees pressing and aggression regardless of the occasion. They won't treat this as a kickabout.

The tactical intrigue here centers on what Tuchel wants to learn. Without his first-choice spine, England will likely line up with a midfield that could feature Conor Gallagher, Morgan Gibbs-White, or Angel Gomes, all players fighting for World Cup inclusion. The back line reshuffling with Maguire and White's returns creates a different dynamic than what Tuchel has relied upon in qualifying. This is a rare chance to stress-test Plan B and Plan C before the stakes skyrocket. How England handle Uruguay's pressing without Rice dictating tempo will reveal a lot about the squad's overall depth.

For Uruguay, the motivation is straightforward. They need competitive minutes against a top-tier European opponent to calibrate their own World Cup preparations. Bielsa will want to see his pressing triggers work against English buildup play, and the interplay between Nunez and the midfield runners behind him is always fascinating to watch. The over 2.5 goals line at -108 reflects the expectation that England's attacking depth, even without Kane and Saka, should generate chances at Wembley, while Uruguay's counter-attacking quality makes them dangerous on the break. This has the makings of an entertaining, open contest despite the friendly label.

This is a celebration disguised as a football match. Norway have qualified for the World Cup for the first time since 1998, and the entire nation is riding an emotional high that makes this friendly far more meaningful than the label suggests. The fact that Erling Haaland is being rested tells you everything about Norway's priorities: protect the crown jewel, let others audition, and keep the momentum rolling into the summer. Alexander Isak will lead the line instead, and the Newcastle striker has been in blistering form at club level, making this a fascinating audition for how Norway's attack functions without its talisman.

Ronald Koeman's Netherlands topped World Cup qualifying Group G without a loss, and the Johan Cruijff Arena will be rocking for this one. The Oranje have their own issues to sort out, though. Frenkie de Jong remains unavailable, which continues to be a nagging concern for Koeman's midfield construction. Without de Jong's progressive passing and press resistance, the Netherlands have had to rely more heavily on the creative output of Memphis Depay, Cody Gakpo, and the electrifying Xavi Simons. Virgil van Dijk marshals the defense, and his leadership at the back gives Koeman the platform to push bodies forward.

The BTTS line at -150 is telling. Both sides have the attacking quality to find the net, and friendlies tend to open up in the second half as substitutions disrupt defensive structure. Koeman will almost certainly use this match to cycle through his squad, testing different combinations in 45-minute blocks. Norway's approach under Stale Solbakken has been fearless in recent windows, and they won't sit back just because they're in Amsterdam. Isak's movement between the lines could cause problems for a Dutch defense that occasionally switches off in transition.

What makes this fixture genuinely compelling is the World Cup context for both sides. The Netherlands are trying to build cohesion and establish their best XI before the tournament, while Norway are trying to prove that their qualification wasn't a fluke. Every minute of competitive football matters when you're building something for the biggest stage. The atmosphere at the Johan Cruijff Arena should be electric, with both sets of fans in high spirits, and the quality on the pitch, even without Haaland, should produce plenty of talking points for scouts and managers watching from the stands.

Here's a stat that should jump off the page: the last three meetings between Switzerland and Germany have ALL ended in draws. That's not a coincidence, it's a pattern. These two nations play remarkably tight football against each other, and the +230 draw price reflects that history while still offering plenty of value. Murat Yakin's Switzerland topped their UEFA qualifying group unbeaten and have quietly become one of the most well-organized sides in European football. Granit Xhaka remains the heartbeat of the midfield, while Manuel Akanji's consistency at Manchester City has made him one of the most reliable center-backs on the continent.

Julian Nagelsmann brings a German squad to Basel that's dripping with young attacking talent. Florian Wirtz and Jamal Musiala represent the future of German football, and they're already playing at an elite level right now. Wirtz has been sensational for Bayer Leverkusen this season, and his ability to drift between the lines and create from nothing makes him nearly impossible to contain. Kai Havertz provides the physical presence up top, and the Arsenal forward has added a clinical edge to his game that makes Germany's attack genuinely frightening when it clicks. Germany are drawn in World Cup Group E and will want to arrive in peak form.

The BTTS line at -189 is heavily juiced for a reason. Switzerland have the quality to score at home, and Germany's attacking firepower makes a clean sheet unlikely for either side. The question is whether this friendly descends into the cagey, tight affair that's defined this rivalry recently, or whether both managers use it as an opportunity to let their attacking players off the leash. Nagelsmann has been building something with this group since taking over, and friendlies give him the chance to experiment with the high press and aggressive positioning that define his philosophy.

For Switzerland, this match is about confirming they belong in the conversation with Europe's heavyweights. They've proven themselves in tournament play repeatedly, reaching the quarterfinals at Euro 2020 and the Round of 16 at the 2022 World Cup. A strong showing against Germany in Basel would reinforce the belief within the camp that they can compete with anyone when the World Cup rolls around. Yakin will want his team to control possession, stay compact, and hit Germany on transitions, the same formula that's produced those three consecutive draws. Don't be surprised if history repeats itself.

Spain are the best team in the world right now, and the numbers back it up. FIFA's number one ranked nation and reigning Euro 2024 champions, La Roja have won four of their last five matches while scoring 18 goals and conceding just two. That's an absurd ratio that speaks to both the attacking brilliance and defensive solidity Luis de la Fuente has cultivated. The headline for this one is the return of Lamine Yamal, the teenage sensation who took Euro 2024 by storm and continues to develop at a pace that defies logic. Having him back in the squad adds another dimension to an attack that already features Pedri, Nico Williams, and a supporting cast that would start for virtually any other national team on the planet.

Serbia, by contrast, arrive in Villarreal with their World Cup dreams already dead. They failed to qualify for the 2026 tournament, which puts this friendly in a strange emotional context. On one hand, there's nothing tangible to play for. On the other, pride and professional motivation should drive a squad that includes Aleksandar Mitrovic, one of the most prolific strikers in world football, and Dusan Vlahovic, Juventus's main man up front. The talent is there individually, but the collective failures in qualifying have left Serbia's program at a crossroads. A new cycle needs to begin, and this match offers a glimpse of what direction that might take.

The -425 home price for Spain is steep but justified. When you're playing at the Estadio de la Ceramica in Villarreal, a ground that breeds technical, possession-dominant football perfectly suited to Spain's identity, and you're fielding a squad this talented, the outcome rarely surprises. De la Fuente has built a system where the sum is greater than the parts, though the individual parts are already world-class. Spain's press is relentless, their ball retention suffocating, and their transition play devastatingly fast. Serbia will struggle to see the ball for extended stretches.

What makes this fixture interesting from a World Cup preparation standpoint is how Spain manage their depth. De la Fuente doesn't need to prove anything about his first-choice XI. He needs to evaluate the players on the fringes, the ones who could become crucial when injuries or suspensions hit during a grueling summer tournament. Look for younger faces to get extended minutes in the second half, which could open the game up if Serbia sense an opportunity against Spain's reserves. But make no mistake, even Spain's B team would be competitive against most nations. The gulf in quality between these two sides right now is enormous.

This is a coronation, not a contest. Argentina, reigning World Cup champions and Copa America holders, host Mauritania at the iconic La Bombonera in Buenos Aires, and the only real question is the margin of victory. Lionel Scaloni's side has been the most dominant force in international football over the last three years, and the 2026 World Cup in North America represents what could be Lionel Messi's final chapter. The man will turn 39 during the tournament, and every friendly between now and then feels like another page in a farewell tour that the entire footballing world is watching with reverence.

The supporting cast around Messi has only gotten stronger. Lautaro Martinez continues to bang in goals at a remarkable rate for Inter Milan, Julian Alvarez provides the relentless work ethic and clinical finishing from his time at Atletico Madrid, and the midfield combination of Enzo Fernandez and Alexis Mac Allister gives Argentina both steel and silk in the middle of the park. This is a squad with depth at every position, built to compete in knockout football when the margins are razor thin. Against Mauritania, those qualities won't be tested, but the cohesion and rhythm that come from playing together will be valuable.

Mauritania, ranked 115th in the world, are winless in their last six fixtures and arrive in Buenos Aires facing a task that borders on the impossible. This is a team that has made strides in African football but simply doesn't have the individual quality or tactical infrastructure to compete with a side of Argentina's caliber. The -10000 moneyline tells the entire story: bookmakers can barely price this match because the expected outcome is so one-sided. Mauritania's objective will be purely about limiting damage and gaining the experience of playing against the best.

The real storyline here is the atmosphere. La Bombonera is one of the most intimidating and passionate venues in world football, and hosting a World Cup preparation friendly there gives the Argentine public a chance to celebrate their heroes before the squad heads north to defend their title. Scaloni will likely rotate heavily, giving fringe players a chance to impress against limited opposition while protecting his key men from unnecessary wear. The crowd will roar for every goal, every Messi touch, every moment that reminds them what this golden generation has accomplished. As a competitive football match, this is a total mismatch. As a spectacle and a World Cup send-off event in Buenos Aires, it's something special.
