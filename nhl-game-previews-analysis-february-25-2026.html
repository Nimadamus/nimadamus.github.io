8-Game Wednesday Slate

Hockey is back. The first night after the Olympics and 4 Nations Face-Off delivers an 8-game slate loaded with intrigue. Toronto visits Tampa Bay on TNT in an Atlantic Division showdown, Colorado brings the NHL's best record to Utah, and Connor McDavid chases another scoring title in Anaheim.

Game 1
TNT

Maple Leafs @ Lightning

Wednesday, 7:30 PM ET | Amalie Arena, Tampa, FL
Puck Line
TBL -1.5
Moneyline
TBL -225 / TOR +185
Total
O/U 6.5

Welcome back to the NHL regular season, and what a way to kick things off. The marquee matchup on opening night back from the Olympic break sends a Toronto team still finding its post-trade identity into the hornet's nest of Amalie Arena, where the Lightning have been absolutely ruthless. Tampa Bay's 19-1-1 run over their last 21 games is the kind of stretch that doesn't just happen by accident. That's sustained, relentless dominance, and it's made the Lightning one of the most terrifying teams in the league heading into the home stretch. At 37-14-4, first in the Atlantic Division, Jon Cooper's squad is built to make another deep playoff run, and they're getting key pieces back from the international stage tonight.

Nikita Kucherov has been playing at a level that defies description this season. His 91 points in 51 games translates to a league-best scoring pace, which puts him on track for one of the most prolific offensive seasons in modern NHL history. Kucherov's vision, his ability to find passing lanes that simply don't exist for normal humans, and his elite shot make him virtually impossible to contain when he's locked in like this. Brayden Point and Victor Hedman return from representing their countries at the 4 Nations Face-Off, and the question for Tampa Bay is how quickly these stars can reintegrate into the system after the break. If history is any guide, Cooper will have them dialed in by the first period.

Toronto at 27-21-9 is a fundamentally different team than the one that started the season. The summer trade that sent their top playmaker to the Vegas Golden Knights altered the offensive DNA in Toronto, and they've been trying to figure out what their identity is without that kind of dynamic presence in the lineup. Auston Matthews remains the engine that drives everything offensively, but the depth around him has been inconsistent. The Maple Leafs have talent, there's no question about that, but they've lacked the kind of devastating consistency that the Lightning have shown during their 21-game heater. Walking into Tampa coming off the Olympic break, with the Lightning fired up and their stars returning to a raucous home crowd, is about as difficult an assignment as you'll find in the NHL.

The -225 moneyline tells you everything about how the market views this matchup. Tampa Bay is a heavy home favorite, and with Andrei Vasilevskiy posting a 2.11 GAA and .920 save percentage this season, the Lightning have a wall between the pipes that can steal games even on nights when the offense doesn't click on all cylinders. The 6.5 total is interesting for the first game back from the break, as rust could cut both ways. Toronto's defense has been leaky at times this season, and if Kucherov and company find their rhythm quickly, this total could be in play early. The puck line at TBL -1.5 reflects the market's confidence that the Lightning won't just win, but win comfortably in their own building.

Sabres @ Devils

Here's a matchup that tells you everything about how fast things can change in the NHL. Buffalo at 32-19-6 with 70 points is legitimately one of the stories of the season. The Sabres, who missed the playoffs for over a decade and became a punchline across the league, are having a monster year and look like legitimate contenders in the Atlantic Division. The culture change in Buffalo has been remarkable, and the young core that suffered through all those losing seasons has finally developed into a group that plays with the kind of confidence and swagger you need to win in this league consistently. Coming out of the Olympic break, the Sabres have every reason to believe they can make noise in the postseason.

New Jersey at 28-27-2 has been one of the more confounding teams in the NHL this season. The Devils have generational talent in Jack Hughes, and when he's dialed in, New Jersey can beat anyone in the league. But consistency has been the demon that's haunted this roster all year. For every three-game winning streak, there's been a head-scratching loss to a team they should've handled. At 58 points, the Devils are right on the playoff bubble in the Metropolitan Division, and every game from here on out carries enormous weight. The break might actually benefit them, giving the coaching staff a chance to reset and refocus a group that's underperformed relative to preseason expectations.

The near pick'em line reflects just how close these teams are in terms of overall quality right now. Buffalo's been the more consistent team this season, but the Devils have home ice and the kind of individual skill that can flip any game. Hughes is one of the most electrifying players in hockey when he's skating with purpose, and his ability to turn nothing into something in transition makes him a constant threat. The Sabres counter with their depth and defensive structure, which has been the backbone of their surprising success. This is a game where the team that manages its post-break rust better will probably walk away with the two points.

The 6.5 total is standard for two offensively capable teams, and the first game back from the Olympics tends to produce some sloppy play as goaltenders find their timing and defensive systems take a period or two to click. If you're looking for early-season-return chaos, this Atlantic vs. Metropolitan crossover has the potential to deliver some wildly entertaining hockey. Both teams have something to prove, both teams need points, and neither team will be in the mood to ease back into the regular season gently.

You want to talk about a living legend? Alex Ovechkin stands at 919 career goals, having already surpassed Wayne Gretzky's once-untouchable record of 894. Every time Ovechkin steps on the ice now, he's not chasing history anymore, he's adding to a legacy that will never be matched. The Great 8 continues to pile up goals at an age when most power forwards have long since faded from the league, and every goal he scores from here on out just extends the record further into the stratosphere. Washington fans should savor every single shift, because there will never be another scorer like him.

Beyond the Ovechkin show, the Capitals at 29-23-7 with 65 points are fighting tooth and nail for a playoff spot in the Metropolitan Division. Washington's got enough talent to be dangerous, particularly when their power play is clicking, and Ovechkin's presence on the man advantage remains one of the most lethal weapons in hockey. The supporting cast has been good enough to keep the Caps in the mix, and the coaching staff has done a solid job of squeezing wins out of a roster that doesn't have the deepest talent pool in the conference. But games like tonight, against a divisional rival at home, are must-win territory if Washington wants to avoid the play-in round.

Philadelphia at 25-20-11 with 61 points is in the thick of the wildcard race, and that alone is a minor miracle for a team that many expected to be rebuilding this year. The Flyers have been surprisingly competitive, playing a blue-collar style that's tough to prepare for and even tougher to play against. They don't have the star power of the league's elite teams, but they've got a cohesive unit that plays hard every night and doesn't give away easy wins. The 11 overtime losses tell you that Philly has been in a ton of close games and just hasn't been able to find the dagger when it counts.

The -148 moneyline makes Washington a moderate favorite, which feels about right given the home ice advantage and the fact that the Capitals have more firepower offensively. The 6.5 total could be interesting in a Metropolitan Division rivalry where both teams tend to play with emotion and intensity. These clubs know each other well, and the familiarity can sometimes lead to tighter defensive play or, on the flip side, an all-out war where both teams throw haymakers. Either way, you're getting a game between two teams fighting for their postseason lives, and that urgency should produce some really compelling hockey on the first night back.

Dallas at 34-14-9 is one of the elite teams in the Western Conference, and they've built a roster that can hurt you in about a dozen different ways. The Stars' combination of defensive structure, goaltending depth, and offensive talent makes them a nightmare for opposing coaches to game plan against. But there's a significant storyline hanging over this team as they return from the break: Mikko Rantanen, acquired to give Dallas another elite offensive weapon, is out with an injury sustained during the Olympics. Losing a player of Rantanen's caliber would derail most teams, but Dallas has the kind of organizational depth that allows them to absorb the blow and keep rolling.

Even without Rantanen, the Stars have Jason Robertson, Roope Hintz, and a supporting cast that's been one of the most productive groups in the league all season. Peter DeBoer's system maximizes every player's strengths, and Dallas plays the kind of structured, suffocating defensive game that makes their offensive talent even more dangerous. When you only need two or three goals to win because your defensive structure is airtight, every point your forwards generate carries extra weight. The 5.5 total reflects that reality: Dallas games tend to be tighter, lower-scoring affairs where every goal matters, and that's by design.

Seattle at 27-20-9 with 63 points has had a solid but unspectacular season. The Kraken are comfortably in the playoff picture but haven't quite shown the ceiling that would make anyone fear them in a seven-game series. Their goaltending has been adequate, their offense has been streaky, and their defense has been good enough to win most nights. The issue for Seattle tonight is that they're walking into one of the toughest buildings in the Western Conference against a team that plays their exact brand of hockey, just better. Dallas's defensive structure will make life difficult for a Kraken offense that relies on transition opportunities and second-chance goals.

The -162 moneyline says the market respects Dallas even without Rantanen, and that respect is well-earned given their body of work this season. The Stars at home have been particularly dominant, and American Airlines Center has become one of the hardest arenas to play in across the league. Seattle will need a near-perfect game from their goaltender to stay in this one, because Dallas is going to generate quality chances even if they're missing one of their best forwards. The 5.5 total is the lowest on tonight's slate, and it's a direct reflection of how both teams play: tight, disciplined, and willing to grind out 2-1 or 3-2 victories rather than engage in shootouts.

Avalanche @ Utah Hockey Club

Moneyline

The best team in hockey visits Salt Lake City, and honestly, it's hard to overstate just how good Colorado has been this season. At 37-9-9 with 83 points, the Avalanche have the best record in the NHL by a comfortable margin, and they've done it with the kind of superstar dominance that reminds you why the regular season exists: to separate the truly great teams from everyone else. Nathan MacKinnon continues to play at an otherworldly level, driving the offense with his combination of skating speed, vision, and finishing ability that makes him one of the most dangerous players in any sport, not just hockey.

And then there's Cale Makar, who has redefined what a defenseman can be in the modern NHL. Makar's ability to quarterback the power play, jump into the rush, and create offense from the blue line while still being one of the best defensive defensemen in the league is absolutely absurd. The MacKinnon-Makar combination gives Colorado a one-two punch that no other team in the league can match, and when both are firing on all cylinders, the Avalanche are genuinely unbeatable. This team is built to win a Stanley Cup, and their regular season performance has made them the consensus favorite to hoist the trophy in June.

Utah Hockey Club at 30-23-4 with 64 points is having a remarkable inaugural season. The franchise relocated from Arizona and dropped into Salt Lake City, and the fans have embraced this team with the kind of intensity and passion that makes you wonder why the NHL didn't put a team here decades ago. Delta Center has been rocking all season, and the energy the crowd brings has given Utah a genuine home ice advantage that's helped them punch above their weight in a loaded Western Conference. Reaching 64 points in their first season as the Utah Hockey Club is an accomplishment worth celebrating, regardless of how the rest of the year plays out.

The matchup tonight is the hockey equivalent of David vs. Goliath, and the -145 moneyline on Colorado reflects the fact that even the best team in the league has to earn it on the road against a hungry team with a loud building. Utah has proven all season that they won't roll over for anyone, and their combination of young talent, solid goaltending, and fanatical home support makes them a legitimate threat to pull the upset. The 6.5 total suggests the market expects offense, which makes sense given Colorado's ability to score in bunches and Utah's willingness to play an up-tempo game. This is appointment viewing for anyone who loves watching elite hockey talent go up against a team riding a wave of enthusiasm and belief.

Both of these teams have been varying degrees of disappointing this season, but Vancouver's fall from grace has been nothing short of stunning. The Canucks at 18-33-6 with just 42 points are having a catastrophic year, and it's almost impossible to reconcile this team with the one that made the playoffs and generated so much excitement in British Columbia just a couple of seasons ago. Everything that could go wrong has gone wrong for Vancouver, from inconsistent goaltending to defensive breakdowns to an offense that's dried up at the worst possible times. Rogers Arena, once one of the loudest buildings in the league, has become a place where frustration hangs in the air like fog.

Winnipeg at 22-26-8 with 52 points hasn't been much better, and the Jets' season has been a masterclass in squandered potential. This is a team that started the season with genuine playoff aspirations and enough talent on the roster to compete with anyone in the Central Division. Instead, they've been maddeningly inconsistent, winning games they should lose and losing games they should win with no discernible pattern. The coaching staff has been searching for answers all season, and the Olympic break may have been exactly what this group needed: a chance to step away, decompress, and come back with fresh legs and a renewed sense of purpose.

What makes this game intriguing despite the mediocre records is the uncertainty of the post-break slate. Both teams had players representing their countries at the Olympics and the 4 Nations Face-Off, and reintegrating those players into the regular-season system is always a challenge. The team that handles that transition more smoothly will have a significant advantage tonight. Winnipeg's slight edge as a -125 road favorite suggests the market thinks the Jets have more talent to work with, and that's probably fair. Even in a down year, Winnipeg has players who can take over a game on any given night.

The 6.5 total is interesting because both of these defenses have been porous at times this season. Vancouver's goals-against numbers have been among the worst in the league, and Winnipeg hasn't exactly been a defensive juggernaut either. If the goaltenders come back rusty from the break, this has the potential to turn into a wide-open affair with goals coming in bunches. But it could also go the other way: two teams playing cautiously in their first game back, feeling things out, and producing a tighter-than-expected contest. That unpredictability is what makes opening night after a break so fascinating, and this game is a perfect example of a matchup where anything could happen.

Pacific Division rivals collide in Los Angeles, and the biggest storyline in this game wears a Golden Knights jersey: the Golden Knights' midseason acquisition from last summer. The elite playmaker has brought his world-class vision to Vegas and given the Golden Knights the kind of dynamic, creative forward they needed to take their offense to another level. Marner's vision and passing ability are among the best in the NHL, and his integration into the Vegas system has been one of the more fascinating subplots of the season. He's the kind of player who makes everyone around him better, and the Golden Knights' top six has been significantly more dangerous since his arrival.

Vegas at 27-16-14 with 68 points has been a solid but not spectacular team this season, and the 14 overtime losses tell you a story about a group that's competitive in almost every game but can't quite close the deal when it matters most. That's a frustrating profile for a franchise that has championship expectations, and the question heading into the stretch run is whether the Golden Knights can convert those overtime losses into regulation wins when the games get even tighter in March and April. The talent is there, the system is sound, and with Marner adding a new dimension to the offense, Vegas has the ceiling of a team that nobody wants to face in the first round.

Los Angeles at 23-19-14 with 60 points has been one of the most inconsistent teams in the Western Conference. The Kings have the veteran core and the defensive structure to compete, but they've lacked the offensive firepower to sustain pressure against the league's better teams. Anze Kopitar continues to provide leadership and reliable two-way play, but he can't carry the offense by himself, and the supporting cast has been streaky at best. The 14 overtime losses mirror Vegas exactly, which tells you these are two teams that play close games but struggle to deliver the knockout blow.

The near pick'em line is the market saying these teams are essentially mirror images of each other right now, and that's hard to argue with given their similar records and identical overtime loss counts. The 5.5 total is on the lower end of tonight's slate, reflecting the defensive identity both teams bring to Pacific Division matchups. These clubs know each other incredibly well, and the familiarity tends to produce tighter, more structured games where goals come at a premium. This is a game that could easily go to overtime yet again for both teams, and the winner will earn every inch of their two points tonight.

Connor McDavid has 96 points this season, and honestly, the fact that we've gotten to the point where 96 points in February barely registers as newsworthy tells you everything about the level at which this man operates. McDavid is chasing another scoring title with the kind of effortless brilliance that makes you genuinely believe he's playing a different sport than everyone else on the ice. His skating speed, his hands, his vision, his ability to process the game at a level that seems superhuman, it all combines to produce the kind of nightly highlight reel that reminds you why you fell in love with hockey in the first place.

Edmonton at 28-22-8 with 64 points has been a puzzling team this season, capable of looking like the best team in the Western Conference one night and a fringe playoff team the next. The Oilers' ceiling is as high as anyone's in the league when McDavid and Leon Draisaitl are clicking together, but their floor has been uncomfortably low on nights when the defense collapses and the goaltending can't bail them out. Coming back from the Olympics, Edmonton needs to find consistency down the stretch, because their spot in the playoff picture is far from guaranteed in a loaded Western Conference where every point matters.

Now here's the thing that makes tonight really interesting: Anaheim at 30-23-3 with 63 points is having a season that nobody saw coming. The Ducks, widely expected to be one of the worst teams in the league heading into the year, have been one of the most pleasant surprises in hockey. Their young core has developed faster than anyone anticipated, and the combination of emerging talent and solid goaltending has produced a team that's not just competing for a playoff spot but legitimately earning one. Anaheim's rise has been built on hard work, defensive commitment, and a young roster that plays with absolutely nothing to lose.

The near pick'em line tells you the market doesn't know what to make of this matchup, and that's understandable. Edmonton has the best player on the planet in McDavid, but Anaheim has been the more consistent team in terms of effort and structure this season. The Ducks' record at Honda Center has been a big part of their success, and their young players feed off the energy of a fan base that's been starving for a winner. The 6.5 total suggests the market expects goals, and with McDavid capable of producing three or four points on any given night, that number could be in play by the end of the second period. This is must-watch hockey: the best individual talent in the sport going up against one of the best team stories of the season.
