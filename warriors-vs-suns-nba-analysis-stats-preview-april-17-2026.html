NBA PLAY-IN ELIMINATION FOR THE WEST 8 SEED

The 10-seed Golden State Warriors (37-45) visit the 7-seed Phoenix Suns (45-37) in a single-elimination game at Mortgage Matchup Center for the final Western Conference playoff spot. The winner draws the defending champion Oklahoma City Thunder (64-18) in Round 1. The loser goes home. Phoenix is a 3.5-point favorite at -175 moneyline. Total 219.5. Golden State won the season series 3-1, including a 101-97 comeback February 5 without Curry. Stephen Curry is off the injury report after returning April 5 from a knee injury. Devin Booker is cleared after precautionary ankle management. Jimmy Butler is out for the year with a torn ACL. Kristaps Porzingis is questionable with right ankle soreness. Tipoff 10:00 PM ET on Prime Video.

Win or Go Home for the West 8 Seed

There is no structural advantage left in this game. No scoreboard carrying over from a prior round, no aggregate, no cushion. The Phoenix Suns and Golden State Warriors play forty-eight minutes at Mortgage Matchup Center on Friday night, and when the clock hits zero, one roster walks off to prepare for the Oklahoma City Thunder on Saturday, and the other walks off to answer uncomfortable questions for the rest of the summer. Phoenix finished 45-37 and earned the 7 seed. Golden State finished 37-45 as the 10 seed. Their records diverge by eight games in the standings, which sounds decisive until you look at the head-to-head: the Warriors took three of four meetings this year, including a 101-97 February 5 comeback at the Suns' arena without Stephen Curry in the lineup.

The market has Phoenix at -3.5 and -175 on the moneyline with a total of 219.5. Those numbers reflect home court, the day of rest advantage, and the recognition that Curry is still shaking off two months of rust after missing twenty-seven games. They do not reflect the head-to-head reality, and they do not fully account for the ceiling Curry provides when every possession becomes a referendum on his team's season.

Phoenix arrives the long way around. The 7 seed usually means two bites at the apple, but the Suns fumbled their first one against Minnesota on Tuesday, blowing an 11-point fourth-quarter lead with 7:13 to play before losing 114-110. Devin Booker's foul trouble derailed the back half of that game. Jalen Green dropped 35 points as Phoenix's answer to Anthony Edwards, but the Suns' transition defense collapsed in the final six minutes. They now face a Warriors team that just survived 114-110 in overtime against the Clippers, with Curry pouring in 35 points, 27 of them in the second half, to force overtime from a 13-point fourth-quarter deficit.

Curry's Two-Month Return Meets Peak Stakes

Curry returned April 5 from a right knee injury that cost him twenty-seven games. His body of work since then is two contests: a low-leverage regular-season closer and the play-in survival against the Clippers where he went for 35 points, 9 rebounds, 6 assists and played 36 minutes without any conditioning cushion to protect him. He did briefly head to the locker room in the first quarter with what looked like a scare, but he returned and dominated the fourth quarter with the kind of late-game takeover that has defined his career. Against Phoenix across four meetings this year he averaged 23.7 points, 7.7 rebounds, 5.3 assists, and he did that while splitting minutes with a Jimmy Butler who is now lost for the season after a torn ACL.

What Curry gives the Warriors in a single-elimination setting is both a ceiling the Suns cannot match and a margin for error they desperately need. The Warriors' half-court offense has basically been Curry plus movement all year. With Butler out for the season and Moses Moody on the shelf with a ruptured patellar tendon, the secondary creation load falls on Draymond Green, Brandin Podziemski, and Gui Santos. All three will have productive stretches. None of them can put a defense in a bind the way Curry can. If Curry is ready for another high-minutes game after his rest-of-the-fourth explosion on Tuesday, that is the single biggest swing variable on the board. If his legs show the two months he missed, this becomes a conversation about depth, and Phoenix's depth is simply better on paper tonight.

There's a reason Curry's moneyline value is what it is even on the road against a superior record. The greatest shooter in NBA history, in a win-or-go-home setting, in what could realistically be the final game of his career if Golden State pivots toward a rebuild this summer, is a nightmare to defend even at 37-45. Curry has always been at his most dangerous when cornered, and there is no corner tighter than this one. The 223.5 combined points the Warriors and Suns averaged across four meetings this year tilts slightly toward the over, but the real gamble is whether Curry can repeat a 35-point performance on back-to-back nights of postseason-intensity minutes.

Booker's First Play-In as The Guy

Phoenix's identity shifted the day Kevin Durant got traded to Houston in July 2025. Gone was the three-star model that produced 49 wins the previous season but went out in the first round. In came a new philosophy built around Devin Booker's primary creation, Jalen Green's downhill explosion, and a deeper, younger core. The results have been legitimately encouraging. Booker averaged 25.6 points, 7.1 assists, and 4.1 rebounds this year and shot 37.2% from three in a true lead-guard role. Jalen Green, acquired in the offseason, has been the ideal secondary scorer, posting 20+ points in 15 of his last 21 games and dropping 35 on Tuesday against the Wolves. Dillon Brooks, acquired to give the defense perimeter bite, had 19 points and 6 rebounds on Tuesday and has been the kind of postseason annoyance Phoenix has never really had.

The problem on Tuesday was Booker's foul trouble and a fourth-quarter defensive collapse, and those are exactly the issues that tend to resolve in a home game with an extra day of rest. Phoenix plays at a moderate pace and ranks in the top ten in offensive rating at 117.4 points per 100 possessions. Against Golden State specifically, they know the Warriors' weaknesses: the lack of secondary rim protection behind Porzingis, the dependence on Curry generating everything, and the relatively thin depth that will be tested across forty-eight minutes. Mark Williams, if he clears his questionable tag, is a legitimate advantage against a Warriors frontline without Butler's switchability.

The season series losses sting, but three of those four games happened before Phoenix traded for Brooks and fully settled on their post-Durant rotations. The February 5 loss where Curry sat and the Warriors came back from 14 down in the fourth tells you something about Golden State's late-game poise but very little about a healthy Phoenix roster in a playoff crowd. On Friday night, with Booker locked in and Green hunting shots, the Suns should impose their pace and their depth on a Warriors roster running on fumes.

The Porzingis Ankle Swing Variable

The single biggest injury question on the board is Kristaps Porzingis. He rolled his right ankle in the Clippers game, finished with 20 points on 8-of-12 shooting along with 5 rebounds, 5 assists, and 2 blocks, and is listed as questionable for Friday. If he plays, the Warriors get a second legitimate shot-creator and a rim deterrent Phoenix's bigs cannot handle alone. If he sits, the frontcourt is Draymond Green and Al Horford defending Mark Williams and whatever lineups Phoenix throws out, and Golden State gets even smaller against a team that has punished smaller lineups all year.

Porzingis has shot just 31.5% from three since arriving in the Bay Area from Atlanta at the deadline. His value right now is not his stroke; it is the threat he creates at the rim and the spacing he opens for Curry's pick-and-roll game. Without him, Curry has to carry a heavier scoring burden against a Dillon Brooks defensive assignment that will be extremely physical, and Draymond has to defend the opposing center for more minutes than his sixty-minute tolerance typically allows. The Warriors can survive Porzingis being out. They cannot survive Porzingis being out AND Curry running out of gas in the fourth.

Phoenix's injury report is cleaner. Booker and Green are both off it. Grayson Allen and Mark Williams are questionable, but the Suns have the bench depth to absorb those absences in a way Golden State structurally does not. When the bench margin matters this much in a single-elimination game, it tilts the math toward the team that can survive rotations.

Head-to-Head: Warriors Owned This Series

The season series tells one story, and the market has priced another. Golden State won three of four head-to-head meetings against Phoenix this year. The December 18 game at Mortgage Matchup Center went to the Suns 99-98 on a last-minute shot with Curry held to 2-of-9 from three. Two nights later at Chase Center, the Warriors won 119-116 behind Curry's 28 points, 9 rebounds, and 6 assists. The February 5 meeting in Phoenix was the most remarkable of the bunch: Curry sat the game out, and Golden State still rallied from down 14 in the fourth quarter to win 101-97 on the Suns' home floor. That's a serious mark against the theory that home court tips the scales here.

In the regular season, Curry averaged 23.7 points, 7.7 rebounds, and 5.3 assists against the Suns. Booker averaged 24.3 points and 6.1 assists against Golden State. The offensive rating splits are almost a wash, and the defensive efficiency actually favors the Warriors in these matchups because Draymond Green is uniquely equipped to defend multiple Suns positions in a single possession. The 3.5 line is not absurd on paper. It just isn't backed by this particular head-to-head history.

Betting Analysis

The -175 moneyline on Phoenix looks like a market price built on record differential, home court, and the rest advantage. All three are legitimate factors. What the price doesn't fully incorporate is the head-to-head history (Warriors 3-1 this year), the fact that Phoenix just lost to Minnesota blowing a fourth-quarter lead, and the reality that Curry is the single greatest single-elimination guard in NBA history and he's healthy enough to have dropped 35 on Tuesday. The +145 on Golden State doesn't have to be a value play to be an intellectually honest price. It just has to be reasonable relative to the matchup, and it is.

The -3.5 spread is where the market gets more interesting. Home favorites in win-or-go-home playoff games have historically gone roughly .500 against the spread since the play-in tournament was introduced. The Suns are competent but not elite defensively, and the Warriors have the one offensive weapon (Curry) who has covered every number in every playoff context you can imagine. Even in losses to Phoenix over the years, the Warriors have tended to keep games within three possessions because Curry refuses to let games get away. The 3.5 hook is significant, but a Golden State +3.5 bet is essentially asking whether the Warriors can stay within a possession on the road in a single-elimination game. History says yes more often than the spread implies.

The 219.5 total sits right on the fault line. The four regular-season meetings between these two teams combined to produce roughly 226 total points per game on average, so the total has been shaded down for the elimination context, which is a reasonable market adjustment. Play-in games tend to tighten defensively in the half court as coaches shorten rotations and trust their best players to play heavier minutes, and both coaches are coming off first-round results that will inform their closing lineup choices. Pace variables cut both ways: Phoenix at home averages 99.2 possessions; Golden State plays slightly faster when Curry is pushing pace in transition. If Curry and Booker both have 30+ scoring nights, the over comes in. If Phoenix controls tempo with Booker at the free throw line and Williams in drop coverage eating up space, the under clears. This is a legitimate coin-flip total.

Keys to Victory

Warriors Keys
1. Curry Minutes and Legs
Curry played 36 minutes Tuesday in overtime after a two-month absence. Steve Kerr needs to manage him into a 38-minute window here while still getting the high-leverage moments. If his legs hold up late, Golden State has a real shot.
2. Porzingis Active
If the ankle holds, Golden State gets a second scorer and rim deterrent. If he's out, Draymond and Al Horford have to cover too much ground against Mark Williams and Phoenix's second unit. This is the single biggest injury variable on the board.
3. Force 40+ Threes
The Warriors' path is through pace and volume from deep. Curry, Podziemski, and Santos need to combine for at least 20 three-point attempts. If Golden State gets below 35 threes attempted, they have lost the math of the game before the result is decided.
Suns Keys
1. Keep Booker Out of Foul Trouble
Booker's foul trouble sank Phoenix on Tuesday. He picked up cheap fouls defending Edwards and had to sit through stretches where the Suns' offense vanished. Against Curry, the discipline has to be better. Under 4 fouls is the target.
2. Jalen Green Shot Volume
Green dropped 35 on 20 attempts against Minnesota and has scored 20+ in 15 of his last 21. Phoenix's offense elevates when he's hunting. 20+ shot attempts is the benchmark. Let him create easy looks against Golden State's undersized perimeter rotation.
3. Dillon Brooks on Curry
Brooks' job is to be physical, annoying, and disruptive for every second Curry touches the ball. Bodying him off screens, denying catches, and making him work on defense all matter. If Curry fades in the fourth, this is the reason why.

Final Thoughts

This is a game that will be decided by whose secondary scoring shows up. Curry and Booker will get theirs. Jalen Green will get to 25 because Phoenix's offense is literally built to funnel him into advantage situations. The question is whether Draymond, Porzingis (if active), Podziemski, and Gui Santos can give Golden State enough auxiliary production to make Curry's gravity matter. If Porzingis is ruled out, the math gets much harder for the Warriors because Golden State loses the one big who can stretch the floor AND protect the rim. If he plays, this is a coin flip with a lot of late-game theater waiting for the final five minutes.

The market has priced this correctly in one sense: Phoenix should win at home with a full roster against a Warriors team on two days rest, missing Butler for the year, and running on fumes after an overtime survival game. Those are real advantages. What the market has underpriced is the Warriors' head-to-head dominance of this matchup and the reality that Curry, when cornered, is a genuine basketball miracle. Three out of four regular-season wins, including one on Phoenix's floor without Curry, tells you that Golden State has Phoenix's number structurally. Whether that number holds in a win-or-go-home setting is the entire question.

Either way, the stakes here are absurd. The winner meets Oklahoma City on Saturday, the best team in basketball, the defending champions, a team with the best point differential in the sport. Nobody is asking about Round 1 yet. Tonight is simply about whether Curry has one more miracle in him, or whether Booker and Jalen Green finally deliver the postseason arrival Phoenix has been waiting on since the Durant trade rebuilt the roster. The storylines are loud. The stakes are absolute. Tip-off 10:00 PM ET on Prime Video from the Valley.
