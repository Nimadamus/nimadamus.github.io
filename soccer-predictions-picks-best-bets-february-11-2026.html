Sunderland vs Liverpool

Wednesday, 3:15 PM ET | Stadium of Light, Sunderland
Sunderland

Draw

This is the marquee match of the entire midweek slate, and it's not particularly close. Sunderland, the fairy tale promoted side, welcome a Liverpool team that is teetering on the edge of a full-blown crisis under Arne Slot. Let's start with the numbers that should terrify every Liverpool supporter: they've won just one of their last seven Premier League matches in 2026, they're sitting in sixth place on 39 points, and they're now five points adrift of the top four. That's not a blip. That's a pattern. Slot himself has admitted that his team must be "close to perfection" to secure Champions League qualification, and frankly, perfection is the last word anyone would use to describe Liverpool's recent output. They've conceded a stoppage-time winner for the fourth time this league season when Erling Haaland's penalty sank them 2-1 against Manchester City on Saturday, and the manner of that collapse, leading through Dominik Szoboszlai's stunning free kick only to concede twice late, was damning.

Liverpool's away form has been genuinely dire. They've won just two of their last ten away league matches, and the underlying numbers suggest the decline is systemic rather than unlucky. They've generated volume, racking up 95 shots across their last five matches, the highest total in the division during that stretch, but the conversion has been woeful. Szoboszlai's red card against City means he serves a one-match ban and won't be available Wednesday, while injuries to Jeremie Frimpong, Conor Bradley, Joe Gomez, and Giovanni Leoni further thin an already stretched squad. Slot's side look like a team with all the ingredients of a contender but absolutely none of the consistency required to act like one, and they're running out of runway fast.

And then there's Sunderland, who are having one of the most remarkable seasons by a promoted side in Premier League history. They remain unbeaten at the Stadium of Light, the only team in the entire division that can make that claim. It's the longest unbeaten home run by a newly promoted side since Nottingham Forest's streak in the 1977-78 season, which is extraordinary company to keep. They've scored 21 goals in their 12 home league fixtures, averaging 1.75 per match, and under Regis Le Bris, they've turned the Stadium of Light into a fortress that even Manchester City couldn't breach, held to a 0-0 draw on New Year's Day. The atmosphere in Sunderland on a Wednesday night under the lights will be absolutely electric. These fans waited years in the lower leagues, and they're squeezing every ounce of joy out of this season.

The absence of captain Granit Xhaka through an ongoing ankle injury is a blow for Sunderland, no question. He's been the heartbeat of their midfield, and losing that experience and composure in a match of this magnitude hurts. But Liverpool's own issues are so profound that this doesn't feel like the typical big-club-visits-promoted-side script. The market has Liverpool at 1.63, implying roughly a 61% win probability, but given their wretched form, that feels generous. Liverpool have won just six of their last 20 league matches dating back to September. Sunderland's unbeaten home record and Liverpool's inability to close out matches on the road create a fascinating dynamic. This is one of those matches that could define both seasons, for very different reasons.

Manchester City are in no position to take anything for granted this season, but if there's one fixture that historically represents a guaranteed three points, it's this one. City have won their last 19 Premier League meetings with Fulham. Let that sink in. Nineteen consecutive victories. Marco Silva has lost all 13 of his Premier League head-to-head matches against City, which is the worst 100% loss record for any manager against a single opponent in the history of the English top flight. That's not just a stat, that's a curse. Every time Fulham walk into a stadium to face Pep Guardiola's side, they're fighting history, psychology, and quality all at once, and they've lost that fight every single time Silva has been in charge.

City come into this one buzzing after a massive 2-1 victory over Liverpool at Anfield on Saturday, a result that kept their title hopes flickering. Bernardo Silva's goal and Haaland's stoppage-time penalty sealed a dramatic win that proved City still have the mentality to compete at the highest level when it matters. They sit second in the table on 50 points, six behind leaders Arsenal, which means every fixture from here until May is essentially a must-win. The margin for error has evaporated completely. A slip against Fulham at home would be catastrophic for their title ambitions, and Guardiola knows it. The reverse fixture in December produced an absurd 5-4 City win at Craven Cottage, which tells you everything about the entertainment value of this matchup even when the result feels predetermined.

Fulham's season has been one of contrasts. They ended 2025 on a five-match unbeaten run that included three consecutive wins over Burnley, Nottingham Forest, and West Ham, and there was genuine optimism that Silva had this squad clicking. But 2026 has been tougher. They suffered a 2-1 home defeat to Everton most recently, and the away form has been inconsistent at best. Six of Fulham's last seven matches have gone over the 2.5-goal line, which is a clear indicator that while they can score, they're equally vulnerable at the back. Against a City side that just put five past them in December, that defensive fragility is a serious problem.

The 1.33 odds on City represent the shortest price on the entire Wednesday card, implying roughly a 75% win probability. That's steep, but it's hard to argue with the logic when the historical domination is this extreme. Fulham will need the performance of their lives to break that 19-match losing streak, and while this City side isn't the invincible machine of previous seasons, they're still far too strong for a mid-table Fulham on home soil. The real question isn't whether City win, it's whether Fulham can make it competitive. Given the 5-4 scoreline from the reverse fixture, don't expect a quiet afternoon at the Etihad. Goals are coming, the only debate is how many and from whom.

Villa Park hosts what should be one of the more entertaining matches on the Wednesday card, and the recent history between these two sides backs that up emphatically. Six of the last seven meetings between Aston Villa and Brighton have produced three or more goals, with both teams finding the net in five of those. The reverse fixture in December was a seven-goal thriller that Villa won 4-3, and there's every reason to expect fireworks again. Villa have hit a rough patch since their eight-match winning streak ended, winning just two of their last seven Premier League fixtures, including a 1-0 home defeat to Brentford and a 1-1 draw at Bournemouth in early February. Those results have effectively ended any lingering title hopes, and the focus now shifts entirely to securing a top-five finish and Champions League qualification.

The injury situation at Villa Park is a genuine concern. Youri Tielemans is out with an ankle problem, John McGinn and Boubacar Kamara are both sidelined with knee injuries, and Andres Garcia has a thigh issue. Losing Tielemans and McGinn from midfield simultaneously strips Villa of their creative engine and their combative edge in the center of the park, which is a massive problem against any opponent. The one bright spot is Ollie Watkins, who has scored more goals (nine) and contributed more goal involvements (eleven) against Brighton than against any other Premier League side. If there's one fixture where Watkins can drag Villa over the line despite the absences around him, it's this one. Morgan Rogers has also been excellent at home, involved in four goals in his last five home matches across all competitions.

Brighton, meanwhile, are in the midst of their worst run of the season and the pressure on manager Fabian Hurzeler is mounting rapidly. They've won just one of their last twelve Premier League fixtures, a run so poor that Brighton fans were chanting "you're getting sacked in the morning" after their most recent defeat, a 1-0 loss to Crystal Palace. The injury list has been brutal. Yankuba Minteh and Kaoru Mitoma have both been problematic absences, Yasin Ayari is out with a shoulder injury, and Adam Webster and Stefanos Tzimas are both done for the season with ACL injuries. Brighton have lost the sixth-most days to injury in the Premier League this season, and it's showing on the pitch.

Villa are priced at 1.95, implying roughly a 51% win probability, which makes this the closest to a coin-flip match on the card. The market respects Brighton's quality even in their current slump, and that's fair. This is still a squad with talent throughout, and their problems feel more like a combination of injuries and confidence than a fundamental lack of ability. But Villa at home, with Watkins' outstanding record against the Seagulls, feels like the right side. The key number here is the over/under: given the head-to-head history, expecting goals feels like the safest assessment you can make about this match. Both defenses have been leaky, and neither side has the personnel right now to grind out a clean sheet.

Crystal Palace need this one badly, and the circumstances couldn't be more favorable. They're hosting the worst team in the league at Selhurst Park, and after finally snapping a nine-match winless run with a 1-0 victory at Brighton last weekend, the confidence should be flowing for Oliver Glasner's side. Palace sit 13th on 32 points from 25 matches with a record of 8 wins, 8 draws, and 9 losses, which is the very definition of mid-table mediocrity. But there's been a nagging issue at home that needs addressing: they haven't won at Selhurst Park in eight consecutive matches. That's an astonishing drought for a side with this much talent, and if they can't break it against rock-bottom Burnley, serious questions need to be asked about what's going wrong behind closed doors.

The injury picture at Palace has been a significant factor in their inconsistent form. Jean-Philippe Mateta has been managing a knee issue since November, though the club confirmed he won't need surgery and is expected back around February 26. Edward Nketiah is out with a hamstring problem, Daichi Kamada has his own hamstring concern, and Daniel Munoz is dealing with a knee injury. When you're missing your primary striker and several other contributors, it's hard to build any rhythm, and that partly explains the home struggles. Glasner needs to find a way to extract more from the players he has available, because the fixture list won't get much kinder than this.

Burnley are, to put it bluntly, a team waiting for the season to end. They sit 19th on just 15 points from 25 matches, a full 11 points from safety with just 13 games remaining. Their winless run now stands at 16 Premier League matches, and they're two games away from matching their all-time club record of 17 without a win, which was set in the 1889-90 season. Scott Parker hasn't found a winning formula, and the numbers tell you everything: 3 wins, 6 draws, and 16 losses. They haven't won away from home all season, collecting just three points from a possible 36 on the road. Josh Cullen is out long-term with a cruciate ligament injury, and the January window provided little in the way of reinforcements. Relegation isn't a possibility at this point, it's a mathematical near-certainty.

Palace are unbeaten in their last five meetings with Burnley, winning three and drawing two, so the head-to-head record offers further encouragement for the home side. The 1.52 odds on Palace imply roughly a 66% win probability, which feels right. But the one variable worth watching is that Selhurst Park home drought. Eight matches without a home win is genuinely bizarre for a squad of Palace's caliber, and droughts like that can become self-reinforcing, where the crowd gets anxious early, the players feel the tension, and even straightforward situations become fraught. If Palace don't win this one, the questions will shift from "what's wrong with the form" to "what's wrong with the culture." Burnley, for their part, have nothing left to play for except pride, and sometimes that's the most dangerous opponent of all.

If you thought the Crystal Palace-Burnley match had relegation implications, this one cranks the tension up to an entirely different level. Nottingham Forest sit 17th on 25 points, just three points above the drop zone, and they welcome a Wolves side that is anchored to the bottom of the table on a miserable tally. This is as close to a genuine six-pointer as you'll find in the Premier League right now, even though Wolves' situation looks nearly hopeless with an 18-point gap to safety. For Forest, this is a match they absolutely must win. Anything less than three points, and the gap between survival and catastrophe could shrink to almost nothing depending on other results around them.

Forest's season has been characterized by a painful lack of goals. They're the joint-second lowest scorers in the division, and that deficiency has been magnified by the long-term absence of Chris Wood, their talisman striker who has been sidelined with a knee injury since October. Wood scored 20 goals in 36 appearances last season, and replacing that kind of output has proven impossible. Lorenzo Lucca could be handed his full debut at center-forward after scoring his first Forest goal in the 3-1 defeat at Leeds last weekend, which gives the home crowd something to be cautiously optimistic about. Defender Murillo is hoped to return from a calf injury, and Neco Williams will be back from suspension, both of which would strengthen a side that badly needs its best available players on the pitch for a match of this magnitude.

Wolves are a team in freefall, and the numbers paint a devastating picture. They've won just one of their last ten Premier League matches, scoring a league-worst 0.8 goals per game during that stretch. The away form is even more horrifying: winless on the road all season, collecting just three points from a possible 36. That's not a road record, that's a white flag. The 3-1 home defeat to Chelsea last weekend was their third consecutive loss, and the injury to Hwang Hee-chan, who suffered a hamstring problem against Chelsea, strips them of one of their few remaining creative threats. With the season's end approaching and safety an increasingly distant dream, the question isn't whether Wolves go down but whether they can summon enough pride to make the final months competitive.

Forest have won consecutive matches against Wolves recently, including a 1-0 away victory and a 3-0 demolition, so the head-to-head record favors the home side convincingly. The 1.65 odds on Forest imply roughly a 61% win probability, but the more interesting angle here is the under 2.5 goals market. Both teams are among the lowest-scoring sides in the league, and three of Forest's last four home matches have stayed under that line. Four of Wolves' last five matches have done the same. Matz Sels faces a late fitness test on a hip issue, and if Forest's first-choice goalkeeper misses out, that adds another layer of uncertainty to a match that already has an edge of anxiety about it. Both teams need points desperately, Forest for survival and Wolves for whatever shred of dignity they can salvage, and desperate teams don't tend to produce free-flowing football. Expect a tight, nervy affair at the City Ground.
